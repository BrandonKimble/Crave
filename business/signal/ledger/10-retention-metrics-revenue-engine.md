# 10. Retention, Metrics & the Revenue Engine

## Where the evidence points

The single highest-quality claim in this section — Blue Throne's Josh, a buyer who has audited real M&A targets rather than narrated his own survivor story — says the whole game is watching the retention curve of your top-20%-by-engagement cohort flatten, and that year-1 resubscribe rate (not DAU, not downloads) is the honest read on product value. Everything else in the corpus is noisier self-reported founder recall, but it converges on the same shape: paywall-mechanics levers (trial-reminder priming, cancel-flow saves, longer onboarding, multi-step paywalls) reliably move conversion a few points to tens of points, hard paywalls reliably beat freemium for apps that already have real utility, and views/downloads are explicitly disowned as vanity by the same people whose channel exists because of view counts. The corpus's one clean install-to-paid framework (Vahe: 4–10% okay, >10% good, <4% a problem) is the most directly usable number for Crave's gate-everything model, because it is denominated exactly the way Crave's whole funnel now works — no free tier to fall back on. The corpus is split on hard-vs-soft paywall philosophy, but that split maps cleanly onto category: apps with no product depth beyond a trigger-action-reward loop (Cal AI-era AI wrappers) benefit from a hard, aggressive paywall because there's no compounding value to protect; apps with genuine day-30-plus retention curves (Symmetry, Jungle, Stronger's own "category defines your LTV ceiling" framing) argue retention-led monetization outperforms — which, read honestly, is an argument FOR Crave's model, since Crave's bet is precisely that local data density produces the retention curve the AI-wrapper apps don't have. The corpus's weakest, most repeated lie is treating views as the KPI while its own best operators (Coconote, Payout's Casper, Nomad Table's Jay) explicitly say they walked away from million-view formats because they didn't convert.

## Consensus (≥2 independent sources)

- **Views/downloads are vanity; conversion and retention are the real metric.** Coconote deprioritized 40M–200M-view "PDF to brainrot" content because it converted at ~$25K vs a hypothetical $360K (`superwall--20260412--coconote-tiktok-ugc-playbook.md`); Payout's Casper calls topical/news videos "views but no sign-ups" vs. outcome-framed videos (`superwall--20260315--creator-equity-viral-formats.md`); Nomad Table's Jay abandoned his highest-view format after 300K views produced only 174 likes (`superwall--20260223--jay-nomad-table-ugc-machine.md`); Jack Frics: "downloads don't equal money" (`superwall--20251221--jack-frics-copy-viral-formats.md`). Four independent, non-echo sources (different podcasts, different eras) — high confidence, low echo risk since it's a claim these speakers make AGAINST their own view-count bragging.
- **Hard paywalls lift revenue materially when the product has real utility.** Stronger: ~25% overnight jump from freemium→hard-paywall-with-forced-trial (`superwall--20250815--fade-in-viral-format-600k.md`); Cardstock: "we're making like 30k a month" after adding a hard paywall (`superwall--20251128--kyle-fowler-cardstock-aso-tiktok.md`); Cal AI's origin story: paid-only beat freemium+ads, "more revenue on fewer downloads" (`thebrettway--20241128--zach-yadegari-cal-ai.md`); Quittr: free access "dropped [usage] significantly," hard paywall improved both revenue and user commitment (`thebrettway--20250210--quittr-hard-paywall-influencer-cpm.md`). Four sources, all first-party founder claims, moderate echo risk (Superwall's own incentive to showcase hard-paywall wins), but the pattern also shows up on non-Superwall channels (thebrettway).
- **Paywall-recovery mechanics (trial-reminder priming, cancel-flow saves, abandoned-cart discounts) reliably lift revenue.** Coconote: 7-day trial extension in the cancel flow saves 27% of would-be cancelers (`superwall--20260412--coconote-tiktok-ugc-playbook.md`); Sunflower: a "we'll remind you before your trial ends" priming screen produced a claimed 30–70% lift across several metrics (`superwall--20260323--sunflower-1m-arr-10-months.md`); Pingo's transaction-abandoned 55%-off flow "performs better" (`superwall--20260419--pingo-500k-creator-viral-playbook.md`); Vahe's placement doctrine explicitly builds trial-cancel and transaction-abandonment offers into the standard paywall stack (`spine/09-vahe-paywall-optimizer.md`). Four sources; Superwall-sponsor echo risk is real but the mechanism (de-risking before the price ask) is consistent and plausible independent of who's selling the tooling.
- **Longer, more invasive onboarding out-converts short onboarding — contra minimize-friction dogma.** Coconote's jump from ~5 to ~13 screens produced +16% trial conversion (`superwall--20260412--coconote-tiktok-ugc-playbook.md`); Bloom's 1-screen→5-story-screen redesign was "a pretty big change" upward (`superwall--20250707--bloom-viral-tiktok-strategy.md`); Quittr deliberately keeps onboarding long as a seriousness filter (`thebrettway--20250210--quittr-hard-paywall-influencer-cpm.md`); Arthur's Glow app moved completion 74%→83% with a progress bar + commitment screen (`arthurspalanzani--20260110--glow-affirmation-app-2k-challenge.md`). Four sources across three different podcast ecosystems — low echo risk, this is a durable pattern.
- **No such thing as instant marketing — every channel needs months before you can judge it.** Sideshift's Drew Levin: 1–2 months to find "your 3 rock creators," SEO agencies pitch 18 months (`spine/05-sideshift-drew-ugc-economics.md`); Dmitri/YourMove AI: SEO needs ~3 months before anything shows and 3–6 months to distinguish "not viable" from "execution failure" (`superwall--20260104--yourmove-ai-seo-30k.md`); Bloom's Jay: minimum 90 days to make any video go viral, and he was in a 60-day dry spell at the time of taping (`superwall--20250707--bloom-viral-tiktok-strategy.md`); StudyFetch bootstrapped 18 months on organic before any real growth capital (`spine/03-studyfetch-creator-machine.md`). Four independent sources — this is one of the most reliable, non-Superwall-dependent claims in the whole corpus.

## Conflicts (position A vs B — what the disagreement is really about)

- **Hard paywall vs. soft/retention-led paywall.** Symmetry's Mauro: "if you have a really good product, that's when you make a lot of money" on a soft paywall, contra hard-paywall-and-long-onboarding thinking (`arthurspalanzani--20260702--symmetry-billion-views-ugc-machine.md`); Jungle's Julian: declinable paywall + free usage + retention-led conversion, because "when you have retention, you have more chances at monetization" (`superwall--20251102--ai-study-app-100k-mo.md`); Phoenix/Copia: "if you do a hard paywall, it's honestly because your app is [bad]" (`superwall--20251022--phoenix-copia-ugc-creator-funnel.md`) — versus Stronger, Cardstock, Cal AI, Quittr all reporting hard-paywall revenue jumps (see Consensus above) — ~~Pepai~~ struck by red team: Pepai's transcript reports no freemium→hard-paywall flip or revenue-jump-from-gating claim; it was misattributed onto this list. This is NOT a contradiction so much as a category split: the anti-hard-paywall voices are all apps with genuine daily-habit/social retention loops (fitness+social network, freemium AI study tool, manifesting-with-creator-partnership) where product depth exists to protect; the pro-hard-paywall voices are mostly single-session AI-wow utilities with no compounding value. Read charitably, both sides agree hard paywalls work best precisely where retention DOESN'T exist yet to protect — which is an argument that Crave, if its repeat-use retention curve proves real, could eventually loosen (the freemium pivot is already built and env-gated per the fact sheet), but should launch hard because the retention curve is unproven at zero users.
- **Weekly pricing: declining scourge vs. legitimate lever.** Blue Throne Josh and Jonathan Parra both frame weekly as an AI-wrapper churn-and-burn artifact, "declining for social/multiplayer apps" (`spine/02-172m-advice-blue-throne-josh.md`, `spine/01-4000-paywalls-jonathan-parra.md`) — versus PushScroll (weekly beat monthly, though "only a little bit of extra ARPU," `superwall--20251018--pushscroll-30k-organic-playbook.md`), Locked ($7/week as the main plan, `superwall--20260607--14-year-old-14k-month-ai-app.md`), and Social Wizard (deliberately pushes weekly for "faster payback period," `thebrettway--20250904--kelechi-social-wizard-clean-eats.md`). The disagreement is about audience age and category urgency: weekly survives where the buyer is young/impulse (under-25, Gen Z, screen-time gamification) or the need is acutely short-duration (dating, quitting an addiction). Crave's adult, planning-oriented, no-urgency use case sits firmly on the "weekly is wrong" side — this is not really a live debate for Crave, just confirmation of the decision already made.
- **Year-1 annual renewal: 30% vs. higher.** Zach Yadegari's team states "annual health/fitness subscription year-1 renewal ≈ 30% (industry average)" as a flat fact (`thebrettway--20260302--zach-yadegari-cal-ai-sale.md`) — this sits below Blue Throne Josh's own "40 good / 30 ok" band (`spine/02-172m-advice-blue-throne-josh.md`) and below the 44% figure the business-model.md docs cite from RevenueCat's own lifestyle-category benchmark data (referenced, not sourced in this corpus). The gap is likely methodology (one is a single operator's casual industry-average claim; the other is an aggregated platform benchmark) rather than a real disagreement — but it means Crave should NOT anchor its kill-criteria on the lower, anecdotal 30% number without checking RevenueCat's benchmark report directly.

## Noise (discard for Crave)

- **Trial-toggle A/B tests showing "no effect"** (teleprompter app, `arthurspalanzani--20260514--teleprompter-app-17k-month-side-project.md`) — single low-traffic data point, directly contradicted by Vahe's much larger aggregate claim that ~10% of users offered a trial-skip toggle take it; not enough signal to act on either way.
- **$50/$100/$200 tiered CPP acquisition to fuel A/B testing at volume** (Sizzle AI, `superwall--20250623--yev-paid-amplify-organic-testing.md`) — presumes a paid-ads budget Crave doesn't have; the underlying idea (buy volume specifically to make tests significant) is sound but the mechanism is inapplicable pre-ad-budget.
- **Aggressive weekly-pricing and price-elasticity experiments from AI-wow/impulse apps** (Glam Up's 3x price raise increasing conversion, `superwall--20260511--nicole-four-apps-ugc-playbook.md`; Social Wizard's $6.99→$9.99 raise, `thebrettway--20250904--kelechi-social-wizard-clean-eats.md`) — interesting directionally (raising price didn't hurt conversion) but these are novelty-purchase psychology in categories with zero considered-purchase friction; treat as a hypothesis worth testing on Crave's own price ladder, not as transferable evidence.
- **Grindclock-style ads-monetization pivots after weak paywall conversion** (PrayScreen, `superwall--20250928--prayscreen-120k-quick-exit.md`) — categorically irrelevant; Crave's model has no ad-monetization fallback and shouldn't develop one.
- **"DAU" bragging numbers threaded through nearly every episode** (150M MAU, 500K DAU, etc.) — per Blue Throne Josh, these are explicitly NOT signal; they're the podcast genre's preferred vanity metric and should be ignored as evidence of anything except audience size for the guest's personal brand.

## Crave transfer

The fact sheet's open score-quality caveat and the "needs-no-users" table both imply Crave can be valuable before a single cohort exists — but the retention-and-resubscribe doctrine in this section is the one place the advice genuinely bites: Crave's entire differentiation thesis (repeat-use local utility, not single-session AI-wow) is _unproven_ until real Austin users produce a real day-30/day-90/year-1 curve, and nothing in the fact sheet currently commits to measuring the RIGHT things from day one. Concretely: instrument (1) the top-20%-by-engagement retention curve, not aggregate DAU, from the first cohort onward — Blue Throne Josh's framework is the correct oracle here, not a growth-podcast platitude; (2) install-to-paid as the whole-funnel number, benchmarked against Vahe's 4–10% band, with <4% treated as a hard stop-and-fix-onboarding signal before spending any more distribution effort, and >10% as the green light to invest harder in the (currently unbuilt) creator/organic engine; (3) trial-start and trial-to-paid rates on the annual-with-trial leg specifically (targets >15% and >30% respectively per Vahe), since the monthly pay-now leg has no trial funnel to diagnose; (4) organic share of installs against the 50/35/20 bands — Crave's no-ad-budget constraint makes this almost existential: falling under 20% organic would mean the "no ad budget" plan is already broken, months before revenue tells the same story.

What changes if X: if the top-20% cohort curve visibly flattens by day 90 (Crave's actual test window given the corpus consensus that channels/products need 60–90+ days to read), that is the "scale" signal — worth then testing price upward per the corpus's repeated (if category-mismatched) finding that price increases didn't hurt conversion, and worth reconsidering whether the hard paywall should loosen at the margins (the freemium pivot is already built and env-gated). If it does NOT flatten — if Crave looks like every AI-wrapper in this corpus, decaying toward organic-inflow-equals-churn (the Grindclock lesson) — that is a kill/fix signal for the PRODUCT, not the marketing, and no amount of UGC volume should be thrown at it first.

Panel questions still open: (1) Is Crave's real analog Blue Throne's "40% good" annual-renewal band, or the more conservative 30% "industry average" — and which one should the kill-criteria actually use, given the corpus's own internal disagreement on that number? (2) Given no ad budget, what specific organic-share number, measured how (onboarding attribution question, as several apps in this corpus do), counts as Crave's own version of the 50/35/20 test? (3) Should the annual-vs-monthly split itself be an early instrumented metric — since several sources (Coconote's cancel-save, Vahe's trial-forward framing) treat annual-mix as a leading indicator of committed vs. impulse buyers — and if so, what mix should trigger concern? (4) At what point (dollar volume, sample size) does Crave have enough paywall traffic to run Vahe-style multi-step paywall experiments at all, given the corpus consensus that under ~50-100 conversions per variant, only drastic tests (not micro-copy) are worth running?
