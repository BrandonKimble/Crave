# Brief: The Concentrated Bet — One Anchor Austin Creator, Not a Diffuse Roster

## The position

For a trust product with no ad budget and one operator, Crave should skip the diffuse small-deal grid and make one concentrated bet: identify a single trusted Austin food creator, vet them through founder-led channel zero, and convert them into the zero-follower "Crave ATX" brand-account persona — StudyFetch's trust-transfer mechanic (`spine/03-studyfetch-creator-machine.md`) scaled from a 750k-follower national influencer down to Austin, structured with a cash retainer plus a modest revenue-indexed kicker rather than a dozen scattered $20-per-video UGC deals. The corpus itself names this as the resolution to its own premium-trust problem: every case of successful premium pricing rode a pre-existing trust persona Crave doesn't have, "the gap a local-creator partnership... must fill" (`ledger/01-positioning-and-category.md`, conflict 3). A diffuse roster optimizes for content volume; Crave needs a recognizable Austin food _identity_ that compounds — and identity does not compound across a dozen interchangeable $20/video creators the way it does behind one consistent face.

## The affirmative case

**The corpus already wrote this playbook, and it names Crave's exact shape.** StudyFetch's differentiator — paying a mid-tier niche influencer five figures a month to launch a brand-new, zero-follower account, letting their loyal audience "follow over" so trust transfers wholesale — is called out twice as the single most transferable mechanic in this entire corpus for Crave: "the zero-follower brand-account mechanic is the single most Crave-relevant idea in this transcript" (`spine/03`), and again independently in the creator-machine ledger: "the highest-fit single mechanic in the corpus is StudyFetch's zero-follower brand account... scaled down to a trusted Austin food creator running a 'Crave ATX'-style account" (`ledger/06-creator-ugc-machine.md`). Two independent close-readings of the same transcript converged on the identical transfer without cross-referencing each other's conclusions — that's a real signal, not an echo.

**The premium-trust literature points the same direction from a different angle.** Waking Up ($20/mo, an estimated ~10x revenue-to-download ratio) and Purpose launched distribution-first on Sam Harris's and Mark Manson's _pre-existing_ trust; Sunflower hired the #1 sobriety influencer as an employee for the "headliner effect" — a big persona makes a no-name app read as category leader (`ledger/01`, conflict 3). Crave cannot rent a celebrity, but the ledger's own synthesis names the bootstrap-scale substitute: "the bootstrapped scale-down both sides point to is one deep local-creator partnership... not a celebrity." That is this brief's position, verbatim, in the grounding.

**The math favors depth because the pool is shallow.** Section 06's own locality-first inversion argues for concentration, not spray: Austin food creators are "dozens, not thousands," which "flips the outreach math from Cal AI's 500-DMs-for-2 spray to relationship depth per creator." A market this small cannot support Jenni's 150-creator, 16-hour-day DM-outreach machine (`ledger/06`, numbers table) — and Crave has no manager to run it (the corpus's own ratio, 1 manager : 10 creators, assumes headcount a solo bootstrapped founder-engineer does not have). One relationship is the operationally correct scale for the team that exists.

**A diffuse roster actively fights the integrity brand.** Section 06 flags "every third or fifth video" of disguised promo as a named symptom of UGC saturation, and names disguised-discovery scripting as "specifically trust-poisoning for a product whose whole brand is evidence-receipted objectivity" (`ledger/06`, Noise). A dozen small, forgettable UGC deals _are_ the ad-fatigue pattern StudyFetch's own zero-follower mechanic was invented to dodge ("audiences understand the videos are ads," `spine/03`). One consistent, recognizable local voice avoids that saturation signature by construction — it reads as a person, not a program.

**The economics scale down cleanly, and equity/rev-share has direct precedent.** StudyFetch's retainer is $10k/mo for a 750k-follower influencer; Crave's own transfer note prices the Austin-scale analog at "plausibly $500–2,000/mo instead of $10k because local food creators are small" (`ledger/06`, Crave transfer). Equity- and rev-share-anchored creator deals are a real pattern (Connor/Casper/Fitted up to 50–70% equity; Symmetry's uncapped revenue-indexed CPM structurally the same idea, `ledger/06` conflict 1 and numbers table), and food creators clear the corpus's creator-fit law cleanly: they don't sell a competing product. Content-wise, the anchor account inherits Crave's built-in advantage for free — dish rankings are native engagement bait ("the #1 birria in Austin is X" is the 50/50-divide mechanic with the product named intrinsically, `ledger/05-founder-led-content.md`) — so the concentrated bet isn't manufacturing virality, it's pointing an already-loaded gun through one durable identity instead of many disposable ones.

## Pre-empting the other side

**Founder-first is the corpus's single strongest consensus, and it comes first, not instead.** Ten-plus independent sources across all three channels agree: the founder runs channel zero before any creator dollar (`ledger/05` consensus 1, `ledger/06` consensus 1). I concede this fully — the anchor bet is sequenced _after_ 60–90 days of Brandon's own Austin-scoped posting, not a day-one substitute for it. That sequencing isn't a weakness of the concentrated-bet thesis; channel zero is also the scouting funnel that de-risks the pick.

**Even the corpus's own "elite roster" camp is still a roster, not one name.** Driss ran under 20 creators, Coconote ran 10–12 "Navy SEALs" — never one (`ledger/06` conflict 3). Power-law variance is real and brutal: "1-in-85 creatives wins; ~97% flop" (`ledger/05`, numbers table). Betting 100% on one unvetted local creator is a real single-point-of-failure risk — the same failure mode Cardstock hit when it lost its sole keyword rank (`ledger/01`, numbers table). I concede this squarely: the honest version of this bet is not "skip diligence, pick one," it's "run the same screening funnel as ordinary UGC (comment density, charisma, audience-geography check) on the local pool, trial the top 1–2 candidates on their own accounts first, then commit depth — not equity or a big retainer — to whichever one clears the gates." Concentration is in the size of the eventual commitment, not in skipping the audition.

**The audience-geography premise might not hold at Austin scale.** StudyFetch's mechanic works because a 750k-follower influencer has a large, addressable, already-loyal following to transfer. An Austin food creator's own following may be too small, too scattered outside Austin, or simply not exist at a price Crave can pay — and Marcus Burke's warning that niche-adset content gets less algorithmic push is an open, unresolved question in the grounding itself (`ledger/05`, open question 1). I concede this is the real risk to the whole thesis: if channel zero's own 60–90 days shows Austin-scoped hooks get throttled even in the founder's hands, the anchor-creator bet inherits that ceiling — no amount of trust transfer fixes a distribution problem.

## Concrete implementation

**Days 0–60/90:** Brandon runs founder channel zero exactly per section 05 — screen-recorded search demo, ranking-controversy hooks, cross-posted IG/TikTok/FB, gated by 3-sec watch-through ≥70–75% and comment-based "what app is that" signal, not views. In parallel, scout the Austin food-creator pool using the identical screen: comment density about the creator/product (not raw views), audience geo-check, food-category fit.

**Day ~60–90, trial:** Pay the top 1–2 candidates a small trial retainer (~$300–500/mo, 4–8 videos) on their _own_ accounts first — StudyFetch vets this way before spinning up a brand account. Apply the same gates as founder content: no double standard.

**On clearing the trial, launch the anchor:** Stand up the zero-follower "Crave ATX" account with the vetted creator at a $500–1,500/mo cash retainer plus a modest revenue-indexed kicker (a small percentage of net-new Austin subscription revenue attributable via a promo code, mirroring Symmetry's pay-per-result logic) — not cap-table equity, given the solo-bootstrap constraint. Perpetual usage rights, non-stacking milestone bonuses on top (the corpus's 29/30-accepted structure), no hard view-cap on a breakout post. Content mix: product-as-hero demo, ranking-controversy, listicle slideshow as the cheap retargeting layer underneath — the same three lanes as channel zero, run by the local face instead of Brandon.

**Escalate, don't replace:** Only after the anchor account clears its own 60–90-day gate does Crave add a small (3–5 creator) supporting roster on the standard convergent deal grid ($20–50/video base + non-stacking milestones). The anchor is the identity; the roster, if it comes, is additive volume. Apply Spark-ad geo-amplification (verified DMA-level TikTok targeting, `crave-fact-sheet.md`) to the anchor's winning posts to buy back the reach a single account can't get organically.

## What would prove me wrong

If founder channel zero itself shows Austin-scoped hooks get throttled as niche content at 60–90 days — confirming Marcus Burke's warning even in the founder's own hands — the whole bet is void: distribution doesn't exist for the anchor to inherit either. If the scouted pool genuinely has no creator with a real, price-affordable, meaningfully-Austin following (the StudyFetch mechanic requires an actual audience to transfer, not just a face), the analog collapses into "a new zero-follower account with nobody following it over." If the vetted anchor, once trialed, fails the exact gates founder content passed — no lift in watch-through, no "what app is this" density beyond Brandon's own posts — that's direct proof the persona isn't earning its premium, and the dollars belong in the standard small-roster grid instead. And if a second or third trial creator from the same pool clearly outperforms the chosen anchor, that's evidence the concentrated-bet framing was wrong and a wider (if still small) roster was the right shape all along — power-law variance is real, and one up-front pick can't out-argue a portfolio of a few cheap trials.
