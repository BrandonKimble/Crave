# Brief: Dark-Seed the Bench

**Panel:** One city vs many — does Crave launch Austin-only or dark-seed a bench of cities, and what fires expansion?
**Position:** Dark-seed 8–10 metros before/at launch (not the full 25) as cheap, decoupled insurance against a cost the corpus proves Crave will pay whether or not it prepares for it.

## The position

Crave should launch its _marketing_ Austin-only — founder-led content, Austin creators, Austin hooks, for the first 60–90 days, no exception — while its _data pipeline_ dark-seeds a bench of 8–10 additional metros in parallel, loaded and validated but invisible, ready to flip visible on a named trigger. This is not an argument for expanding go-to-market; it is an argument that data coverage and marketing spotlight are two separate decisions with two different cost structures, and Crave is the first product in this entire corpus positioned to make that separation for ~$600–780 a city instead of the five- and six-figure monthly retainers every other guest needed to manufacture the same addressable reach. The corpus's own geo-dilution math says a chunk of Crave's future viral reach is going to spray outside Austin regardless of what Crave does; the only live question is whether that reach lands on a validated, ready-to-monetize city or on dead air.

## The affirmative case

**1. Geo-dilution isn't a risk to model — it's a certainty the corpus already measured.** Pingo's own dashboard shows a viral spike produced ~200K+ unmonetizable Russian users, roughly half of its ~23K peak new iOS users, sanctions-blocked and worthless (`ledger/04-launch-shape.md`, `crave-fact-sheet.md`). StudyFetch's operating rule is that even a _national_ US creator only pulls ~80%+ US viewership — Reels spray internationally and convert near-zero (`ledger/04`, `ledger/05-founder-led-content.md`). Ledger 01 states the mechanism plainly: "nobody in the corpus solved city-level targeting through the algorithm" (`ledger/01-positioning-and-category.md`, Conflict 2). TikTok and Instagram do not respect city boundaries. So the moment Austin content actually breaks out — which is the entire goal of founder-led channel zero — a majority of its views are, by construction, non-Austin. That isn't a maybe; it's the same physics that hit Pingo, just smaller in absolute numbers.

**2. The fact sheet's own math names this the strongest quantifiable case for pre-seeding.** Ledger 04's Crave-transfer section works it through explicitly: "if a breakout video would otherwise waste >95% of its views outside Austin, seeding even 8–10 major metros converts a large fraction of that same video's audience into addressable users, for less than the corpus's own creator/ad budgets... require to manufacture that reach organically" (`ledger/04`). That sentence is doing real work: it's comparing a ~$5–8k one-time data cost against StudyFetch's five-figure/month retainers and Cal AI's $2–3 CPM deals — the actual price the corpus's own guests paid to buy reach Crave would otherwise be handed for free and then throw away.

**3. The cost floor makes this insurance, not aggression.** Austin's full archive load came in at ~$600 ($560–780 band), mechanized to a two-command load→validate→flip-visible pipeline (`crave-fact-sheet.md`). Seeding a bench of 8–10 comparable metros runs roughly $5–8k; the full 25-metro ambition tops out at $15–40k one-time (`ledger/04`, `ledger/11-scaling-and-sequencing.md`). Both ledgers independently flag this as the fact that makes the whole question tractable where the corpus itself has no analog for it (no guest "ever had a founder-controlled data pipeline that pre-seeds a new market cheaply," `ledger/04`). Ledger 11 adds the amortization frame directly: Crave's fixed asset to amortize is the collection pipeline, and what it amortizes across is _markets, not apps_ — the one place in the whole corpus where the portfolio-studio logic actually applies cleanly (`ledger/11`, Conflict 3).

**4. The waitlist branch is already built and is higher-intent than anything the corpus's waitlist data describes.** Onboarding's city-pick step already branches non-Austin/NYC selections into a waitlist (`crave-fact-sheet.md`, "Onboarding as built"). The corpus is unambiguous that a cold, standalone waitlist converts terribly — Massive's 40–50K signups converted ~0.5% with zero paid (`ledger/04`, Consensus 1) — but that finding is about the waitlist as the _primary_ growth mechanism. Crave's waitlist entries are a free byproduct of someone who already downloaded the app, ran onboarding, and hit a wall — a qualitatively different, higher-intent signal than a cold capture form, and it comes with zero incremental cost or campaign. It gives Crave something no corpus guest had: a real, ranked, self-reported demand list for exactly which cities to seed first, instead of a population-density guess.

**5. The fast-follower clock argues for having the bench ready, not for building it under fire.** PrayScreen's sole "Bible focus" listing decayed to ~70 competing listings in 2–3 months (`ledger/01`, `ledger/04`). If Crave's dish-ranking model proves out in Austin and a clone starts replicating city-by-city, the difference between "flip visibility on a bench that's already loaded and validated" and "start city #2 from a standing start" is the difference between winning and losing that same 2–3-month window on Crave's own turf.

## Pre-empting the other side

**Their best point: score-quality calibration is real and unresolved, and a shallow 25-city flip is an integrity risk.** This has to be conceded in full — the fact sheet itself flags Score constants (ρ, acclaim/praise weights, upvote premium, opinion floors) as "re-derive against production density," and that recalibration hasn't even been finished for Austin yet (`crave-fact-sheet.md`, "Open score-quality caveat"; `ledger/04`, Recommended Shape). Ledger 04 states the risk directly: "a city that's seeded but not retention-tested is a liability if UGC sends traffic there before the Score is trustworthy — a shallowly-validated 25-city flip risks the integrity brand everywhere at once." I answer this not by disputing it but by taking it as the reason the position is _dark-seed_, not _launch_: seeded cities stay invisible behind the existing city-pick allowlist until each one individually clears the same calibration bar Austin has to clear first. The insurance is in the data being loaded and waiting, not in the marketing spotlight ever touching an unvalidated city. This also means I concede the full 25-metro figure is the wrong day-one target — a bench of 8–10 is the honest ask; 25 is a 6–12-month target contingent on the calibration _methodology_, not just the data pipeline, proving repeatable.

**Their second point: home-market-first (Alberto, Mauro, Blue Throne) is the least echo-risked consensus in the whole corpus** — three independent channels (Spain-first budget app, Spain-first Gen-Z gym app, Blue Throne's category-narrowing rule) converge on winning one place completely before broadening (`ledger/04`, Consensus 4). I don't contest this; I narrow what it governs. It's a claim about where _marketing attention and creator dollars_ go — and this brief agrees completely: 100% Austin-scoped content, no dark-seeded city gets a single creator dollar or founder content-hour until its own trigger fires. It says nothing about whether the backend data layer should be idle in the meantime, and no corpus guest ever had the option to ask that question, because none of them had a $600 mechanized seed.

**Their third point: founder attention, not cash, is the real bottleneck, and Brandon is solo.** Ledger 11 makes this explicit: "the real bottleneck is founder attention and score-quality calibration, not cash" (`ledger/11`, Crave transfer). This is the honest reason the ask is a bench of 8–10, sourced preferentially from the 783 archives already downloaded (`crave-fact-sheet.md`) to minimize new collection babysitting, not a blanket 25-city push that would compete with Austin content time and product-build time during the exact 60–90-day window ledger 05 says the founder needs to be running channel zero personally.

## Concrete implementation

1. **Sequence:** finish Austin's full-load score recalibration first (already the plan) — this produces the calibration _methodology_, which is the reusable asset, not just Austin's specific numbers.
2. **Bench selection (8–10 cities):** pull from the 783 already-downloaded city/food subreddits and the 1,240-sub worldwide manifest (`crave-fact-sheet.md`) — prioritize by subreddit density/saturation profile similar to r/austinfood's own signal, cross-referenced against the live waitlist ranking from onboarding's city-pick branch. Estimated cost: ~$5–8k total at the $600–780/city floor (NYC and larger metros will run higher; budget accordingly).
3. **Gate:** each seeded city stays behind the existing city-pick feature flag — invisible — until it individually clears the same score-quality bar Austin clears.
4. **Trigger to flip a city visible (any one of):** (a) an Austin video clears Flame's watch-through/comment gate as a genuine breakout whose view geography is majority non-Austin; (b) Austin organic/creator RPM plateaus, mirroring Cal AI's single-niche-ceiling pattern (`ledger/11`); (c) a dish-ranking clone appears, invoking the PrayScreen fast-follower clock.
5. **Amplification held in reserve:** when a trigger fires, use TikTok Spark Ads' verified DMA/city-level targeting and Meta's city+radius targeting (`crave-fact-sheet.md`) to aim a bounded few-hundred-dollar spend at the top 3–5 dark-seeded metros' DMAs — converting diluted reach into addressable users at a fraction of a dedicated creator program's cost, exactly the mechanism ledger 05 flags as "Sideshift's spark-ads-with-geo-targeting play" (`ledger/05`).
6. **Marketing discipline:** zero creator dollars or founder content hours go to a dark-seeded city before its trigger fires. The bench is a backend readiness bet, not a parallel launch.

## What would prove me wrong

If Austin's score-recalibration turns out to be city-idiosyncratic — constants that don't transfer and instead require bespoke founder-weeks of tuning per market — the marginal cost stops being $600 and becomes $600 plus scarce founder attention, and the case inverts from cheap insurance to integrity liability. If Austin's founder-led content simply doesn't break out nationally in the first 90–120 days (stays under the corpus's own ~15K-view candidate threshold), there is no dilution to insure against yet, and the bench spend would have been better spent on Austin density or product. If Apple Developer enrollment, the paywall re-skin, or legal-URL blockers push the Austin launch date out further, any founder hour spent on bench-seeding instead of clearing those blockers is mis-sequenced — solo-founder time, not cash, is the real constraint. And if TikTok/Meta's DMA-level Spark targeting proves unreliable in execution — bleed into neighboring metros, or CPMs that spike disproportionately at city-level granularity — the recapture mechanism this case leans on doesn't work as cheaply as assumed, and the RPM-multiplier math weakens accordingly.
