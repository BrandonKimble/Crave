## Arc

Josh, M&A lead at Blue Throne (a platform that buys consumer apps for 7–8 figures to make the "1-to-10" jump after founders do "0-to-1"), presents a six-rule playbook for building an app toward a 9-figure exit on the Superwall podcast. Core thesis: valuation is a story — growth stories get ~6x forward run-rate, stalled apps get valued on trailing twelve months — and the levers are narrow category ownership, sticky cohorts, organic distribution, premium pricing (post-ChatGPT anchor reset), buyer-specific narrative, and selling before the peak.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- ChatGPT reset consumer price anchoring: pre-ChatGPT ceiling was "maybe 60 bucks a year" for consumer apps; ChatGPT normalized $20/mo, and consumers now pay it for apps that "really solve their problem." (claimed/opinion)
- "The cheapest path to $30 million of ARR is fewer payers at higher prices": at $4.99/mo you need ~500K payers for $30M ARR; at $20/mo only ~125K payers — less UA spend, less infrastructure. (arithmetic, first-party framing)
- Waking Up (Sam Harris): $20/mo, $150/yr; ~25K downloads/mo per Sensor Tower but >$1M/mo revenue — a ~10x revenue-to-download ratio vs the typical ~1:1 (host: 1:2–1:3 is "pretty good"). Josh is a paying user; explicitly not sponsored. (claimed — Sensor Tower estimate)
- Purpose ("your AI mentor," Mark Manson–associated): ~20K downloads/mo, ~$200K/mo revenue; similar ratio observed since launch day. (claimed, host's own research)
- Photoroom: $15–30/mo at $50M ARR. Calm: $70/yr — formerly the premium benchmark, now less than half Waking Up's $150/yr. (claimed)
- Advice: if charging under $10/mo, push price up via A/B tests on 10/15/20% of the audience — "try and find the ceiling." (first-party recommendation)
- Weekly pricing works only for short-duration-need categories (dating, Instagram-stalker/ex-tracking apps); weekly = weak long-term/exit strategy but can be a fine cash-flow business. Host's synthesis: "$20 a month is better than $10 a week if the $10 a week churns after one week — and it's better for the multiple."

### Retention & product

- Rule 2 "locals, not tourists": revenue multiples are "present value math on LTV"; leading indicator of LTV = cohort retention.
- Diagnostic: retention curve of your top 20% of users by engagement (pick your own engagement metric); if it flattens (higher = better) rather than plunging, "you may have a 9-figure asset hiding inside a consumer app" — i.e., SaaS-like retention inside consumer.
- Year-1 resubscriber benchmarks (general rule of thumb, category-dependent): 50% = amazing, 40% = good, 30% = okay, 20% = bad. Dating apps excluded (structurally lower).
- DAU is a weak signal (downstream of downloads/funnel); subscriber resubscribe rate is the strong signal — "did they get value from what they just spent a year paying for?" D7/D30 retention matters especially if there's a free tier.
- Apps with distribution but weak retention are still attractive to Blue Throne (their specialty = product + monetization work post-acquisition); e.g., "60,000 downloads organically a month, they're just not hanging around" is "pretty interesting."

### Content strategy (organic)

- Rule 3: organic install share benchmarks — 50%+ great, 35–50% good, under 20% not great ("UA treadmill"). "You have to earn it. You don't buy it."
- Josh's personal stance: TikTok is actually PAID, just less measurable/predictable — you paid for the video, creator, iterations even when attribution shows up as organic/ASO. No industry standard on classifying it.
- The diligence question isn't "last video that went viral" but "the machine you built to replicate it": how often you go viral, how fast you can respin a working creative into 20/50/100 videos.
- Growth-story credibility: 12 months of growth = a machine; 3 months "could be distilled down to three viral videos" — but early 3-month rockets can still attract buyers wanting to ride the trend up.
- Early-stage advice (six-figure ARR): crack organic as early as possible — "you don't want to be stuck at 10 million ARR fully relying on UA."

### ASO & app store

- 80% more apps submitted to the App Store this year than last (claimed, no source) — ranking organically much harder; top keywords dominated by legacy apps 5–8 years old.
- AI search: nobody he talks to (4–5 founders/day) names AI search as a growth strategy; no consumer app has visibly nailed it (host: e-commerce/B2B brands are doing well on it).

### Launch & sequencing

- Waking Up launched "distribution first" via Sam Harris's podcast/audience; Jeff Nippard's MacroFactor (transcribed "MacAractor" — uncertain) same pattern; Purpose via Mark Manson.
- If you have access to an influencer with deep audience trust, build the app WITH them — "distribution from day one" — but those people are very hard to find, and acquirers see influencer-bound distribution as fragile (the deal must keep the influencer making content; influencers resist earnouts).
- Focus one distribution channel until you find its ceiling (ROAS curves degrading) before adding channels — channel mastery over breadth.
- Flo team reportedly ran "over 100 experiments a month" (secondhand, from a graph he remembers); push experiment velocity on pricing, paywall UI/UX, colors, fonts early and frequently.

### Exit mechanics (Other)

- Two valuation paths: strong growth + 30–50% EBITDA margin → up to ~6x forward run-rate, ~$17M ARR can reach $100M+ valuation; slower growth → valued on LTM revenue, need ~$30–33M ARR for the same outcome. "People are paying for the growth story."
- Never extrapolate a single month (seasonality — fitness in Jan/Dec); use a quarter or 6 months if stable.
- Rule 1, category: define your category narrowly enough to be credibly #1 (Flo: health → women's health/period tracking, 70M+ MAU, first women's-health unicorn, $200M+ raise at $1B+; Runna: not #1 sports app but #1 couch-to-5K). Too-narrow = not a believable category; GLP-1 trackers possibly viable sub-niches if market grows.
- Market sizing method: Sensor Tower, cut into logical categories, top ~30 apps ≈ lion's share.
- Four buyer types: (1) aggregators/platforms (Blue Throne — recurring revenue, clean books, "can I 10x this"); (2) strategics — pay the HIGHEST multiples via synergies (Strava×Runna = funnel extension: couch-to-5K graduates into Strava; likely metric = beginner-runner top-of-funnel + graduation rate — explicitly his guess); (3) private equity — predictable cash flow + strong EBITDA, but founder must stay 3–5 years to run it; (4) acquihire/IP (Google×Character AI) — rare for consumer. IPO ≈ 2% of consumer companies, irrelevant.
- MyFitnessPal×Cal AI read: MFP bought the FUNNEL and the team's distribution skill ("Zach and the team are incredibly good distributors"), not the tech.
- Rule 6, timing: sell BEFORE you need to / before the peak — let the buyer pay for the coming peak. MyFitnessPal counterexample: Under Armour paid $475M in 2015 for future growth; growth stalled; Francisco Partners paid "over 100 million less" in 2020 for the past.
- Start building acquirer relationships 6–12 months pre-exit; study their last five deals; build the metric narrative that buyer wants. Ask any buyer what moved the needle on their past deals (ads? price testing? product?) to infer what they'd do to yours.
- Blue Throne acquisition range: apps doing ~$400K/yr minimum up to ~$15M/yr; team of 70 covering infra, R&D, QA, distribution, growth, monetization, ad monetization, CFO/HR functions.
- Raid Shadow Legends (Plarium): "a billion plus in revenue for many, many years." (claimed)

## Deal structures

No concrete creator/UGC deal terms (no flat fees, rev-shares, per-view rates). Influencer-founded app M&A discussed structurally only: deals must be constructed to keep the influencer on board making content, influencers typically refuse earnouts, making these apps hard to sell. Blue Throne deal shapes: buy outright with founder free to leave, or founder joins to "figure out product" with their resources.

## Contrarian positions

- TikTok/UGC is paid acquisition, not organic — against the common founder framing of UGC as "organic."
- DAU is a near-useless valuation metric; resubscriber rate is the one that matters.
- Raise prices aggressively — most consumer apps underprice post-ChatGPT; premium price is itself a positioning/valuation lever, not just revenue math.
- Don't diversify channels — most founders haven't found the ceiling of channel #1.
- Weekly pricing (currently trendy) is mostly a cash-flow play that damages exit value outside short-duration categories.
- Never try to sell at the peak; deliberately sell into visible growth.

## Crave transfer

The most transferable claim is the pricing thesis: the $20/mo post-ChatGPT anchor and the fewer-payers-at-higher-prices math argue Crave's $7.99/mo is nearer the old floor than the new ceiling, and Josh's concrete tactic (A/B price on 10–20% of the audience until it breaks) is directly runnable — though Waking Up's 10x ratio rides on Sam Harris's celebrity trust, which a cold-launch Austin utility does not have; treat that ratio as unreachable, not aspirational. The category rule maps cleanly: "the #1 dish-level food discovery app in Austin" is exactly the narrow-until-you-win framing (Flo/Runna pattern), and the year-1 resubscriber benchmarks (50/40/30/20) plus the top-20%-cohort flattening curve are the right internal health metrics for a repeat-use local utility. Discount heavily: nearly all app-level numbers are Sensor Tower estimates or secondhand deal lore from a buyer whose business benefits when founders adopt "reach out to acquirers early, sell before peak" behavior; none of the featured apps are local/geo-constrained, so the organic-share benchmarks (50%+ great) say nothing about whether a single-city app can generate that organic volume at all. His TikTok-is-paid stance is a useful sobriety check on Crave's no-budget UGC plan: the asset to build is the repeatable creative machine, not one viral hit.
