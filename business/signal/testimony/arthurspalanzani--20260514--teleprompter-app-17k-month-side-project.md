## Arc

First episode of Arthur Spalanzani's "Tap and Swipe" podcast. Guest Alexey is a full-time software engineer at a trading firm who, with co-founder Max, built a teleprompter/video-recording app (rendered "Everlize"/"Aveline's" in captions — name uncertain) natively twice for iOS and Android. The app did $17,000 in revenue in the last 30 days; neither founder has quit their job. The conversation covers side-project sustainability, why marketing is "pseudo science," A/B testing thresholds, Android economics, and organic-vs-paid distribution.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Current focus is "optimizing the funnel and the pricing" because better conversion or more money up front lets you "spend more money on ads... which means you can grow faster." (claimed strategy, no numbers)
- A/B testing needs volume: "You won't get any significant results if you don't have enough traffic. You need like at least 100 purchases on each [variant] to drive meaningful conclusions." (claimed rule of thumb)
- With low traffic, only test drastic changes: "Double your price and see what happens." (claimed)
- Tested a user-facing "enable free trial" toggle on the paywall: "It did not make any difference." (claimed, no data shown)

### Trial & onboarding

- Feedback-for-trial deal: in-app banner + push notifications offering "book a call with us and get three months free"; people booked calls even with little traffic. This is how they learned their users are "for the most part business owners who are trying to make content," not bloggers/creators. (first-party account, no counts)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Alexey finds organic "next level... even more vague, even more ambiguous" than paid; paid acquisition is "a fixed formula... an equation that you need to solve" — the closest distribution channel to engineering thinking. (opinion)
- Organic upside: "if you find something that works... it can just give you lots of free things without the limiting return on ad spend." (opinion)
- Posting once a week on a new Instagram page does nothing: "Absolutely not. If you're doing content, it needs to be a lot of content and very, very consistently" — e.g. at least one piece of content per day across three networks; "that's what your life is becoming." (claimed)
- Organic requires no upfront capital, unlike ads: "if you don't have a few spare thousand in the bank, it's pretty hard to do anything" with paid. (claimed)
- Host's hybrid approach: post organic, then put ad spend behind pieces that outperform, because "my CPM would be lower... my CPC will also be lower" on proven creative (host claim, no figures). Alexey counters that others argue organic performance does not predict ad performance — "I heard this saying so many times." (secondhand, contested)

### Paid ads

- Consulted multiple experts on running Meta ads for iOS apps: "if you talk to three people... you'll get three completely contradicting answers"; conclusion — no cohesive theory exists, "no one truly understands how all of these big marketing machines work," algorithms change constantly; just try things. (first-party experience)
- Intro framing (host): Alexey "tried to hire four different people to do his marketing, and... it failed every single time." (host paraphrase; not elaborated in the body of the transcript — the detail may have been cut)

### ASO & app store

- Screenshots "matter a lot, I believe." (opinion)
- Custom product pages: NOT tested — "one of the big missed opportunities. Some founders achieved like crazy conversion rates." (secondhand/speculation)
- Host: changing his app icon to increase contrast between logo and background color "doubled my conversion, literally." (host anecdote, no numbers/method)
- Host: ~95% of his own revenue comes from the App Store (iOS). (host claimed)

### Launch & sequencing

- Revenue: $17,000 in the last 30 days across both platforms; "our ambitions are way higher." (claimed, dashboard referenced on screen)
- Android ignored "for too long" — for "a few years" they assumed "Android users don't pay"; a proper test showed "that's not true... what the hell were we doing these two years?" Android only recently started picking up; now committed to both platforms. (first-party, no split figures)
- Android caveats: users are "less willing to pay there"; wrong-audience installs produce one-star reviews complaining the app is paid. Advice: start iOS-first; if cross-platform framework, Android is "just worth launching and testing"; two native codebases is "a lot of effort" (they built pre-AI-era, "figure it all out by hand"). (claimed/opinion)

### Retention & product

- Feedback loop: email works fine; an in-app popup to their servers "doesn't really matter, it works roughly the same." Rule: write feedback down; "once you have like 10 same feedbacks" consider building the feature. Example: Bluetooth microphone support built because of complaints (release status uncertain at recording). (first-party)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Two co-founders (Alexey + Max), both keeping full-time jobs. Quit threshold: "If our business is not doing a few millions per year, there's no reliable way of us going into it full-time." (claimed threshold)
- Employment valued for "sustainability and peace of mind" vs. the daily "roller coaster" of full-time founding. Goal is a business to go full-time into — ideally "10 different products... it's diversified, there's less risk." (opinion)

### Other

- Core thesis, repeated: "Distribution, that's the game. Product is okay. Often product doesn't even matter that much." Engineers over-invest in resilience/design; "the only thing that matters is you just get it over the line." Advice to dev-job builders: "Forget everything you've learned." (opinion)
- Marketing is "pseudo science": expects no cohesive picture; contradictory expert opinions can all work; pick one and test. (opinion)

## Contrarian positions

- "Product doesn't even matter that much" — distribution over product, stated bluntly to an audience of engineers.
- Do NOT quit your job on $17K/mo; the bar is "a few millions per year" — against the standard indie-hacker "quit at ramen profitability" narrative.
- Android is worth real money and the "Android users don't pay" consensus cost them two years — pushback on the iOS-only orthodoxy (while still advising iOS-first).
- Marketing expertise is unfalsifiable pseudo science; expert consultations yield contradictions, so stop seeking a coherent framework.
- A free-trial toggle A/B made no difference (against the common "trial toggles lift conversion" claim).
- Skepticism that organic-winning creative predicts paid-ad performance (contra the host's boost-your-winners playbook).

## Crave transfer

The most transferable claims are the cheap, testable ones: the ~100-purchases-per-variant threshold before trusting any paywall/pricing A/B (Crave's early Austin volume will be far below this — so test only drastic changes like price doubling, exactly as he says), and the "book a call, get N months free" banner as a near-zero-cost way to discover who actually pays (his users turned out to be business owners, not creators — Crave should expect similar audience surprises). His organic math — one post per day across three networks or nothing — is a sober planning input for Crave's no-ad-budget launch, though it comes from a tool app where he admits organic never cracked for him, so it's a warning about cost, not a proven playbook. Weakly transferable: everything category-specific — a teleprompter is a global-utility tool with session-based value, not a local-density repeat-use discovery product, so his funnel/Android/pricing observations carry no structural insight for a city-locked hard paywall. Discount the $17K headline itself: it's the podcast hook, read off his own dashboard with no cost, profit, or channel breakdown, and the host's "icon change doubled my conversion" is an unquantified anecdote worth an ASO experiment at most.
