## Arc

Brett and Zack, two ex-Loom employees (first PM / iOS engineer), built Coconote, an AI note-taker and study assistant for college students, bootstrapped it to $6.7M ARR and 2M registered users, and sold to Quizlet ~18 months after launch. The video exists to showcase their TikTok/Reels UGC playbook and monetization tactics on the Superwall podcast, which itself sells paywall tooling and community access.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Trial conversion rose **16%** after roughly doubling onboarding length; framed as "pretty huge for us." (claimed)
- Moving **login to after the paywall** was "significant" / "a big deal" — users pay before creating an account. (claimed, no number)
- Second paywall placement: show paywall **after** the user records an entire lecture ("an hour plus into using it") instead of before recording — "a big win." (claimed, no number)
- They are Superwall customers running paywall experiments; Brett now runs subscription growth at Quizlet/Cram and says experiments there "can easily increase revenue by a million dollars a year." (claimed/promotional)
- Host plug: Superwall's free AI tool trained on **422 profitable paywall experiments** at paywallexperiments.com; Superwall dinners for apps doing **$100K+/month**. (host promotion, not guest testimony)

### Trial & onboarding

- Onboarding lengthened from **~5 screens to ~13** ("at least doubled") in one big jump, after iterative 1–2-screen tests yielded only small wins — the jump drove the **16%** trial-conversion lift. (claimed)
- Added screens: login/account creation, push-notification permission, personalization ("understanding what they're studying").
- Anti-churn: in the cancel flow, offer a **7-day trial extension** — saves **27%** of users who enter the cancel flow from canceling in that moment; "a smaller percentage convert on the trial but it's still a big win." Most customers come in through free trial; "it's really hard to win them back once they turn it off." (claimed)

### UGC & creator deals

- Content team at its biggest: **10–12 part-time contractor creators** ("Navy SEALs, not the Navy"), all with high creative freedom; **content coaches at 1:12** coach-to-creator ratio.
- Hired **marketers, not influencers** — nobody on the team self-identifies as an influencer. Sourcing: Handshake, IG/TikTok DMs, email outreach, referrals; framed as talent-manager work finding "undiscovered talent" (e.g., Zack's Spanish tutor Luisa became one of their best creators, focused on LatAm/Spanish content, ~9 months from first ask).
- Lead creator (Allison) was recruited via DMs/email, led a contractor sub-team, and "did hundreds of millions of views with us"; team lead must have earned respect via own viral track record.
- Zack personally interviews **every** creator; decision within 15–30 minutes.
- Creator skill pyramid: (1) want to win / show up, (2) deconstruct others' viral videos (guess viral-or-not with view counts hidden), (3) create own scripts/trends.
- Creators sit in Slack with engineers/product and request **creator-only fake app screens** (e.g., a giant rainbow-wave recording screen with "Coconote.ai" in huge text) built purely to be eye-catching on camera — not real product screens.

### Content strategy (organic)

- Viral-but-low-intent case study: "PDF to brainrot" feature did **100–200M views** across videos; the flagship video hit **40M views**; produced **3,000 trial starts over a couple of days** (their highest ever) but converted to only **~$25,000** revenue vs a hypothetical **$360,000** at 100% conversion. Takeaway quote: "we would rather have 10 million views on a video that converts well… as opposed to a 40 million view video that… drives low-intent traffic."
- Higher-converting brand-page videos: "My mom just changed my college life forever" lecture-hall demo hit **18M views**, ~all within first 2 months, **~99% organic** (minimal boost spend; "haven't really found a ton of success" with paid boosting). Several brand-page videos had ~**20M views** each early on and "were converting much better" because they framed the product as a solution to a problem.
- 2048-study-game video (helpmestudy.com redirect): **2M views**; study-games as a lead-magnet concept.
- pdftobrainrot.com was a lead-magnet/Trojan-horse web funnel into the app.
- Timing thesis: "lazy" shock-hook + product-demo content works only while the product category is novel (theirs: March–May 2024); 6–8 months later the same style pulls a younger, lower-disposable-income audience and reads as a toy.
- Distribution-first founding assumption: decided pre-launch that short-form video (TikTok/Reels) was where students hang out; "if you build it, they will not come" (lesson from Zack's prior venture-backed studio Scout / music app Crew, which drove "hundreds of thousands of sign-ups" via early UGC-style content).

### ASO & app store

- First App Store screenshot says "great for ADHD" — deliberately congruent with ADHD-leaning content; copy like "never miss a key detail" is mined verbatim from user reviews/conversations. (claimed)
- At launch (~early 2024) there were essentially no competing AI-study-tool apps on the App Store; existing AI note-takers targeted professionals. (claimed)

### Launch & sequencing / revenue timeline

- **$100K ARR in 45 days; $1M ARR at 4 months; ~$2M ARR at 5 months; $5M ARR at 1 year; $6.7M ARR** crossed "last year." **2M registered users.** **>50% EBITDA.** Completely bootstrapped. Acquired by Quizlet ~**18 months** from launch. (all claimed, first-party)
- "Momentum is like oxygen for a startup" — early traction justified pouring in energy.
- M&A: inbound from strategics started when the company was a couple months old; advice = most M&A talks waste time; get a ballpark number after a couple of calls; if committed to selling, use an investment bank (they used Chris Parker at "Creative Partners" — name possibly garbled in captions) to keep founders from taking their eye off the ball.

### Retention & product

- Product-selection framework: winners combine (1) repeatable use case, (2) core to user identity, (3) a wow factor on top — all three simultaneously is hard and is the bar.
- Wow-moment half-life is shrinking; without one of the "7 Powers" moats (book by Hamilton Helmer — captioned as "Hemler," uncertain), copycats arrive "next week," so continuous wow-shipping is the growth engine.
- "What's possible today that wasn't possible 3–6 months ago" as a product-ideation lens (examples: Runna/Strava, Ladder).
- ADHD positioning leaned into from day one as a user-articulated need.

### Team, tools & cost structure

- **4 engineers**, all-native iOS/Android (no cross-platform), each must flex beyond one specialty; AI-assisted development credited for output.
- 10–12 part-time contractor creators + content coaches (1:12) = the whole marketing org.
- Tools plugged: Superwall (paywalls), viral.app (creator/UGC tracking — Zack knows the founders), Spytalk (host ad, not guest usage).

## Contrarian positions

- **Views are a vanity metric**: they explicitly deprioritized their own biggest viral hits (40M–200M views) because conversion was poor; prefer 10M targeted views over 40M novelty views.
- **Paid boosting doesn't work for them**: ~99% of an 18M-view hit was organic; "haven't really found a ton of success" putting money behind posts.
- **Longer onboarding converts better** — one big 5→13-screen jump beat iterative screen-by-screen testing.
- **Paywall before login**; also paywall AFTER an hour-plus of product use (full lecture recording) rather than gating up front.
- **Tiny elite creator team over scale**: 10–12 trained marketer-contractors vs. the common spray-and-pray influencer/volume approach.
- **Fake product screens built solely for content** — the recording UI in their best videos isn't the real app.

## Crave transfer

Category mismatch is the biggest caveat: Coconote is a single-utility AI-wow app with a screen-recordable "aha" (record lecture → notes appear) and a college-student TikTok-native audience; Crave's value is data density and repeat local utility, which doesn't demo in 3 seconds the same way — the "PDF to brainrot" lesson (viral novelty converts terribly) actually argues FOR Crave's restraint about chasing wow-format virality, and their conversion-over-views discipline transfers directly to any creator program. The most transferable, testable claims are the monetization mechanics, since Crave is also hard-paywall: longer personalization-heavy onboarding (+16% trial conversion), paywall-before-login, and the cancel-flow trial extension (27% save rate) — though Crave's pay-now monthly tier has no trial to extend, so the save tactic applies only to the annual-trial cohort and the "let them experience value before the ask" finding directly tensions Crave's hard-gate-everything model. Their creator playbook (10–12 part-time undiscovered marketers, 1:12 coaching, mine review language for ASO copy) is scale-appropriate for a solo founder and Austin-local talent (UT students) is a natural Handshake-style pool. Discount the revenue timeline entirely: bootstrapped-to-$6.7M-in-a-year is survivorship on a 2024 AI-category tailwind Crave doesn't have, told on a podcast whose sponsor they use and promote.
