## Arc

Kyle Fowler built Cardstock (baseball/sports card scanner + value lookup) starting ~2019 while in college, validated it with a Reddit post, launched at $4.99 paid, then rode a single ASO keyword niche ("baseball card scanner") to ~$10k/mo passive for ~2 years with zero ad spend. When he lost the #1 ranking in May 2024 he pivoted to organic TikTok — hiring his friend Jacob from Wendy's — iterated to a repeatable scan-and-reveal format, then scaled to mass slideshow distribution via the Noise platform (~$1 CPM, thousands of posts). Now ~$41k MRR combined across Cardstock and the newer Scanamon, ~$700k lifetime, fully bootstrapped.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Launched as a $4.99 one-time paid download; launch day = 12 paid downloads (~$60 day one) from the Reddit launch post. (first-party, App Store Connect on screen)
- Switching from paid-download/lifetime to a free download + subscription was "by far the biggest revenue jump ever for us... It was insane" — visible as the big 2021 bump on his lifetime chart. (first-party chart, though he audibly struggles to pin which bump is which — attribution partly reconstructed)
- Subscription pricing: $5/mo, $50/yr, $99 lifetime; kept a lifetime tier at launch of the sub model. (first-party)
- He initially disbelieved $5/mo recurring would sell ("It costs $15 for a Netflix subscription. Who's paying a third of that for Cardstock?") — advisors said "they'll buy it"; they did.
- Early sub-era paywall: 5 free scans then pay; paywall also shown on first open with an X button (soft) — "most of our subscriptions came from people just seeing the first paywall... most people would just buy it right then sight unseen without even using the product." (claimed)
- Later added a HARD paywall: "then we added a hard paywall and I was like oh my god we're making like 30k a month." Timeline: hired Jacob → hoped for $20k by end of next year → Christmas organic success → hard paywall → ~$30k/mo. (claimed; confounded with the concurrent TikTok success)
- On paid-download apps today: "there's just no reason not to do a hard paywall. If you insist on [paid download], you might as well just do free" — free+sub switch was the biggest jump.

### ASO & app store

- Core engine of the business: keywords "baseball card scanner" (biggest), "baseball card value", subtitle "find value for baseball cards", title "Cardstock sports card scanner" (because "baseball card scanner" didn't fit the character limit). (first-party)
- Held the #1 App Store ranking for "baseball card scanner" for 2–3 years; claims first-ever baseball card scanner app in the store.
- Result: ~$9–10k MRR (he says "like 14K I guess not recurring, like probably 9 or 10K recurring") passively for ~2 years through college, "not much done to the app," zero ad budget.
- Research process: entry point was Apple's $100 free Apple Search Ads promo → wanting to spend it optimally → Googled ASO (Sensor Tower / App Figures posts). Spent 1–2 weeks researching. Tools: Sensor Tower free trial ("with as many emails as you want"), tracks with Astro (Mac app).
- Method: brainstorm keyword list → check rankings/popularity → put highest-value ones in title + subtitle ordered by priority. "Just putting them in there is such a big win that takes like no time."
- Before optimization the title was just "Cardstock," probably no subtitle — "we weren't really getting that much passive at all."
- Lost the #1 ranking in May 2024 → forced the channel diversification. Space now crowded ("now there's a billion of them").
- New-niche advice: look for daily-life problems, check if the store serves them, use Sensor Tower/Astro trials to check keyword popularity. Self-caveat: "I threw one thing at the wall and it stuck," so he's "not the best at knowing" replicable niche discovery. (speculation, self-flagged)

### Content strategy (organic)

- TikTok started after May 2024 rank loss: hired friend Jacob (literally working at Wendy's). Neither knew content; first videos ~20–23 likes, no downloads. Iterated for a long time (memes, silent pack openings, brand-page content mimicking Duolingo/Flixbus/Brita — that model "is not how it works at all").
- Winning Cardstock format: show a desirable card 3–5 seconds with a suspense text hook ("what's this MJ card worth"), scan it with the app visibly in hand, reveal the value. Vlog/voiceover variant from card shows ("I was at a card show in Orlando and I found this Michael Jordan card...").
- The conversion goal is "what's the app?" comments — not link-in-bio from a grown brand page. CTA in caption: "probably no" measurable lift; not really needed since the app is on screen.
- Put the app's brand name/logo INSIDE the scanner UI so every video self-identifies; built a setting in Scanamon that toggles app-promo/marketing text for creator shoots. Copied from Cal AI ("Cali")/Fig-style scanners. "It's really hard to measure conversion rate in organic content. Almost impossible... might as well do the random little tweaks."
- Wrote a one-page internal TikTok strategy doc (hook theory, storytelling commentary, "every action/text/word should have a purpose," minimum time to convey the idea, feel organic) used as a feedback checklist with Jacob.
- Philosophy: prefer formats where you control every variable (hook text, first image, story) → repeatable series with testable variations, vs. uncontrollable negotiation-style footage.
- One first-party datapoint: an early in-format video hit 184k (he reads "1 in 84K" off screen — figure uncertain, likely 184k views); in-house Pokemon account got "multiple posts over 100,000 views."
- Used the apps at card shows for content AND arbitrage: bought a $500-value card for $30 and flipped it for ~$500 at the next show ("we out-compete most people because we have better data").
- Anti-agency stance: learn the channel yourself first — "you have to learn enough of how to do something yourself in order to recognize someone great at it." Only NOW (post-competence) engaging a UGC agency he could vet. Bootstrapping framed as a useful forcing function: "I couldn't afford to make the wrong choice hiring an agency."
- Channel meta-take: "these channels all probably work... which one are you best at? What can you make work for your company?... You probably just didn't try it hard enough." Cites weekly contradictory Twitter takes on paid ads.

### UGC & creator deals

- Scale phase = Noise platform: creators mass-post pre-templated slideshows. Economics: you pay ~$1–2 CPM total; Noise takes half; creators effectively get ~$0.50 CPM. (first-party dashboard shown)
- Output: ~7,600–8,000 posts in the last month, 9,000+ posts total in the run; 27M(?) views ("like 27 views" garbled — paired with the spend it reads as millions) for $2,600 spend over ~2 months (Oct–Nov). ≈ $1 per thousand views. Noise's cut on that: "we spent 2600... they made 1300 off us."
- Template mechanics: he supplies ~38 hook images + ~230 body images + pre-made hook captions in Google Drive/Noise; Noise auto-generates slideshow variants, attributes, and handles payouts; dashboard breaks performance down by text hook and image.
- Distribution physics: one outperforming video converts better (social proof: likes/bookmarks/comments = trust), but "a bulk of small videos is still good. They still convert" — most posts get hundreds/low-thousands of views; volume does the work.
- Also ran a direct-creator CPM campaign on "Growy" (name uncertain — auto-caption; possibly Grwy/Growee): creators pull photos from a Google Drive, hooks pre-made, minimum friction.
- Pack (another hired friend) posts the Pokemon slideshow account; they AI-generated a girl as the profile picture because "it appeals to girls more, guys aren't gonna mind... win-win." (claimed, no data)
- Caveats he flags himself: slideshows "seem to be trending out a little on TikTok"; "this probably doesn't work in a bunch of niches"; Noise found via Twitter networking, not search.

### Launch & sequencing

- Idea validation: pre-build Reddit post in r/baseballcards (~10–15 replies) — small but "huge for us"; a Reddit DM 6 years ago ("you still working on this?") is why he finished the app at all.
- Launch post linked back to the validation post ("some of you might remember me posting about this") — read as authentic, not an ad. Got banned later in r/OCcards for promo; r/baseballcards was fine.
- Sequence: paid $4.99 launch (2019/2020) → COVID-era: hired two friends, split rev three ways (he retained 100% ownership; they later left for internships) → ASO discovery → sub switch 2021 (big jump) → ~2 yrs of ~$10k/mo passive → May 2024 rank loss → TikTok organic → hard paywall + Christmas spike → ~$30k → Noise scale + Scanamon → ~$41k MRR, $700k lifetime.
- Scanamon: built on the existing framework with AI dev tools, launched fast specifically to test whether the marketing engine transfers to a bigger market (Pokemon > baseball cards). ~$20k monthly rev / ~$9k MRR; "got to figure out the retention and stuff."

## Deal structures

- Noise slideshow platform: advertiser pays ~$1–2 CPM per thousand views; Noise keeps ~50%, creator gets ~$0.50–1 CPM. $2,600 spend → ~7,600–8,000 posts in a month (9,000+ total), Noise's margin on him ~$1,300. Templates, attribution, and payouts handled by the platform.

## Contrarian positions

- Hard paywall > soft: no reason not to, given modern tooling; many subs came from a paywall shown before the product was even used.
- One niche, all-in — explicitly the OPPOSITE of the "throw many apps at the wall" app-Twitter meta: "I threw one thing at the wall and it stuck."
- ASO as a primary, durable channel (2 years of passive revenue) vs. the current TikTok-virality-first meta; but also: rankings are a single point of failure (May 2024).
- "Every channel probably works" — channel choice is about founder fit and effort, not finding the secret underpriced channel; the "how do you find underpriced channels" question is the wrong question.
- Volume of small posts converts; you don't need virality — 9,000 posts at hundreds of views each beats waiting for one hit (though one hit converts better per view).
- Don't hire an agency/creator expert until you've done the channel yourself well enough to evaluate them.
- Brand-page content marketing (Duolingo-style) does NOT convert to downloads for a utility app; product-demo-in-hand does.
- Optimizing individual video CTAs is unmeasurable in organic; ship cheap structural tweaks (brand in the UI) instead of measuring.

## Crave transfer

Category match is decent: Cardstock is a repeat-use utility with a data moat (card values), not an AI-wow app — structurally closer to Crave than most Superwall guests. Transfers well: (1) hard-paywall confidence — his "people bought sight unseen at the first paywall" plus the hard-paywall revenue step supports Crave's card-required gate, though his $5/$50 price points are below Crave's $7.99/$39.99 and his users had already seen the product demoed in the exact TikTok that brought them; (2) the show-the-app-in-hand, value-reveal-as-payoff demo format maps directly to "scan this street / here's the #1 taco in Austin" content, and branding inside the visible UI so every video self-attributes is cheap and directly copyable; (3) ASO for a genuinely unserved keyword — worth checking whether "austin food ranking / best dishes austin" style keywords have any Cardstock-like gap, but food discovery is nothing like the empty 2020 "baseball card scanner" SERP, so expect no 2-year moat. Does NOT transfer: the Noise $1-CPM slideshow engine monetizes a nationwide audience — Crave's Austin-only funnel would pay for ~99% geographically wasted views unless targeting exists; and his numbers are one survivor's single niche, self-flagged as such, told on a podcast whose host sells paywall tooling and the community he credits. Treat the channel-agnostic meta-lesson ("pick one channel, iterate a controllable repeatable format until it converts") as the real transferable asset, not any specific platform.
