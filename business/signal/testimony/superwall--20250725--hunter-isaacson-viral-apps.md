## Arc

Hunter Isaacson, builder of NGL (anonymous Instagram-story messaging) and a string of prior consumer-social apps totaling ~70M downloads pre-NGL, walks through his portfolio in Figma and explains his playbook: build viral loops on top of existing distribution graphs (Instagram foremost), keep the product dead simple, monetize a tiny power-user slice with cheap weekly subscriptions. The video exists as Superwall-sponsored content-marketing and to promote the host's Consumer Club community.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- NGL revenue is "a multi-8 figure a year business," derived primarily from a **weekly subscription** priced by region/GDP per capita, "anywhere between a dollar and $7" — deliberately "a low impulse buy." (claimed)
- "A very small percentage of our downloads actually monetized. It's primarily used as a free product" — paid tier is only for power users (insights on who's sending messages / viewing profile, extra "link games"). Revenue works purely because of enormous free-user volume. (claimed)
- Bags monetization: **1% fee on transactions**, with "the majority of that fee" passed back as commission to the friend who invited you or the group-chat member whose token post you bought from; plus token launchpad with a **1% royalty on every trade** — "you could run up $2 million of volume and you'll make $20,000." Deposits via Apple Pay through MoonPay (transcribed "Moonay" — uncertain, likely MoonPay). (claimed)

### Trial & onboarding

- NGL onboarding is "15, 20 seconds" — drop the user straight onto the core screen. "One flow, one funnel, don't overcomplicate it," organized around a single north-star metric: people posting the link and posting at least two replies. (claimed)
- Copywriting sells the end result, not the install: "get your own messages" converts far better than "download this app"; "a very very high ratio" of new users hitting that screen go straight to the App Store (no number given). (claimed)
- Multi-step tutorials (3–6 taps, e.g. adding an app to the Instagram share menu or installing a Safari extension) only work "if prior to that tutorial, the user's fully sold" on the value — sell first, then educate. Measure fall-out per step. (claimed/opinion)

### UGC & creator deals

- Pre-virality NGL marketing: small scattered influencer tests — "$100 here, $100 there, paying someone to post the Instagram story." One paid influencer post activated a school "on the other side of the world" ~4 months into the 6-month dead period. (claimed)
- The breakthrough was **one TikTok** — a girl showing a controversial NGL message ("I was with your boyfriend last night") — that got "like a million views very quickly" and drove "our first thousand users in one day," then activated global spread "over the next week." They tested "a bunch of different message texts" (funny/controversial/edgy hooks) before one hit. (claimed)
- PopStream (celebrity FaceTime product) died because "getting a top tier influencer to just post a link was like 20 grand" at the time — unit economics didn't work. (claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Core framework: "find existing things that happen on other networks... and just switch the distribution mechanism" — build on the biggest distribution graph available (Instagram > Snapchat > X > Facebook). NGL out-scaled predecessors (YOLO, Yik Yak [transcribed "yik asm" — garbled], Formspring) because it rode the Instagram graph, unlocked when Instagram opened story links to all accounts (previously 10k-follower minimum) at end of October 2021. (claimed)
- NGL's retention mechanic: churned users re-enter because friends' link-in-bio keeps re-exposing them — "high redownload rate" offsets churn. In some countries it's "every teenager... 17, 18, 19 year olds" with it as their link in bio. (claimed)
- Zoom University: grew via meme pages posting funny content; **40,000-person waitlist** with a **7:1 girl-to-guy ratio** ("in dating is unheard of"); went **top 10 in the App Store social category**; "maybe a couple hundred thousand downloads." Lesson: women participate when paired with a friend (quadruple opt-in double dating). (claimed)
- Instagram share-menu real estate (recipe-app idea, Fade): getting installed there takes 3–4 taps but "now it's there forever" — every Instagram scroll surfaces your app. Fade "went close to number one." (claimed/secondhand for Fade)

### Paid ads

- The $10K–$20K/month scaling question: "a good amount of that money should be put towards either UGC with influencers or ads," broken into **many small experiments** — "don't spend all the money on one specific creative. I would just test like a dozen of them," tracking which cohort came from which ad/TikTok. (speculation/advice)
- Pair spend with product-led growth so effective CPI is "considerably lower because a certain percentage of users are bringing somebody else" — the flywheel is hook-format discovery + invite mechanics. (advice)
- Gaming is the exception: "most of it is run by paid," extremely competitive, mobile gaming companies "have so much money to burn on ads" — "if you're a small guy... games is not where you start. It's double hard" (fun + hour-plus retention + paid-dominated acquisition). (opinion)

### Launch & sequencing

- NGL timeline: built in ~1 month after the Oct-2021 Instagram link change (Halloween Zoom call); then **6 months of nobody using it**; rebrand + rename + ngl.link domain mid-way; first blip at month ~4 from a paid influencer; built Android "very quickly" (May 2022) after realizing the activated graph was a lower-income country; the viral TikTok came right after Android shipped. (claimed)
- "We thought everyone was going to do this... No one cloned us until after we went viral." (claimed)
- Track record context: first app took **2.5 years** (started 2017, paid a dev agency, died in TestFlight, sunset 2020); Wink did **70 million downloads**; NGL now **150 million monthly active users** "3 years later." Also: "there's a TestFlight graveyard" of unshipped apps. (claimed)

### Retention & product

- NGL barely changed post-virality: "optimized and refined," not expanded — predecessors died partly because they "overcomplicated" (added feeds, profile tools) after success. (claimed/opinion)
- The mini-games app had "some of the highest average time spent of any app I've ever built. Close to an hour per user." (claimed)
- Success skill: predicting what a person will feel looking at a screen and how that emotion dictates the requested action — groupthink/social psychology as the core competency. (opinion)
- Always add social/invite elements: "I put that in every product... it's like an endorsement" when a friend texts or story-posts an app. (opinion)
- Link-tap flow is a "lightweight interruption" inside Instagram — easy to bypass, "not going to be like a 10-minute funnel." (claimed)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- NGL built by "myself and a few friends"; described as staying "extremely profitable" (host framing: "hundreds of millions of downloads, 8 figures revenue"). No headcount or cost figures given. (claimed/host-framing)

### Other

- Crypto PvP games "will happen, it's a question of when" — no one has "packaged it right"; failure mode is focusing on the money over the player experience. (speculation)
- Credit-card-recommendation Safari-extension idea (host's): card companies "will pay you like up to $500 per person that you refer" (host's number, not Hunter's); Hunter advises a company doing this — works only past the user-education hump. (secondhand/speculation)

## Deal structures

- Influencer seeding: ad-hoc micro-payments, "$100 here, $100 there" for Instagram story posts / TikToks (pre-viral NGL). No structured terms discussed.
- Top-tier influencer link post priced ~$20,000 circa 2020–21 (PopStream era) — cited as economics-killing.
- Bags user rev-share: majority of the 1% transaction fee passed to the inviting friend / token poster; launchpad creators earn 1% royalty on all trades ($20K per $2M volume example).
- No CPM/per-view/flat-fee creator contracts detailed. Otherwise none discussed.

## Contrarian positions

- Mass TikTok virality is NOT wasted on churny users — if the viral loop lives in friends' bios, churned users re-enter; churn + high redownload beats standard retention framing.
- Don't monetize the masses: run a giant free product and convert "a very small percentage" via cheap weekly impulse-buy subs — against the maximize-ARPU/hard-paywall meta (notable given the Superwall sponsorship).
- Post-virality, resist adding features; simplicity is the moat. Competitors died by adding feeds/tabs.
- Multi-step onboarding friction (4–6 taps) is fine if the user is pre-sold — against the remove-all-friction orthodoxy.
- Consumer social built on someone else's graph is still wide open in 2025, and it augments rather than competes with the host platform.

## Crave transfer

Almost none of the growth mechanics transfer directly: NGL's engine is a person-to-person viral loop (your link in a friend's bio) with a near-zero-value-prop install ("get your own messages"), while Crave is a paid, single-player, local utility with no inherent K-factor — and Hunter's monetization model (free for ~everyone, $1–7 weekly impulse subs on a tiny power-user slice at 150M-MAU volume) is the exact opposite of Crave's hard $7.99/mo paywall, so his pricing testimony argues against Crave's model only if you ignore the category mismatch. What does transfer: (1) sell the end result before asking for the install or the tutorial — Crave's onboarding-to-paywall should pre-sell the payoff ("the 10 best dishes within a mile") before any friction, since Hunter's evidence says pre-sold users tolerate multi-step friction; (2) the small-experiments doctrine for any creator/UGC budget — a dozen $100-level tests hunting for one hook format, not one big creative; (3) one flow, one funnel, one north-star metric. Heavy discounts apply: all numbers are unverified founder self-report, he's a survivor with an admitted graveyard of failures, and the interview is sponsored content. His "ride the biggest graph" framework is worth a thought experiment for Crave (what's the Instagram-story-link equivalent for local food content — TikTok food creators' comment sections? the share menu on food videos?) but is not evidence Crave's utility category can go viral that way.
