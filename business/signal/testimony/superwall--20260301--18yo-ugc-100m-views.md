## Arc

Driss, an 18-year-old who ran the UGC program at Cluely (the hyper-viral 2025 startup), left to start Instinct (a distribution agency) and Methods (a creator marketplace framed as "the new way to make money online"). He claims $1.2M annualized contract value signed in the first 7 days with no product, and a client campaign that did 100M views in 2 weeks at sub-30-cent CPM. The episode is a creator-psychology and UGC-playbook interview, with heavy promotion of Methods' new self-serve platform.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Cluely UGC program: scaled "from zero to 100 million views at sub $1 CPM" in the first few weeks; Cluely lifetime "hundreds of millions of views." (claimed, first-party role)
- Cluely UGC ran on **fewer than 20 consistently posting creators**. "You do not need... scale immediately... start with a few creators that are able to consistently get you views." (claimed)
- Counter-example company he consulted: **2,000 creators producing only 500,000 views in the last month** — creator count ≠ views. (secondhand)
- Power law: ideally one creator generating "a million, 10 million views a day"; the **top 1% or 0.1% of creators are ~90% of the views**; everything else is experimentation. (claimed heuristic)
- Client campaign (Decart [uncertain; captioned "Deckard"], "billion-dollar AI lab," 60 engineers / 2 marketers): **100 million views in 2 weeks at sub-30-cent CPM**, app hit **top 30 in the App Store**, lifetime **400+ million views**. (claimed)
- UGC economics for companies he's worked with: **4x higher converting, 10–20x cheaper on average than any other paid user-acquisition source**. "UGC is the method." (claimed, no methodology given)
- Why UGC programs fail: (1) they find one format and stick with it instead of experimenting; (2) managing creators at scale is hard.
- Once a format works, "if a format can go viral once... it can go viral 100 or 1,000 more times with different companies in different niches and different creators" — send the working format to more creators, then kill it and expand.
- Format sourcing: don't invent — most successful formats are taken from other companies/creators in similar or different niches and adapted; "all new ideas are references of existing works."
- It's "much easier to make someone a better creator... versus 10x or 100x your number of creators."

### Team, tools & cost structure (Methods / Instinct)

- Left Cluely, signed contracts worth **$1.2M annualized in the first week**, "in stealth," with **no product** — pure sales calls promising views/users/revenue. (claimed; annualized contracts, not revenue)
- Methods stats: **$500,000+ paid out to creators; ~50,000 creators** (he says the public numbers are "slightly outdated" i.e. now higher). (claimed)
- Pays creators **$40 for their very first video** regardless of experience (once stated as "$30" earlier in the same riff — likely the same offer, number inconsistent in transcript); framing: immediate reward beats delayed gratification, casino-style hook (host's analogy).
- Marketplace cold-start solved by signing companies first, then recruiting creators with the $40 offer.
- Methods user count "**doubling every two weeks**"; claims first search result for "make money online." (claimed)
- Growth loop: people see a Methods video on TikTok/IG → join → make more Methods videos ("infinite user method") → then join client programs.
- Platform provides exact scripts, example videos, account warm-up guidance, lighting how-tos, and per-post AI feedback.
- Biggest churn problem: creators quit when video 1–2 gets ~100 views; retention requires reinforcing that persistence wins — longest-tenured creators are the highest paid on the platform.
- "Biggest signal" since: "several more million ARRs after more than 7 days." (claimed, vague)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Framing > product: renaming Methods from "UGC marketplace" to "the new way to make money online" flipped growth from stalled to explosive. Same on the B2B side: sell "we'll make you money / make you viral," never "influencer marketing."
- Twitter launch cinematics (Cluely-style) = highest top-of-funnel for tech/investors/hiring, but UGC on TikTok is what actually drives users and conversions; the famous Cluely launch video was partly rage-bait aimed at investors, not users.
- Content is a scale game: "keep posting until something hits," then copy the format. His Minecraft channel: **6 months of zero views**, then blew up → **200,000 subscribers in 9 months, 180 million views, top-500 fastest-growing channel** (sold ~4 years ago). (first-party anecdote)
- Viral sense doesn't fully transfer: every new platform/niche requires regaining instinct on what people enjoy.
- Founders should make content themselves before hiring for it: "how hard is it to record one 30-second video every day?" Don't hire for things you're inexperienced in (early stage).

### Retention & product

- "People want to feel performative" — perceived efficacy beats actual efficacy. Duolingo cited: hundreds of millions of users despite (his claim) not really teaching languages; same logic for standalone calorie trackers and Bible-quote apps.
- Standalone single-task apps beat general AI interfaces because the experience is abstracted and simple; "the average person... needs to be told exactly what something is and exactly how to use it."
- Value accrues at the application layer, never the underlying technology (internet analogy: Google/Amazon/Netflix, not protocol inventors); prediction: within 3–5 years people won't say "AI" (stigma of "artificial").
- Mainstream dislikes AI: anecdote of Minnesota friends saying they'd boycott products that visibly use AI. If OpenAI's ~1B MAU is face value, ~7B people haven't touched AI. (speculation/secondhand)
- Hardware take: hardware should enhance software, not vice versa. Plaud as the model: **$130 device + $20/mo software, claimed ~$250M ARR in 2024** — specific niche (professionals), great software, hardware removes friction. (secondhand, figure unverified)
- Predicts a mainstream AI photo/video editor app within a year ("the next Instagram/Snapchat"); bullish on AI-for-fun/content-creation category; no dominant image-gen consumer brand yet (Midjourney lived on Discord too long).

### Launch & sequencing

- Sold contracts before building product ("we had no product... 'We're going to get you a lot of views'").
- "Work with winners and make them even bigger winners... I don't want to work with losers" — Instinct only takes clients already winning.
- Startups = survival game: outlast competitors and take their employees/market share.

## Deal structures

- No creator exclusivity, CPM-rate cards, or affiliate terms discussed.

## Contrarian positions

- You need very few creators (<20) — reject the "recruit thousands of creators" model; quality/consistency over headcount.
- Pay creators up front ($40 before any results) — inverts standard performance-only UGC deals; treats first payout as an acquisition/retention cost.
- Perceived usefulness beats actual utility for consumer apps (Duolingo argument) — explicit product-truth heresy.
- Ignore all advice/mentors/books: "no one has any idea what they're doing"; copying the greats guarantees mediocrity; question every default.
- Don't innovate formats — steal and adapt what's already viral; originality is recombination.
- Framing is the product: identical offering renamed ("make money online" vs "UGC platform") flipped growth; sell outcomes, never the mechanism.
- UGC beats paid ads: 4x conversion, 10–20x cheaper (against the paid-UA-first consensus for subscription apps).

## Crave transfer

The most transferable claims are structural, not numerical: small-roster UGC (<20 consistent creators, format-copying, kill-and-expand) is exactly the shape a solo founder with no ad budget can run in one city, and "founder makes the content first before hiring creators" is directly actionable for Austin launch. The framing lesson maps cleanly: sell "never eat a bad meal in Austin again," never "Reddit-mined dish ranking." But every headline number here comes from viral-native, single-session AI-wow or make-money-online products with national/global TAM — 100M views in 2 weeks, sub-30¢ CPM, and top-30 App Store are not achievable or even meaningful for a geo-fenced utility where 99%+ of viral reach can't convert (non-Austin viewers hit a hard paywall for a city they don't live in); the 4x-converting/10–20x-cheaper-than-paid claim is unmethodologied self-promotion from someone selling UGC services. His "perceived efficacy beats utility" thesis actually cuts against Crave's core bet (objective data-density, repeat use) — worth noting as a paywall/onboarding lesson (make the score FEEL authoritative instantly) rather than a product strategy. Treat all ARR/payout figures as unaudited founder marketing; the durable takeaways are the mechanics (few creators, format iteration, outcome framing, founder-generated content), not the magnitudes.
