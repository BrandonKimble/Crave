## Arc

Zach Yadegari, a 17-year-old high-school senior from Long Island, shows the host App Store Connect proving Cal AI (snap-a-photo calorie counter) did $1.14M in October 2024, six months after launch. He previously built and sold an unblocked-games website for $100k, then partnered with Blake Anderson (Umax, RizzGPT) as co-founder for high-level strategy. The episode is a detailed walkthrough of Cal AI's influencer-marketing machine — and a soft launch for viral.tech, the SaaS version of their internal tooling.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Cal AI pricing: **$30/year or $10/month**; "most people go for the yearly." (claimed)
- **Premium-only hard paywall**: "you can't use the app without paying," but with a **3-day free trial**. (first-party account of own product)
- Anti-freemium lesson from GrindClock: launched free-with-ads + premium upgrade during a 20k-download surge; after switching to hard paywall, "we saw more revenue coming even though we have fewer downloads." "Once people are used to something being free... they don't want to pay." (claimed, own experiment)
- Explicit advice: utility-app builders "shouldn't be scared to just hard paywall and not let anyone use the app without paying upfront."
- **50% profit margins**, fully bootstrapped, "profitable every day." (claimed)
- Apple pays out ~2 months after revenue is earned — they reinvest month-N-minus-2 payouts into current-month marketing; hadn't paid themselves anything as of recording. (claimed)

### Trial & onboarding

- Onboarding asks weight, target weight, height, goals + "why" questions, then generates a calorie/protein plan.
- **A/B-tested finding: purposeless extra onboarding questions raised paywall conversion.** Questions with "no purpose" for the plan ("why are you doing this, how will this help your life") made users articulate commitment before hitting the paywall — "by the point they see the trial option they are more likely to get it." (first-party A/B claim, no numbers given)
- Framing at the paywall: sunk effort + "it's just a free trial for three days... you may as well take the first step."

### UGC & creator deals

- Core math: pay influencers a **CPM (cost per 1,000 views) below your RPM (revenue per 1,000 views)**. Starting heuristic when launching: assume **$5 RPM** and pay ~**$2–3 CPM**. (claimed)
- RPM measured as revenue correlated **immediately after video posting** (not LTV-adjusted; retention unknown, app <1yr old).
- Deals are **flat fees predicted from past performance**: average an influencer's last ~10 videos (e.g., 50k avg views × $2 CPM = ~$100/video). Creators reject pure pay-per-view except the smallest ones.
- Funnel numbers: **~1 in 100 influencers reply to DMs**; "after 500 DMs maybe you get two decent influencers." "People give up after DMing 100 influencers — you have to DM thousands." (claimed)
- Negotiation tactics: bundle **4 videos/month paid upfront** to cut effective price; **monthly retainers** are the standard scaling deal; **minimum-view clauses** ("guarantee this amount of views or keep posting"); reframe an ask like "$4,000 for one video" as "$4,000, I don't care how many videos" and stretch the deliverables. "Creators are a dime a dozen — for every creator you find you could find 12 more."
- Current scale: **~150 influencers posting regularly**, mostly monthly retainers; spend ranges **$100/month to $100,000 per two months** per influencer, priced on views AND engagement quality.
- Engagement diligence: read comment sections for real community (top comments >100 likes, replies to each other); red flags = same accounts commenting every video, comment sections full of other influencers (story-ring check on Instagram) = comment-pod farming.
- **DM, don't email**: email routes to managers, who quote much higher rates.
- Sourcing: create a fresh TikTok account, train the FYP purely on the target niche (health/fitness), scroll and log creators; started as founder-does-it + Google Sheet, now done by **virtual assistants**; a social-media manager closes deals on calls.
- Outreach account matters: with ~40k followers on the brand account, reply rates rose; early on a verified personal account works better ($10/mo Instagram verification).
- Creative direction: creators keep creative control; requirement = **show the product in the first 15 seconds**; no scripted hard-sell. Typical format: "what I eat in a day" with the app shown but **never named** — app name deliberately placed at top of the screen so viewers ask "what app is this" in comments.
- **Comment-section control**: they plant their own comments, require creators to pin a comment naming the app, and reply to everyone. "Controlling the comment section" is called a big part of the strategy.
- Content rights: Cal AI keeps every influencer video and **reposts all of them on its own Instagram account — "tens of millions of views a month"** from reposts alone; same video can be reposted after a few months' gap. TikTok flags duplicates (reposting only works on Instagram). (claimed)
- Attribution: link-in-bio tracking fails because viewers go straight to the App Store/Google; instead they **correlate download/revenue spikes with video post times and view curves** — the core feature of viral.tech.

### Content strategy (organic)

- Idea selection rule: **validate the marketing channel before building** — "making sure we know how we're going to blow it up... hundreds of health and fitness influencers... we just needed to build it and show it to them."
- Only build apps with **inherent virality**: the product demo itself (photo → calories) is the hook; "the influencer doesn't even need to mention it."
- TotallyScience precedent: self-made TikToks (screen recordings in class, text-to-speech) got millions of views; "comment what games you want next" reply-video series drove a loop; grew to **5M users** solo, **$60k/year** revenue, sold at 16 for **$100k** on Flippa. (Note: transcript reads "$660,000 a year" at one point and "few hundred dollars a day" at another — the number is garbled; $60k/yr is consistent with a $100k sale price, treat the exact figure as uncertain.)

### Paid ads

- Paid ads started only ~2 months ago (month ~4–5), after influencer engine matured; currently **$7,000/day**, "profitable every day," mainly Meta (FB+IG) plus TikTok. (claimed)
- "90% of paid ads is the creatives" (attributed to others, endorsed) — their edge is the archive of influencer creatives to test.
- Influencer marketing framed as **higher-profitability and easier to start**; paid ads = the scaling channel "years into the future." Sequence he endorses: product → DM ~100s of creators → 2 first videos → grow to ~30 posting → build archive of ~100 creatives → cycle profits into paid ads.
- Considering **TV ads** as a next-scale hypothesis. (speculation)

### ASO & app store

- Cal AI ranks **~#10 in the App Store fitness category**, behind only long-established apps like MyFitnessPal. (claimed)
- **Apple Search Ads surprised him positively**; his heuristic: bid on a specific competitor's name — **if that keyword is profitable for you, it signals your product is better than theirs**; unprofitable = worse product. "Not a one-for-one thing."
- SEO: "we're really bad at search engine optimization right now"; not a priority at 6 months old.

### Launch & sequencing

- Timeline: launched ~May 2024; goal was $50k/mo by end of summer; hit **$600k/mo by end of summer**, ~$800k when the podcast was booked, **$1.1M+/mo** at recording. (revenue first-party via App Store Connect screen; intermediate points claimed)
- Team at $1.1M/mo: **12 people + 4 co-founders** (Zach 17, Henry 17, Blake Anderson 23, Jake 28). Scaling moves at each milestone: after $100k/mo added developers, a full-time designer, more marketing help, more VAs.
- Partnership origin: cold outreach on X to Blake Anderson (Umax + RizzGPT, "a few million downloads" combined); Blake joined as co-founder for high-level strategy; idea chosen jointly partly BECAUSE it could go viral.
- Now becoming an **app studio**: next apps will stay in health/fitness specifically to reuse the same influencer network — "plug them in and blow them up... extremely fast"; bundling two apps into one influencer deal = leverage to lower CPM.
- Building **viral.tech** (launch ~early Dec 2024): go-to-market generator, influencer CRM, post/view tracking, download-correlation attribution via App Store Connect. Rationale: the playbook will become public knowledge anyway; better to own the platform.

### Retention & product

- Retention unknown: "we haven't seen our retention because we're less than a year old... we're going to find out soon." $5 RPM is immediate, not LTV.
- GrindClock counter-datapoint: after stopping all marketing, revenue **flatlined at the same monthly level for a year** (churn ≈ organic acquisition). (claimed)
- Product philosophy: radical simplicity vs MyFitnessPal's bloat — calories, carbs, protein, fat only; single-player, no social/gamification; simple onboarding then straight to scanning.
- Accuracy: v1 was **one ChatGPT prompt, ~80% accurate — equal to the FDA-permitted margin of error on food labels**; now a multi-prompt pipeline reaching **~90% average accuracy**. Users expected "x-ray vision" (steak under rice in a Chipotle bowl) and email angrily — required an in-app first-scan guide. Lesson: lower your baseline assumption of user technical intuition to "foolproof."
- Planned viral experiment: free-for-a-day on Thanksgiving with a themed scan + prompt to share to Stories (Spotify-Wrapped-style "how festive is your dinner"). (untested at recording)
- End goal: **beat MyFitnessPal** — cited (secondhand, uncertain) at "$100 million a year" / "$8–10 million a month," stagnant, sold by Under Armour at a loss; optimal exit = force MFP to acquire them.

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Stack: **native Swift** (Flutter/React Native "feel janky"), Firebase Cloud Functions backend, Mixpanel analytics, Slack.
- Influencer ops: VAs for sourcing/outreach, one social-media manager for closing calls, all DMs from the shared brand account.
- Chose editors-vs-VAs tradeoff: rather than hiring 10 editors to remix creatives across sub-accounts, hire 10 more VAs to source more influencers.

## Deal structures

- Flat fee per video derived from (avg views of last ~10 videos) × ($2–3 CPM target), e.g. 50k avg views → ~$100/video.
- Standard scaling deal: **monthly retainer, 4 videos/month, paid upfront** (upfront bundling used to negotiate the rate down).
- Range across roster: **$100/month up to $100,000 per two months** for one influencer.
- **Minimum-view clause**: paid their asking price but must guarantee X views or keep posting until hit.
- Pay-per-view deals exist only with the smallest creators.
- Payment rails: PayPal or bank transfer, "whatever they want."
- Content rights: brand keeps and reposts all videos on owned accounts.
- Required: pinned comment naming the app; product shown in first 15 seconds.

## Contrarian positions

- **Hard paywall everything** — no freemium, no ads tier, for utility apps; free users train themselves never to pay (directly against the dominant freemium orthodoxy).
- **Useless onboarding questions increase conversion** — friction before the paywall helps, contradicting "minimize onboarding friction."
- **DM creators, never email** — managers/email are a worse channel with higher rates.
- **Don't let creators say the app's name** — invisible integration + engineered comment-section discovery beats disclosed sponsorship.
- **Validate distribution before building the product** — if there's no influencer niche to tap, don't build it.
- Link-based attribution is dead for apps; correlate revenue spikes to post timing instead.
- Crypto = "just gambling."

## Crave transfer

The strongest transferable claims are the paywall ones: hard-paywall-plus-short-trial at a nearly identical price point ($30/yr vs Crave's $39.99/yr) generating 50% margins in a utility category, and the A/B-tested finding that commitment-building onboarding questions raise paywall conversion — both align directly with Crave's card-required onboarding and are worth stealing wholesale. The influencer math (start at $5 RPM / pay $2–3 CPM, flat fees off trailing-10-video averages, retainers, minimum-view clauses, DM-not-email) is a genuinely concrete playbook, but Cal AI's economics rest on a globally scalable, inherently viral one-shot demo with hundreds of fitness influencers to tap; Crave is single-city at launch with local-only value, so national-CPM math breaks — the analog is Austin-local food creators, a far thinner pool where per-creator terms matter more than DM volume. His "validate the distribution channel before building" test actually cuts against Crave as specced and should be answered deliberately: who are the 50 Austin food-content accounts and what does the never-name-the-app "what I ate in Austin" integration look like? Discount his retention silence heavily — he admits they have no retention data and RPM is measured on immediate post-video revenue, whereas Crave's whole thesis is repeat use; and remember the influencer segment is pre-launch marketing for his own tool (viral.tech), so the ease and margins of the playbook are being sold, not just reported.
