## Arc

Joseph Choi runs Consumer Club, a Discord community where indie and VC-backed consumer-app founders trade TikTok-organic distribution tactics. The episode is a tour of the current "underpriced distribution" meta — mass UGC, slideshows, comment-section conversion, AI avatars — argued from e-commerce precedents (Tabs Chocolate) and app case studies (Cal AI, Focus Tree, NGL). It exists to promote Consumer Club and Superwall to app founders; treat it as a tactics catalog, not audited data.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Influencer marketing is the "old way": influencers ask $1,000–$2,000 per video (host: $2–3k plus a retainer) and the video can flop at ~800 views. (claimed)
- Cheaper model: do the creative direction yourself — hand a creator an existing proven video to copy "second for second," modifying only the product plug — so you pay for their time, not creativity. Cited as how Consumer Club members avoid "$1,000–$2,000 for one video." (claimed, no exact per-video rate given for the cheap tier)
- Tabs Chocolate (Oliver Brocato): $0→$10M in 1 year, pure TikTok organic, zero ads, no influencers — hired teenagers to post organic-feeling product videos on brand-new zero-follower accounts. Brocato then pivoted to consumer SaaS (Study Buddy) explicitly for better margins/multiples. (secondhand, oft-repeated story)
- Volume operation from a Consumer Club dinner: one founder posts 15 UGC videos/day per person by running 5 accounts per phone × 3 posts/day/account (3/day is the believed shadowban ceiling); flew to the Philippines, gave creators iPhones with US SIM cards to reach US audiences, trained them on account warm-up and post spacing. (secondhand)
- Focus Tree (Laurent, Consumer Club member): faceless creators in the "StudyTok" niche flash the app screen for a couple seconds inside aesthetic study-setup videos; conversion happens in the comments — viewers ask "what app is that," creator replies with the name. (secondhand)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Format progression tweet (e-com leads, apps follow): 2021 influencers → 2022 TikTok mass UGC → 2023 slides/carousels → 2024 HeyGen AI avatars with slides behind → 2025 "flux avatars." All still work; each saturates as marketers copy it. (claimed)
- Slideshows/carousels: extremely cheap to make ("a paragraph or two of text" + filler photos, ~30 min of work per host). Example shower-filter account: one post ~200,000 likes; account posts daily, baseline ~5–10k views/video, occasional 1–2M-view hits. (secondhand, from screen-share)
- Soft CTAs beat hard ones: Cal AI never mentions the app verbally — just shows the screen + name for a second or two; viewers close TikTok and search the App Store by name. Link-in-bio conversion is "just not very good" anymore. (claimed)
- Comment-section conversion stack: convert in comments; then make a video reply to a "what app is this" comment — TikTok resurfaces the reply to deep-engagers of the original video, functioning like a free retargeting campaign; the reply is where you hard-sell. (claimed)
- 7-second looping text video: B-roll + song + long on-screen text forces multiple loops to read it → ~5x watch-time/retention in analytics. Text must be genuinely interesting; CTA can be as soft as "maybe this account can help." (claimed, example shown)
- Views without commercial intent are worthless — 1–2M views with no hypothesis/CTA won't produce downloads. Pick content formats with a conversion hypothesis (e.g., phone-in-hand content converts for apps). (claimed)
- Choi's tool (self-plugged): TikTok search that surfaces videos outperforming their account's median engagement (algorithm-driven, not follower-driven), used to find proven formats to modify. (first-party product pitch)
- Inversion thesis: when a format saturates, the anti-format wins — Sam Sulek's 40–50-minute slow, unedited videos vs. Mr. Beast's meta; faces may become less trusted than voices as AI avatars spread; "faceless storytelling" = B-roll + good AI voice (e.g., Erewhon-smoothie video selling an adaptogenic mushroom supplement). (speculation/claimed)
- AI-generated UGC: HeyGen avatars + ElevenLabs narration over carousel slides; "the average person can't tell anymore" — comments treat the AI actor as a real person. Ethical concerns acknowledged and waved past. (claimed, examples shown)
- Choi first-party test: brand-new TikTok account, first video 50,000 views — 2–3-minute unedited monotone ramble with a decent hook; took ~1 hour because he re-recorded clips ~20 times. Proof that fundamentals + hook beat production. (first-party)
- Starter checklist: pick the app you've been thinking about; research what content works in your niche; pick ~3 formats from already-working TikToks; copy/iterate; the bar is cracking 5,000 views — if you can, keep going. (claimed)

### Launch & sequencing

- "Start with the channel / start with distribution": reverse-engineer the viral TikTok moment first, then build it as a feature (Cal AI's scan is the viral moment; the business/retention is boring calorie tracking à la MyFitnessPal). Building product first risks discovering "no content will ever resonate." (claimed)
- Niching down a proven winner: a verticalized Cal AI (e.g., vegans-only) at a tenth of Cal AI's claimed $2M/month is still $200k/month. New AI model releases each spawn "a hundred new wrappers" attacking slightly different audiences. (speculation with claimed anchor numbers)
- Tollan (Consumer Club member): AI companion app, launched a couple weeks prior, hit #1–#2 in the App Store; Choi's read is anyone could reskin the same voice models for other audiences. (secondhand)
- NGL high-school seeding: private per-school accounts (school name + app name, school logo) mass-followed students; tribal trust → follow-backs → first post lands on a pre-seeded audience. NGL-adjacent founders described as making "millions a year just cash flowing." (secondhand)
- Web-to-app funnels called out as a whole hidden money channel (web onboarding + Stripe payment before store download); host plugs Superwall's in-development web-to-app product. (claimed; host has direct commercial interest)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Agencies for a new underpriced channel: approach with caution; the best operators are founders (obsessed + financially incentivized), not retainer marketers; good agencies have a lifespan of "no more than 6 months" before quality collapses from over-booking or they quit to build their own apps. Founder must learn the channel personally, then hire to execute the figured-out playbook. (claimed)
- Affiliates (10% rev-share on gambling offers) framed as the best consumer marketers because they compete on identical commodity offers; tactics flow affiliate → e-com → consumer tech as products commoditize. (claimed)
- Audience-first entrants: poker influencer (Slick Rick Poker, viral on Reels) joined seeking a technical co-founder; Alex Lieberman (Morning Brew) starting an app with a Consumer Club co-founder, Choi advising. "Apps as a revenue channel for people with audiences" — happens "several times a week." (secondhand)
- Oasis (Cormac): started ranking water quality by brand, expanded to air quality, protein bars next; all content is founder green-screen talking-head over the app; believed to be a one-person business. (secondhand)

### Retention & product

- The viral feature and the retention engine are different things: Cal AI's scan gets the download; daily calorie/macro tracking is the actual business. (claimed)
- Distribution is the moat: Blake Anderson predicts Cal AI will be the most-copied app on the App Store and won't lose because the edge is distribution, not product; also a talent-magnet effect — the best growth hackers want to work for visible winners. (secondhand)

### Other

- Media is effectively required for consumer: "everything is media" — you pay for content via ads, creators, or your own time; owning media is preferred over perpetually renting audiences, though ads matter for scale phases. (claimed)
- Build-in-public: fine for differentiated products or for network benefits; unwise for commoditized products marketed on TikTok while narrating strategy on Twitter. (claimed)
- Anyone smart can do this now — AI coding (Blake Anderson famously built with ChatGPT) removed most of the technical moat; experienced people "only have 1 year on you." (claimed)

## Deal structures

No concrete creator contracts disclosed. Reference prices only: influencers $1,000–$2,000/video (host: $2–3k + retainer); gambling affiliates 10% of driven revenue; the Philippines UGC operation implies employed creators supplied with hardware (iPhones + US SIM cards) rather than per-video deals, but no wages given.

## Contrarian positions

- Start with distribution/channel, then build the product around the viral moment — inverts product-first orthodoxy.
- Don't hire agencies or "organic experts" for a new channel; the founder must master it personally first (agency half-life ~6 months).
- Being copied is fine, even good — distribution is the moat, not the product; commoditization is "just the game."
- Soft/invisible CTAs (flash the screen, convert in comments) beat explicit CTAs and link-in-bio.
- Low-production authenticity can beat retention-edited content (Sam Sulek inversion; his own 50k-view ramble).
- Post-cap workaround: the 3-posts/day shadowban ceiling is per-account, so run 5 accounts per phone.

## Crave transfer

The most transferable idea is "reverse-engineer the viral moment before launch": Crave's map of ranked dishes per city is inherently screenshot-able, and Austin food-TikTok ("best X in Austin" listicles, slideshows) is a proven, dense niche — the slideshow/carousel format and comment-section conversion ("what app is that map?") map directly onto a local discovery app with no ad budget. The Focus Tree flash-the-screen mechanic fits better than Cal AI's, because Crave is repeat-use utility, not one-scan wow. Be skeptical of every revenue number here: Cal AI's $2M/mo, the $400k-MRR study app, and Tabs' $10M are unaudited survivor headlines from a guy selling community access on a podcast run by a paywall vendor, and the niching math ("a tenth is still $200k/mo") assumes global-market TAMs — a single-city launch has a hard local ceiling that none of these examples faced. The 15-videos/day Philippines operation and AI-avatar spam are volume plays tuned to commodity AI utilities; for a trust-dependent local recommendation product, the "inversion" point (authentic, low-production local voice) is likely the better-fitting half of the advice. The hard-paywall-with-card question is never addressed — nothing in this transcript validates or contradicts Crave's monetization design.
