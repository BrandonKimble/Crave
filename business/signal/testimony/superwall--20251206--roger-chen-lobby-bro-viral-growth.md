## Arc

Roger Chen built two consumer social apps that each hit #1 on the App Store: Lobby (post-COVID group video hangout, 500k DAU, product-led viral growth with no paid marketing at scale) and Bro (AI companion floating over your existing apps, #1 in the US off TikTok). The episode is his playbook: prototype with fake mockups before writing production code, use paid TikTok ads purely as validation, then scale with UGC creators and product-led invite mechanics. He's now folding Bro's learnings into a next "real social app."

## Claims

### Content strategy (organic)

- Bro's TikTok format = "classic hook and demo": hook is a scenario with a real human on camera ("people love people... they see an app interface, they scroll"). (claimed)
- Output is extremely top-heavy: they mass-produced videos; "a few videos got like two, three million views"; "the ones I thought would work just didn't work, and the ones I thought were ridiculous worked surprisingly well." (claimed)
- Best brand-account video: 7.9 million views, made by co-founder Leo, filmed on a ProtoPie mockup ("Protoy"/"ProtoI" in captions — uncertain, almost certainly ProtoPie) before the app was even finished. (claimed)
- Best creator video: 2.3 million views — "autocorrect sent I love you to my crush" scenario; the demo is disguised as just showing the iMessage conversation. (claimed; video shown on screen)
- Made ~5-6 more variations of the hero creative, later discarded because they showed an outdated design. (claimed)
- An earlier lock-screen-focused format (morning affirmations from Bro) also performed well — it was NOT invalidated by ads; they dropped it only because segments had outdated designs. The lock-screen tests are where they "got the hunch that this proactive caring thing could really work." (claimed)
- Content is unpredictable vs product logic: "with content... there are not rules... you just have to try everything." (claimed)

### Paid ads

- Framework: ads for VALIDATION, not scaling — "when you're actually scaling, no ads... the more you scale, the more expensive it becomes"; "in the beginning, ads are really the fastest way to get feedback." (claimed/philosophy)
- Rationale: idea-to-launch dead zone is "2 weeks, a month, even 2-3 months... the most dreadful period because you're just in your head"; "a few thousand dollars of ads is nothing if you can get feedback instantly." (claimed)
- The validation ads bought two things: (1) at least one working format, (2) proof people want the product. (claimed)
- UGC breakouts took "two, three months" to materialize — too slow for validation, hence ads first. (claimed)

### UGC & creator deals

- Creator sourcing: searched TikTok hashtags (e.g., #relationship), found lookalike creators, cold-emailed them to start posting. (first-party process description)
- Peak roster: ~50 creators; many stopped posting, decaying to ~10-20 active. Creators also outside the US ("in Norway or something"). (claimed)
- Managing creator campaigns was far harder than expected — "not the outreach part, but the getting-the-content-to-work part... so much work"; he brought in the host's people to manage campaigns. (claimed; note the incentive flag)
- No payment terms, rev-share, or per-view rates disclosed.

### Launch & sequencing

- Bro sequence: ProtoPie mockup → daily personal use of the fake mockup ("when I'm on the toilet I just play with it") → paid TikTok ads on mockup footage → finish app → family-and-friends testing → creator scaling → #1 US App Store. (first-party account)
- Mockup rationale: "such a huge gap between the Figma and something that you can tap... a lot of things look good in Figma but it just does not work once you get your hands on it" — cuts unnecessary engineering. (claimed)
- Vision must be narrowed to a "concrete wedge": Bro's ambitious always-there companion vision was funneled into one scenario — texting first, then dating apps, then misc. (claimed)
- Anecdote: a defunct phone-brand founder carried a brick shaped like the next phone as a form-factor prototype. (secondhand anecdote)

### Retention & product (Lobby's product-led growth)

- Lobby stalled ~1 year: power users existed but "retention was not that good... we did not really grow for like a year." (claimed)
- Breakout: went organically viral in Israel (~7 million people, "nobody's going to market in Israel"), hit #1 and stayed. (claimed)
- Israel day-90 retention: 25-30%; other markets roughly HALF that. (claimed number)
- The one key difference = network density: % of users reaching 5 friends on day 1 was ~20% in other markets vs ~60% in Israel (timeframe: start of 2022). (claimed numbers — the load-bearing stat of the episode)
- Conclusion: density is "not something solvable through content marketing" (that just adds "random people without friends") — must come from product growth features driving friend invites. (claimed/philosophy)
- Group-picture feature: during a video hangout, a button (lighting up every ~5-10 minutes as a hint) triggers a 3-2-1 countdown + vibration + boomerang group photo, watermarked, shared to Snapchat/Instagram stories; absent friends see it and think "how did this happen without me." Also prompted on chat exit. (first-party)
- Result: 3 months after the density push, #1 in the UK, then Italy, then Germany; UK users spread it to international-school students in Cairo (local quasi-influencers) → #1 in Egypt, then Saudi Arabia. (claimed)
- One-minute call feature = a growth/"roll call" feature, not the core: at a random minute at night, a bar pops up — talk to friends for exactly one minute; deliberately low pressure ("I don't want to be held hostage"); the app, not the user, is the initiator ("it's not me, it's a game"). (first-party)
- Two densities required for real-time products: relationship density AND temporal density ("if I miss you by one second the app is useless"). Explicitly inspired by BeReal's synchronized daily post; BeReal worked because "you get a whole lot of pictures back at the same time" — trickling in wouldn't have taken off. (claimed/analysis)
- Shared-experience law for viral media: a group photo everyone anticipates ≠ a screenshot ("that's just creepy... sniping you"). (philosophy)
- Seeding still required: "you do still need to seed the users for them to invite their friends, but after that, to scale, it really comes down to the product." (claimed)

### Other

- Companion thesis: AI companions shouldn't "live in a black box" or displace relationships — they should augment them, living inside apps you already use; framed via the DoorDash analogy (not as good as a restaurant, but the point is accessibility). (philosophy)
- Next act: use AI to create CONTEXT, not content — an AI "yappy friend" character who carries group conversation; social in the core flow. (stated plan)
- Founder-market fit: their 2023 attempt Super Movie (AI-generated interactive story app, "think Episode but AI-generated") fizzled because Roger lacked passion for it; contrast with the AI Dungeon founder's world-building obsession. (claimed)
- Host mid-roll: Consumer Club members/interviewees build apps at "a median of about a million dollars ARR"; Superwall's free tool at paywallexperiments.com is built on 422 profitable paywall experiments. (host promotional claims)

## Contrarian positions

- Invert the standard funnel: ads for early validation, organic/product-led for scale — most advice says organic to validate cheaply, then ads to scale. He argues scaling on ads only gets more expensive.
- Content marketing CANNOT fix a density/retention problem — it actively worsens it by importing friendless users. Growth must be engineered into the product.
- Prototype-and-advertise before the app exists (fake ProtoPie mockup footage as ad creative) — most builders ship an MVP first.
- Don't optimize prototypes for what looks cool on TikTok; optimize for "I should feel good using it."
- Nobody can predict which creative pops — "truly humbled"; volume + variation beats taste.
