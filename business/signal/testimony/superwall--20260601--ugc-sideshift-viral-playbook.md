## Arc

Joseph Choi (Superwall Podcast) interviews Drew Levin, co-founder of SideShift, a UGC creator marketplace that "mints its own supply" via a 6-hour training course. Drew lays out the operational playbook behind the biggest UGC-driven app campaigns on his platform — creator sourcing, manager ratios, payment structures, engagement-rate thresholds, and Spark/partnership-ad amplification. The video exists to position SideShift as the default UGC infrastructure for app founders; the host monetizes the same audience via Superwall, Consumer Club, and sponsor reads.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Building virality into the product itself is the top-performing strategy: "people who build apps where you can earn money or get something in return do really, really well with organic" (Snag, Settlemate class-action apps). (claimed)
- Rage-bait works for AI products: lean into people hating AI. Suno's TikTok comments are full of "I hate this AI music" — controversy = engagement. "You want to get under people's skin a little bit, but also show value. That's like the easiest way to go viral." (claimed/secondhand)
- TikTok algorithm optimizes for two things: engagement rate and watch time. Engagement rate = (likes + comments + saves + shares) / views. (claimed)
- Engagement-rate thresholds: above 7.5–8% is good; above 12% "it'll start to go very very viral generally"; the SideShift job-market example video hit 16% engagement and 9.6M views. (first-party for the SideShift video)
- Manager-to-creator ratio: 1:10 is "the winning ratio," up to 15 max; below 5 creators per manager wastes money. (claimed)
- Suno reportedly has ~8 creator managers (Drew's assumption — "I would assume"); no dedicated coaches, managers double as coaches. (speculation/secondhand)
- Two roles: campaign managers (logistics — posting on time, CTA quality, recruiting new creators, firing bad ones) vs content coaches (craft — "your tone's too slow… look at the camera at the 3-minute mark"). (claimed)
- Top 50 programs on SideShift: founders are barely involved — payouts + monthly check-ins only; but all were heavily founder-involved at the start. Once running it's "a cash-flowing side income… once it's kicking you $5,000 of cash, it just does it forever." (claimed)
- 6 months ago the best UGC programs were run by founders under age 25. (claimed)
- SideShift scale: 5,000 new brands create accounts per month; 850,000 creators total, ~150,000 monthly active; ~100 vetted campaign managers available for hire (all interviewed, all have gone viral). (first-party claimed)
- Creators after 1–2 months on SideShift earn $10k–15k/month ("10, 12, 15,000 dollars"). Lifecycle: creator → campaign manager → own UGC agency on the platform. (claimed)
- SideShift fronts unpaid creator payouts when brands stiff them — "thousands and thousands… almost six figures a month" — to build creator loyalty. (first-party claimed)
- SideShift spent more money/time on its 6-hour Duolingo-style creator training course than on building the product itself. (first-party claimed)
- Emerging format: one top creator posts 10-minute story videos daily on ONE multi-brand account (grew it to 10k followers on one brand, then reused it for others); average watch time 16% of the video; CTA dropped at minute 9. (anecdote)
- Flagship campaign shown on dashboard: 10,000+ posts, 500M views, 14M engagements → #1 on App Store. (first-party dashboard, app unnamed)
- Format evolution example: co-founder Nick's "I have a master's degree and couldn't find a job" video (posted April 19, [year garbled — said "2026" then "a full year away"; likely April 2025], not talking-head, text at bottom); once cracked, given to creators: 6–7 of ~15 subsequent videos got 150k–300k+ views each; the talking-head evolution a year later got 9.6M views. (first-party)
- First-30-days playbook: lock a consistent creator source; hire ~10 creators through one identical A-to-Z process (5-min call → Notion onboarding); weekly phone reviews; first 3 days review videos BEFORE posting; copy videos already going viral in your niche. (claimed)
- Creators are hard to manage, "not always the smartest tools in the shed" (though "high IQ creators" now entering); friction kills programs; expect high churn at low ticket prices — fire bad creators fast to keep CPMs down. (claimed)
- Nickel-and-diming creators backfires: capping a creator to save $50 "might cost you thousands of dollars down the line." (claimed)
- Cautionary anecdote: a creator got ~180M views in 2 weeks on one Instagram account (incl. a 45M-view video) but had a monthly CPM-payout cap of ~$4k; uncapped he'd have made ~$75k that month; he made ~$4k, brand lost the creator. (anecdote)
- Performance-only deals (pay % of ad spend or % of sales, nothing upfront): all risk on the creator — one creator made 35 unique videos for an e-com brand before earning a dollar. "If you are a brand and can find creators to do this, definitely do it. And if you're a creator, I would never do this." (anecdote + claimed)
- Exceptional "goat" creators (sub-$2 CPM on every campaign) are worth a $10,000/month retainer from month one. Host adds anecdote: a study tool paid one TikTok creator (~100k followers) who made ONE video on a fresh account → ~20k MRR from that single video (~3 years ago). (claimed/anecdote)

### Paid ads (Spark / partnership ads)

- Core current strategy: make organic UGC, then amplify winners as Spark ads (TikTok) and partnership ads (Meta). "Spark ads are incredibly cracked. They're like a cheat code." (claimed)
- Spark trigger rules: test Spark on any video above 10% engagement rate (elsewhere he says 7.5–8%); start with $20 test budget, or $50–$100/day per video; run for a week at a time; kill the Spark when engagement drops below 5%. (claimed)
- High-engagement videos see "way below dollar CPMs on Spark." CPMs won't start rising until the video's engagement rate decays to ~5%. Keep running while CPMs stay flat. (claimed)
- Don't Spark prematurely: let organic climb as long as possible; if a video is at 1M views still gaining 100k/day, wait a day or two before Sparking. (claimed)
- Spark priority ranking (SideShift's recommendation engine): comment sentiment first ("what is that app?" comments = high conversion signal), then engagement rate, then average views. (first-party product claim)
- GoWish went to #1 on the App Store "almost entirely from Spark" (campaign run by Seen Marketing agency on SideShift) — "the cheapest way to gain users." (secondhand)
- Sparking a video that's doing 3–5x the account's average views also lifts the account's future ORGANIC performance — helps escape shadow-ban, signals legitimacy. (claimed)
- ROAS correlation: if you make $10 per $5 spent on Spark, organic ROAS is typically 15–20% better than that. (claimed)
- Spark accounting trap: don't pay creator view-bonuses on Sparked (paid) views — e.g., 800k organic + 200k sparked, creator expects the extra ~$1,000 view bonus. SideShift separates sparked vs organic views; if you lack a system, beware. (claimed)
- Really good creators may demand a % of Spark ad spend; generally you bake Spark rights into the contract. SideShift has creators OAuth accounts so Spark codes are delivered directly to the brand. (claimed/first-party)
- Partnership ads (Meta's Spark equivalent, via creator code): convert BETTER than Spark generally, even on small UGC accounts with a few thousand followers; usually disclosed as paid (Spark often isn't); Instagram profiles can carry links. (claimed)
- Platform tactics differ: on TikTok optimize for reach; on Meta target audiences early (Meta weights follower-audience similarity; TikTok pushes to viewers similar to watchers). (claimed)
- Spark/partnership ads outperform whitelisted ads on your own branded account because they look native and come from a non-brand account. (claimed)
- UGC is "the fastest testing engine for positioning" — paid-creative testing with organic upside on top. (claimed)
- Organic-sourced users are higher quality than paid: an organic install requires stop-scroll → App Store search → download → buy → return to comment. (claimed)
- B2B UGC "on the come up": Brax [uncertain; likely Brex] used SideShift with "really cheap CPMs" acquiring credit-card customers; Spark retargeting (country, language, age) makes B2B viable. (secondhand)

### Team, tools & cost structure (CPM economics)

- CPM trajectory for a new program: expect $5–8 blended CPM in month one; sub-$3 by month three; then "pretty much go sub one as long as your product is good and has viral potential." (claimed)
- "All the top brands running UGC have a sub 90 cent CPM" — typically 80–90 cents; the biggest spenders on SideShift each spend $100,000+/month and all drive sub-$1 CPMs. (claimed)
- Early CPM is high not because you set it higher, but because retainer-paid creators underperform until you find "three solid rocks"; takes a month or two. (claimed)
- Never outprice the market — don't offer higher rates than needed to get in the door. (claimed)
- "There's no such thing as instant marketing" — comparison: an SEO agency pitched SEO as an 18-month investment. (claimed)
- The title's "reduce UGC costs by up to 80%" framing (host intro) maps to the best-platform-only payment + free-views mechanics below. (host framing)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Copy proven-viral formats in your niche and hand them to creators — fastest path to early wins. (claimed)
- Tie content to what people are angry/emotional about right now (job market + Trump-era anxiety for SideShift; anti-AI anger for Suno; anti-food-industry for scanning/peptide/GLP-1 apps). "You want to make people feel something." (claimed)
- GLP-1 companion apps are now the most common sign-up category on SideShift; food/supplement scanning apps ("thousands of them," e.g. one transcribed as "Olive" [uncertain]) all doing well riding the health obsession of younger generations. (claimed)
- Format guidelines are minimal and prescriptive: e.g., mandatory big text "I have a master's degree" (or PhD), hook + demo, weird facial expressions; later evolved to "I graduated from Yale and I can't even get an interview." (first-party)
- Formats decay; the durable edge is a system that keeps finding new formats — 2–3 "golden" backbone creators + a pipeline of new ones. (claimed)
- Founder relationship-building with top creators (texting, sending stuff) drives loyalty in a low-loyalty market; a million-view video "will generate so much revenue" that buy-in matters. (claimed)

## Deal structures

- Current best-program structure: **$800/month retainer for 60 videos** (2 unique videos/day, each cross-posted to 4 platforms — TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Snapchat) **plus non-stacked view bonuses** (hitting 1M replaces the 100k bonus — pay $1,000, not $1,100). (claimed)
- New-creator (0–2 prior campaigns) starter deal: CPM-only, or **$200–300/month for that same 60-video package**. (claimed)
- 4 months ago's meta: **$400 base + $2 CPM** — "the biggest alpha ever," now superseded. (claimed)
- Cross-post best-of-four payment (what "Clueless" [likely Cluely, uncertain] and others run, built into SideShift): post to 4 platforms, **pay view-bonus only on the single best-performing platform**. (claimed/first-party)
- "Free views" optimization: after a video passes ~1M views (or the view window ends), all further views are unpaid; the biggest programs purely optimize for free views — at $30–50k+/month spend "you feel those free views… huge money." Brands also time-cap rather than view-cap bonuses so creators feel like winners. (claimed)
- Goat-creator exception: $10,000/month retainer justified for proven sub-$2-CPM creators. (claimed)
- Host's baseline sketch (Drew doesn't correct): ~$10–30 per video retainer + tiered view bonuses (100k, 500k levels), ~$2/1,000-view bonuses. (host framing)
- Performance-only (% of ad spend or % of sales, $0 upfront): great for brands, terrible for creators; hard to retain talent on it. (claimed)
- Old-world comparison: 1–2 years ago creators would charge ~$2,000 per video off PR-campaign experience. (claimed)

## Contrarian positions

- Founders should NOT stay involved in UGC day-to-day once CPMs stabilize sub-$4 — top-50 program founders only do payouts and monthly check-ins (against the "founder-led content forever" school).
- Lean INTO hatred of your product category (AI backlash as fuel), deliberately provoking negative comments — most brand advice says avoid controversy.
- Pay creators generously and never cap upside — the $50 you save costs thousands (against squeeze-the-vendor procurement instinct).
- Spark/partnership ads on tiny no-name UGC accounts beat whitelisted ads on your official brand account.
- Sparking a video improves the account's subsequent organic reach (paid spend as an organic-algorithm unlock) — not commonly claimed elsewhere.
- Don't Spark a still-climbing organic video — wait; most paid-ads instinct is to amplify immediately.
- Comment sentiment outranks engagement rate outranks views as the Spark-selection signal.
- UGC-marketplace endorsement itself is contrarian to the host's own year-old viral doc that said marketplaces suck (he now exempts SideShift — while interviewing its founder).

## Crave transfer

Almost every number here is secondhand or self-interested — the guest sells the marketplace, the managers, and the Spark tooling he prescribes, and the flagship 500M-view case study is an unnamed app with no revenue attached. The transferable core for Crave is the process, not the outcomes: copy already-viral formats in the local-food niche, run a small identical-onboarding creator batch, review videos before posting, use engagement rate (>7.5–8%, kill <5%) as the amplify/kill signal, and treat month-one $5–8 CPMs as normal rather than failure. The emotion-hook principle fits Crave well (rage-bait against pay-to-play "best of Austin" listicles, Yelp/influencer astroturf — "you want to make people feel something"), and cross-post-pay-best-platform deal structure is directly usable at Crave's budget. What does NOT transfer: the sub-$1 CPM benchmarks come from $100k+/month national-audience programs, while Crave's payer must be in Austin — national viral views are mostly worthless installs against a hard $7.99 paywall, so Crave's effective cost per _useful_ view will be far higher and Spark's geo-targeting (country/age, not city) only partially fixes this. Also note the category mismatch: every cited winner is a broad-audience earn-money/AI-novelty/health app with single-session wow; none is a local repeat-use utility, so expect the "week-one viral" survivorship stories to be irrelevant and the 18-month-patience framing to be the honest part.
