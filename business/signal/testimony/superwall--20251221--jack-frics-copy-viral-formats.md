## Arc

Jack (surname uncertain in captions) is a solo founder who spent ~3 years failing at print-on-demand, Amazon KDP, crypto YouTube, and newsletters before learning to code ~2.5 years ago. His first app, Curiosity Quench, took 8 months to make meaningful money, then hit $15k in one month off a copied TikTok slideshow format posted ~400 times. He then built PostBridge (B2B scheduler, turned down a $1M offer from Sahil Lavingia of Gumroad), Doof ($1k in 30 days), and Lovely (couples app, 100k downloads in 10 weeks). The episode exists to showcase the "copy proven viral formats, don't invent" organic playbook — on a podcast run by Superwall to market paywall tooling.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Original Curiosity Quench monetization was a confusing "quench credits" shop with a "premium access" store page — users didn't understand it; app made $500–$1,000/mo total over its first ~5 months. (claimed)
- Adding a real paywall was the "light bulb moment": first via RevenueCat ("revenue cap" in captions — couldn't customize, "very clunky"), then migrated to Superwall. Revenue went from ~$1,000/mo to $2,000–$4,000/mo. (claimed; incentive-flagged — told on Superwall's podcast)
- 3–4 months of A/B testing headers, copy, and feature gating on Superwall made "the average user way more likely to actually buy." No specific lift numbers given. (claimed)
- Both of his main paywalls were stock Superwall TEMPLATES — logo + color swap only. "Templates are really good. So I mostly just use those." (claimed)
- Says paywall setup on a new app now takes ~5 minutes and "increased the chance that someone's going to buy cuz it looks legit." (claimed)
- Lovely launched with a HARD paywall, then he switched to freemium because of bad reviews about the hard paywall and because he wanted friends to use it free. (first-party decision, claimed outcome)
- Admits some of his apps "could make more money if I had hard pay walls" but he optimizes for fun. (first-party stance)
- Because Lovely is freemium ("premium" in captions), he says paid ads "don't really make sense" — but immediately hedges: "I've never really used paid ads... maybe I'm silly. Don't take that to heart." (speculation, self-flagged)
- Couples-app buyer dynamic: the boyfriend pays if the girlfriend asks — "you don't love me, you didn't pay for this couple's app." Two-person use also drives word of mouth. (anecdote)

### Trial & onboarding

- "What really helped was the free trial, like having an option" — trial + paywall standardization credited for the $1k→$2–4k/mo jump on Curiosity Quench. (claimed, no isolation of trial vs. other changes)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Core method: scroll TikTok in your niche every morning; ~1 hour/day total on marketing (scroll + make one video); tried a different video daily for ~100–200 days before finding a winner. (first-party process)
- The winning Curiosity Quench format: a 4-picture "most attractive hobbies" slideshow copied from a ~1M-view Instagram post; he swapped in his app's widget pictures and caption. Made that video ~400 times. Result: 5 million views, thousands of downloads, the $15k month (Sept 2024; October 2024 second-highest). (claimed)
- CTA placement: download link in a pinned comment, not the video; angry comments ("thousands" arguing about the hobby lists) boosted visibility of the pinned comment. (first-party tactic)
- Every ~20 videos he'd put the actual app screenshot in one slide; those got millions of views and thousands of downloads. One cited: 1.8M views, 47k likes with the App Store screenshot included. (claimed)
- Explicit tradeoff he names: 10,000 views/1,000 downloads vs 3M views/100 downloads — view-count and conversion trade off; the slideshow format was "very low converting" overall. (first-party observation)
- He cannot attribute downloads to specific videos: multiple accounts, same format, "every week a few popped to 1–2M views... impossible to track." (first-party admission — key epistemic caveat on ALL his conversion claims)
- Baseline before the viral format: 50–60 downloads/day over 8 months; the format spiked days to 1,000 downloads. The format decayed ~3 months later; "golden geese don't last forever" / "milked dry at this point." (claimed)
- Doof: reused the same slideshow format; one video hit 5M views and brought "quite a bit" of downloads; got the app to $1,000 revenue in the first 30 days (a public 30-day challenge). (claimed)
- Doof caption tactic: content is a meme ("people who eat like this feel good, sleep good, and poop good"), app mentioned only casually in the caption ("feel good today by downloading doof"); "a few %" read the caption. (first-party tactic)
- Organic-only works because "people don't want to see ads" — every video is engineered to not look like an ad; he says this format approach "only really works with consumer apps." (first-party stance)
- Lovely's gold format: 8–10 second video of him directly using the app (shared-whiteboard feature styled like a lock screen), app shown at the end — converted "like 20 times better than the 2x slideshows." (claimed, no absolute numbers)
- He found that format by watching a competitor couples app go viral with it on TikTok, rebuilt the feature in his own app IN ONE DAY (presentation mode on an existing shared whiteboard), posted the next day. Fifth video hit 490k views; scaled from 1/day to 2/day to 3/day; several videos hit 500k–1M+; first Instagram cross-post hit 300k views, several more hit ~1M — all within 2 weeks. (claimed)
- Earlier Lovely TikTok attempt: second-ever post hit 150k views but brought only ~100–200 downloads — "silver or copper, not gold." Posted via a US VPN on an old burner phone (he appears non-US based). (claimed)
- Format-hunting ritual: 15-minute timer scrolling + save/bookmark; 30–60 min to make the day's video; "finds a gem every session" but often 8 months stale. TikTok and Instagram "usually don't correlate" — same video rarely works on both. (first-party process)
- Explicit philosophy: don't engineer or analyze WHY a format works — "I just needed to get the video out... trust the algorithm." Copying beats creating; "it takes way more effort to make something new than something that you know works." (first-party stance)

### UGC & creator deals

- Reinvestment plan for Lovely (first time ever reinvesting): hire people to post the winning format across 20–40 accounts instead of 1–2, targeting "hundreds of videos a day." Paid ~$1,000 so far; accounts being set up; explicitly "still in the testing phase," worried algorithms will detect it; thinks Instagram is the best bet for mass-account posting. Found the posters via Twitter word of mouth. (first-party, in-progress — NOT a validated result)

### Launch & sequencing

- Lovely launch = a public build-in-public Twitter arc: launch tweet ("$0 to $5,000/mo in 10 weeks"), quote-tweeted ~6 times with updates; each QT gained views because it read as a story. (first-party tactic)
- A user repurposed the love-note widget as a grocery list; that tweet got 16M views / 33k likes; his follow-up context tweet got ~300k likes; combined ≈20M views → 40,000 downloads overnight. (claimed)
- Russian news picked up the launch tweet ~a week after launch → 10,000 downloads from Russia the next day. (claimed)
- Twitter + Russia wave alone added ~$2,000–$3,000 MRR before any TikTok posting began (TikTok started ~1 week after the tweets went viral). (claimed)
- Lovely totals at recording: ~100,000 downloads in ~10 weeks; dashboard read as 102,000 users, 10,000 "proceeds" (garbled — plausibly trials/subscribers), ~$4,200 MRR; on pace for the $5k MRR end-of-year goal. (closest to first-party — read off screen)
- PostBridge acquisition story: April (of "last year"), Sahil from Gumroad publicly offered $1M on Twitter; PostBridge was making ~$12k/mo, at all-time high, growing 30–40% month-over-month; he declined because he was enjoying it; growth later slowed; now ~$20k MRR per host. (claimed)
- Career sequence: 3 years of failed ventures (print-on-demand, Amazon KDP, t-shirts, crypto YouTube, newsletters) → learned to code ~2.5 years ago → Curiosity Quench (desktop first, then mobile) → PostBridge → Doof (30-day challenge) → Lovely. (first-party)

### Retention & product

- "Downloads don't equal money" — the 8-month lesson of app #1. (first-party)
- Ideation pattern he self-identifies: personal problem + "everything I've made has a successful counterpart" — a revamp of something already working, never a de-novo invention; marketing angles also copied from incumbents in the niche. (first-party stance)
- Lovely virality was product-led: the widget output itself (love notes / the grocery-list misuse) IS the shareable artifact; "the app itself is what's going viral." (host + guest observation)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Solo everything until now; "profitability is the number one metric... I want to stay solo, stay very small." (first-party)
- PostBridge exists because manual cross-posting took ~1 hr/day; batching a week or two of scheduled posts cut it to ~10 min/day. (first-party)
- Host frame: Consumer Club founders build apps at a median of ~$1M ARR; Superwall's paywallexperiments.com tool is built from 422 profitable paywall experiments. (host marketing claims)

## Deal structures

None discussed in concrete terms. The only adjacent item: ~$1,000 paid so far to people (found via Twitter) to set up 20–40 posting accounts for volume-posting Lovely's winning format — no rev-share, per-view, or bonus terms disclosed, and no results yet.

## Contrarian positions

- Copying viral formats verbatim beats original creative; deliberately does NOT analyze why a format works before shipping it.
- Turned down a $1M acquisition on a ~$12k/mo SaaS (≈7x ARR) because he was having fun — explicitly acknowledges it may have been irrational.
- Softened a hard paywall to freemium in response to bad reviews (against the pay-up-front consensus this podcast's audience often holds), and openly leaves money on the table for enjoyment.
- Massive raw view counts are worth chasing even at terrible conversion, as long as any downloads flow — vs. the common "views are vanity" advice.
- Has never run paid ads across four revenue-generating products.

## Crave transfer

The strongest transferable claim is the format taxonomy: slideshow/meme formats = millions of views at near-zero conversion; an 8–10s "me directly using the app" demo = ~20x better conversion — for Crave that maps to short screen-capture videos of the map/dish-ranking answering a real "where should I eat in Austin" moment, and the 15-min/day scroll-your-niche ritual (Austin foodtok) is cheap and directly actionable. His paywall arc superficially validates Crave's Superwall-style trial+paywall setup, but note the incentive distortion (Superwall's own podcast) and that his one hard-paywall experiment (Lovely) was ABANDONED for freemium under review pressure — that's a mild counter-signal to Crave's hard-gate model, though Lovely is a $0-utility social toy while Crave sells repeat-use utility, so the pressure differs. His virality was product-led (widget output is inherently shareable); Crave's map/scores are not self-broadcasting, so the 40k-downloads-overnight Twitter events and Russia spike are survivorship lottery tickets, not playbook. His conversion numbers are self-admittedly untrackable ("I have no idea"), all categories are global-audience lifestyle apps vs. Crave's single-city launch, and the 20–40-account volume-posting scheme is unproven ($1k in, zero results reported) — treat it as an experiment to watch, not adopt.
