## Arc

Dmitri, a data-science manager at Opendoor, built YourMove AI in 2022 as a drunken joke — a text box that turned a Hinge message into three flirty replies on GPT-3/Da Vinci-2, pre-ChatGPT. Being ~a year early to AI dating got him mainstream press (WSJ, Fox, CNBC, Wired, Washington Post, a Newsweek op-ed) that drove almost no users but seeded strong domain authority, which he converted into an SEO machine driving ~half of traffic to ~$30k/mo. He funded 10–12 Upwork contractors from his day-job salary, ran the business unprofitable on purpose for his final employed year, quit at ~$17–18k/mo (target $20k), caught an Opendoor layoff severance on the way out, and sold the company ~4–5 months before this recording. He has since "defected from bootstrapping" into an a16z-speedrun venture.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Core product runs a SOFT paywall to this day; only the AI-photos product is HARD-paywalled, and only because "the compute cost on that is quite high." (claimed)
- Took ~6 months after building to monetize at all — he was on the old "get users first, monetize later" model, which he now says "everybody now knows kind of doesn't make sense." A mentor had to push him to monetize. (claimed)
- Revenue milestones: ~6 months to first monetization; ~1.5 years total to first $1,000 (transcript reads "$,000" twice — first-thousand reading is from the intro narration "another year to hit the first thousand dollars"); then ~1 year to ~$10k/mo; then ~1 year to ~$28k/mo; peak $30k/mo at ~80% margin. Total arc ~2.5 years, employed full-time nearly the whole way. (claimed)
- Ad-auction logic: on Meta/Google you compete against anyone monetizing the same demographic, so ads that win auctions are "definitionally... a little bit scammy" — your app must monetize as well or better than the next bidder. (claimed/speculation)

### Trial & onboarding

- No trial mechanics discussed for YourMove. Host aside (secondhand, re: hard-paywall apps generally): "your sales pipeline is the onboarding experience and the paywall" — onboarding is the most labor-intensive build; the core feature is often the LEAST labor-intensive.

### Content strategy (organic) / PR / SEO

- PR (Fox, CNBC, Wired, Washington Post, WSJ, Newsweek op-ed) converted ~zero users: a Fox Business appearance "got me less views than a single decent Reddit post." Its real value = backlinks from credible media → "very good domain authority at the door." (claimed)
- With that authority, targeted keywords that were low-competition but relatively high-volume (e.g. "pickup lines", "good morning messages", "best dating apps" listicles) with blog posts carrying pop-up ads for his own product → SEO drove "nearly half of our traffic" at the $30k/mo level. (claimed)
- Never paid for or hunted backlinks; "right place at the right time with a very viral product." Explicitly cautions the strategy may not work now — their SEO has "declined pretty meaningfully" as AI/Google changes made such blog posts redundant. (claimed)
- Reddit: no strategy, just posting in small niche subreddits (r/SwipeHelper and similar dating-optimization subs); got early attention and STILL gets residual SEO traffic from those old posts. His Reddit account was eventually suspended (reported by a customer, per the on-screen aside). (claimed)
- Flyers in Washington Square Park: did not work. (claimed)
- Every marketing channel is hard and needs significant time investment before verdict: SEO ≈ 3 months of intentional work before seeing anything, 3–6 months before you can distinguish "not viable" from "execution failure." "There's no real marketing channel that you can just spin up in like two weeks and be done with it." UGC "takes an insane amount of work even when you're managing people." (claimed)

### Paid ads

- Did "a decent bit of Google ads"; worked well for some of the ~12 products tried, not at all for others — different channels route to different products, easier because it's a web funnel where different content routes to different offers. (claimed)
- Framework: Google/SEO = capturing LATENT demand — capped by the "area under the curve" of existing search volume; Meta/TikTok = creating INDUCED demand — higher theoretical ceiling but harder because you bid against everyone monetizing that demographic. (claimed/speculation)

### Launch & sequencing

- ~12 products tested under one website/brand; survivors: texting assistant, profile writer, profile review, AI photos. A keyboard app was a bad bet ("deeply broken on the inside," over budget, over time); dating coaching monetized fine but was "very high maintenance" and dropped. (claimed)
- Quit-the-job rule: self-set line at $20k/mo revenue ≈ $10k/mo profit — the point of covering all NYC living expenses with no savings burn and no staff cuts. Actually quit at ~$17–18k/mo; Opendoor's November layoff then handed him severance ("the happiest moment in the past couple of years"). (claimed)
- Deliberately ran the business UNPROFITABLE during his last employed year, funneling salary into growth experiments and contractors: "my hourly rate is like $150 an hour and I can hire a bunch of people for $50 an hour that know better." (claimed)
- Personal arc: first-ever project had TAM under $1M ("you can probably guess how well that went"); three failed projects; 4 years from starting to first dollar, 5 years to not needing a job. (claimed)
- Capital-fit heuristic: bootstrap problems "too small to do viably with venture capital" — his VC-backed competitors "didn't go anywhere." Don't bootstrap network-effect products (e.g. new dating apps): painkiller, yes, but definitionally needs big budget. "Build an app that sends tweets, not the next Twitter." (claimed/speculation)

### Retention & product

- Founding signal worth trusting: unsolicited PULL ("wait, can I use this? can I give it to friends?") vs. the usual push ("would you try this thing?"). High intent = highly monetizable. (claimed)
- Painkiller vs vitamin: dating is a painkiller tied to core human needs — friends cite $10k, $50k, $100k marriage bounties (ALF's $100k marriage bounty referenced) — "a pain that doesn't go away until it's solved"; vitamins (calendar 10% better, friend-making apps) require convincing. Exception: longevity vitamins monetize because of "Peter Task types" (almost certainly "Peter Thiel," auto-caption garble — UNCERTAIN). (speculation/anecdote)
- Meta-heuristic: build solutions to problems technology invented in the last few years (dating-app marketing burden, screen-time blockers, anti-AI-cheating, "is my boyfriend cheating" scanners). (speculation)
- Landing page didn't need to sell the problem: "dating apps are exhausting" is the whole pitch. (claimed)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- 10–12 Upwork contractors at peak, most only a couple hours/week: one dev at 40 hrs/wk, plus email-sequence writer, SEO, ads manager, designer, Webflow, and a $5/hr customer-support person. (claimed)
- Hire-as-coach pattern: hire someone who does the work AND trains you (e.g. Google Ads), so you learn what good looks like and hire better next time. First dev terrible, second good; agencies = "terrible time." (claimed)
- Host-relayed (secondhand, founder "lots" from Consumer Club): break each app into 3–5 priced parts, use ChatGPT to estimate complexity, negotiate, hard cap ~$2,000 per iOS app on Upwork; onboarding/paywall is the labor-heavy part, core feature the cheap part.
- Tech at start: GPT-3 era Da Vinci 2 (he corrects himself from GPT-2), when commercial use required an OpenAI Google-form approval taking weeks. (claimed)
- Post-exit lifestyle datapoint: ~5 months of 20–30 hrs/week while it "printed money"; "work 5 hours a week and this prints $15,000." Started as a digital nomad in Latin America, settled in NYC. (claimed)

### Other

- Big-tech lessons mostly don't transfer to small business: "the biggest failure point... is people trying to take lessons from big tech and apply it here." Marketing and product are a discovery process; "I built the product, now I just need someone to do marketing" is a joke. (claimed)
- Keep the day job as the funding engine — an "infinite runway" psychology; people who quit first "flounder... 3 to 6 months, can't figure anything out, start panicking, get a job again." Status maintenance made the transition psychologically easier. (claimed/anecdote)
- Host ad-read numbers (incentivized, treat as marketing): Consumer Club members/interviewees at median ~$1M ARR; paywallexperiments.com built from 422 profitable paywall experiments; SpyTalk found a "106x outlier" fitness TikTok; SpyTalk claimed used by ElevenLabs, "Ammo" (uncertain), and Voodoo (owner of BeReal).

## Contrarian positions

- PR is worthless for direct user acquisition and valuable ONLY as a domain-authority/backlink asset for SEO — inverts the usual "get press for downloads" advice.
- Deliberately running unprofitable for a year while employed, treating the W2 salary as venture funding for contractors, rather than the indie-hacker default of solo + profitable from day one.
- Hired 10–12 contractors extremely early — against the "stay solo as long as possible" indie consensus.
- Soft paywall retained on the core product in an era when this very podcast's ecosystem preaches hard paywalls; hard paywall applied only where unit economics force it (AI photos compute).
- "Every channel takes months; there is no two-week channel test" — against the rapid-fire channel-testing meta.
- Explicitly disclaims his own playbook's repeatability: the SEO window came from being early + press, "I don't know if the SEO strategy would work now."

## Crave transfer

The transferable core is the PR-as-backlinks insight and the channel-patience rule: for Crave, local Austin press (Statesman, Eater Austin, Austin Monthly, local TV) should be pursued for domain authority and local-SEO ranking ("best breakfast tacos austin"-class keywords are exactly his "low-competition, decent-volume" pattern), while expecting near-zero direct installs from the coverage itself — and budgeting 3–6 months before judging any channel. His latent-vs-induced demand frame fits Crave well: local food search is real latent demand, so search-capture (SEO/ASO/Google) is the natural first channel over TikTok-induced demand. Do NOT transfer his soft paywall or slow-monetization arc: he had a web funnel, ~80% margins on novelty AI output, and admits the 2022 AI-novelty + press window is gone — Crave's hard paywall decision is already made and his 6-months-before-monetizing is the mistake he himself renounces. Also discount the whole story for survivorship (he says outright he got lucky and captured little of a market Rizz-style apps later took) and note the host's paywall-tooling incentive shapes what gets asked. His quit-line arithmetic (revenue ≈ 2x personal breakeven before quitting) is a sensible, transferable personal-finance rule independent of category.
