## Arc

Matt vibe-coded a citation-checking web tool over ~11 months, posted his first-ever TikTok (Dec 2024) which hit ~245.6K views, and ~5 months later was acquired (effectively aqua-hired) by Jenni AI via an unsolicited Instagram DM. Three months into running Jenni's UGC program he claims 0→75M views at under $2 CPM with 150 creators. The video exists to showcase his creator-scouting/training playbook ("camera charisma," engagement bait) on Superwall's founder-marketing podcast.

### Content strategy (organic)

- "Engagement bait" > rage bait: engineer statements that split the audience roughly half-and-half (some attack, some defend the creator) — "that is the sweet spot. And that's exactly how we got 30 million X views."
- The 30M X (Twitter) views case: a 5-second TikTok (Ruby's second video ever, on a brand-new account) — surprised-face reaction + long text overlay ("just seen a girl in the library writing an essay. No notes, no open tabs, no ChatGPT, no Jenni AI... like a psychopath") — got screenshotted onto X; the initial dunk-tweet plus a defending quote-tweet that itself got 15M views. ~3,000 TikTok comments; a supportive comment got ~95,000–100,000 likes. Matt wrote the overlay copy himself. First-party (screens shown on-air).
- Long text on a 5-second video forces ~3 loops to read it (~15s read time) — a deliberate watch-time/loop mechanic (host's observation, Matt agreed).
- Second Ruby example: ~1.5M-view video ("professor said if I even think ChatGPT wrote your essay, you get a zero... use a humanizer for the grammar and Jenni for the fake citations") — fewer views than the 30M-spawner but judged higher-converting because the product plug is explicit and high-intent. Both scripts "written verbatim by me [Matt]."
- Vanity-view warning: "A viral StudyTok that didn't mention Jenni is completely useless." Always balance virality vs. plug strength; adjust when you fall too far either way.
- Viral-sense training: prescribed doomscrolling — 30 min/day in your niche + 30 min/day explicitly OUTSIDE your niche, to import formats across niches. Also study videos that DIDN'T work, not just outliers (his critique of outlier tools like "SpyTalk" — name uncertain, host's tool suggestion).
- Luck is a real factor: two near-identical videos (same script, same creator, 3 days apart) — one got 2,000x more views. "The answer is it was luck... optimize your surface area for luck." Don't over-attribute causality to micro-variations; iterate to make videos BETTER rather than copying more exactly.
- Tested pattern at scale: creators who are "emotive with their eyes" outperform — claims enough volume to "almost run hypothesis tests." Claimed.
- Matt's own first video (for his startup, Dec 8–9 2024): the comically-long-URL format ("ihaveanessaydueatmidnightandchatgptwontstopgivingmefakecitations.com"), adapted not invented — 245.6K views on a first-ever TikTok. Advice: at 0→$1K MRR, imitate/adapt proven formats; novelty only matters at hundreds-of-K MRR scale.
- Format-first product development: Jenni is running an experiment building a study tool starting FROM a TikTok format ("this hasn't been done in study... if we had a feature that did this") and working backwards to product — "the future of consumer apps is starting with the TikTok format and building the product backwards."
- Viral videos as public product-market-fit signal: you can "spy on product market fit" via other apps' video stats — content-market fit as a proxy (host's framing, Matt endorsed).

## Contrarian positions

- Ignore follower counts and marketplace stats entirely; scout "camera charisma" in unrelated niches and train from scratch (vs. standard influencer-list outreach).
- Expect 25+ videos before a new creator breaks 10K views — patience + coaching, vs. the common expect-viral-in-week-one churn model.
- Frame-for-frame copying of viral videos doesn't work (has watched it fail); understand principles and improve one element instead.
- Outcome-only analysis (outlier tools) is "a fool's game" — luck dominates single-video outcomes; develop viral sense by also studying failures.
- Build product backwards from a proven TikTok format (growth→product, not product→growth).
- Hire young founders as marketers over experienced pure creatives.
- Deliberately engineered polarization ("engagement bait" splitting the audience ~50/50) as a repeatable growth mechanic, distinguished from rage bait.

## Crave transfer

Category mismatch is the biggest caveat: Jenni is a single-decision AI utility for a globally huge, meme-dense pain point (essays due at midnight) with 5–6M users to fund a 150-creator program; Crave is a local, repeat-use discovery product launching in one city, so "75M views at <$2 CPM" economics and the engagement-bait-at-scale machine do not transfer at launch — most views would land outside Austin. What DOES transfer: (1) founder-led content before any creator spend — Matt's own first-video-245K story is the zero-budget path Crave actually needs, and Austin food TikTok/Reels is a proven, geo-taggable niche; (2) the scouting model — small Austin food/lifestyle creators with camera charisma but low followers are exactly the cheap, coachable pool Crave can afford, and "better deals with non-influencers" is directly usable; (3) engagement-bait mechanics adapt well to food ranking ("this app says the best queso in Austin is X" is natural half-agree/half-outraged bait that plugs the product intrinsically, like the Jenni library video). Distrust the headline attribution ($750K/mo "thanks to" a 3-month-old program) and note zero paywall/trial/pricing content here despite the Superwall wrapper — nothing in this transcript informs Crave's hard-paywall decisions.
