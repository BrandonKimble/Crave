## Arc

Dan founded Massive (AI job-application autofill, "Tinder for jobs") as a pivot after a VC-backed product failed ($3M raised). A LinkedIn validation post went unexpectedly viral (~8M impressions), the waitlist then converted terribly, and he ground from ~0 to ~$200K/mo over ~2 years via guerrilla outreach, then UGC/influencers/paid ads. The episode is a Superwall-sponsored playbook interview: what worked (audience-targeted hooks, copying proven formats, pricing changes), what didn't (waitlists, skits, monthly-retainer influencers), plus a callout of "gray-hat" fake-testimonial UGC by a competitor.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Massive is a hard-paywall subscription: "You got a hard pay wall, like sell your product... if it solves a problem, people want to pay for it." No price points given. (claimed)
- "We doubled our revenue from pricing changes" — no detail on what changed. (claimed)
- Yoga Body anecdote: he paid ~$100 for a stretching course through a terrible ~15-minute checkout ("website from like 2010", checkout button hidden on mobile) because the creative was that good — lesson: creative > conversion-rate optimization; "the marginal benefits aren't going to be there... it's going to be in the creative." (anecdote)
- Onboarding flows are commodity: he "ripped" his onboarding from somewhere, as did a peer (Zach); differentiation isn't there. (anecdote)

### Trial & onboarding

- Waitlist verdict: ~40–50K waitlist signups collected in ~2 months pre-launch, but "only about half a percentage of the waitlist converted to even signing up... No one paid for it." Waitlists are "useless"; if redoing it, skip the waitlist entirely and "get people to pay," even for a small version. (first-party)
- Idea-validation alternative: spend $1–2K max on a handful of influencers with a tight value prop to test whether the message converts to subscriptions; influencers over UGC for testing because UGC "takes too long to iterate" and influencers have "proven distribution" (consistent views). Cites Cal AI as having done this right. (claimed/speculation)

### UGC & creator deals

- Current scale: ~3–4M views/week from UGC/influencer content; earlier at ~500K views/week they "were actually probably converting as much" — scaling views broke conversion. (claimed)
- Getting 3–5 viral videos/day now and can't tell which drive conversion; build the video→revenue attribution muscle early "when you do get one you can clearly see in Stripe... is this converting." (first-party)
- 80/20 rule: 80% of organic output should replicate formats already proven viral ("it's irresponsible... to not do formats that are already doing well"), 20% net-new experiments. (claimed)
- "Career Dave" format (from competitor Source): consistently-viral two-face authentic-discovery style; cites view counts read off screen "837 [K?] 210 [K?] 280 [K?]" (units garbled in captions — uncertain). Creator "Alyssa" iterated the same format for weeks: 382 views vs 1.7M views for near-identical videos differing only in micro-expressions/authenticity; started from hundreds of views, hit 6,000, kept iterating ~4 more weeks. Conversions on this format "very high" (no number). (claimed/secondhand)
- Most UGC accounts are "slop": 500 videos at a few hundred views each because creators never iterate on what got 6K views. (claimed)
- 11M-view video case study: "Stop using Indeed" video did ~11M views but "almost no conversions... like 20K MRR" attributable (phrase garbled — could mean ~$20K MRR or "20" conversions; uncertain). Watched live: 0→3M views no conversions, 3→3.5M "a ton," 3.5→5M none — audience mix shifting within one viral run. (first-party)
- Audience targeting via hook wording: "Stop using LinkedIn to apply to jobs" → ~1M views AND high conversion (white-collar audience); "Stop using Indeed" → views but near-zero conversion + hate comments (blue-collar audience won't pay). The hook's first 3 seconds is the audience-targeting mechanism, like Facebook ad targeting. (first-party)
- College students "don't convert for us at all" — skits targeting them failed; skits generally don't work for Massive because they don't target the problem. (first-party)
- UGC scaling verdict: "UGC just doesn't scale that hard" — to scale it he'd need 200–400 creators doing the same style with heavy management; his year-long UGC program produced the 11M video within 4 days of a restart, then "that program dies" without constant management. (first-party)
- Paid ads > UGC for learning: "you should pay to get that data" — paid gives real conversion data fast vs guessing with organic. Exception: broad everyone-products (Neurogum: ~100K TikTok-shop affiliate creators, "maybe nine plus figures") where views alone matter. (claimed/secondhand)
- Gray-hat callout (competitor PrepAI): fake "I landed a job" testimonial videos copied word-for-word from real people's layoff/offer videos, moderated comments as the conversion surface ("Trojan horse"), fake persona "Nicole" accounts; he calls it illegal, predicts FTC trouble, refuses the tactic even if it's "the difference between $100 million a year or $200 million a year"; also says PrepAI copied Massive's onboarding "word for word... colors, buttons." (secondhand/claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Simple positioning wins: "Tinder for jobs" instantly understood; "increases reply rates from creators... two to three times more views" vs vague phrasings like "job search on autopilot" (caption garbled around the 2–3x claim — uncertain what it modifies). (claimed)
- Organic has no conversion-optimization loop (platform optimizes views, not purchases) — host's framing, Dan agrees; this is why one viral video sprays across wrong audiences. (claimed)
- Rip and adapt: study competitor funnels/ads ("funnel hack"), spend heavily on research but then execute — "you'll spend 90% of your day researching... just go do and try it out." (claimed)

### Paid ads

- "We increased revenue 50% by doing ads in a specific way" — specifics not given; came out of "80% of stuff that didn't work." (claimed)
- Yoga Body as best-ad-account example: hyper-specific problem callouts ("If you are running more than 10 miles and you have tight hips..."), ~6 ads seen before he converted, same creatives running ~2 years, "probably spending millions of dollars a month" (speculation). Cold-audience insight: target people who aren't yet problem-aware, then make them aware. (anecdote/speculation)
- Headway (secondhand): "$200 to $300 million a year, 30% margins, two times year-over-year growth on straight paid... performance marketing," bootstrapped, product doesn't solve a painful problem — they won on paid-ads arbitrage persistence. (secondhand)

### Launch & sequencing

- Origin: viral LinkedIn validation post from ~500 connections → ~8M impressions, ~5,000 comments; 2 months to build; ~40–50K waitlist; launched ~April; waitlist converted ~0.5% to signups; first ~100–500 paying users came from manual Hacker News pushes, scraped emails, "heartfelt" cold outreach. (first-party)
- April→December: manual grinding to ~$20K MRR ("orr" garbled — likely MRR; uncertain) with founder + 1 engineer (Rohan), weeks from running out of money. (first-party)
- Prior context: had raised $3M from VCs for the pre-pivot product. (first-party)
- At five-figure MRR trying to reach six: stop researching, pure execution — pricing changes, UGC, whatever you have energy for; 90% of people never post a single video or hire a single creator out of fear; his wins came from the 20% that worked out of 80% failures. (claimed)

### Retention & product

- Audience choice = revenue lever (Sensor Tower walk-through, numbers read off screen, all approximate/secondhand): note-taking apps for younger users ~170K downloads / ~$100K/mo combined; "Answers AI" maybe $10–15K MRR; Minutes AI targeting an older audience ~30K downloads / ~$300K/mo — "three times more money with 1/5 or 1/6th the downloads... same freaking app just targeted towards a different audience." (secondhand, read live off Sensor Tower)
- Master School / Maestro: bootcamp at ~$50M/yr tripled revenue in under a quarter via an AI-positioned funnel + 100% scholarships (likely government money, he speculates, analogous to Main Street's R&D-tax-credit play until "the government closed that loophole"). (secondhand/speculation)
- Team: founder + 1 full-time engineer + "a handful of VAs and ops people" at ~$200K/mo. (first-party)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Covered above: ~3-person core team at $2M ARR; VC money spent pre-pivot; nearly out of money at ~$20K MRR before growth kicked in.

## Contrarian positions

- Waitlists are useless (~0.5% conversion first-party); charge from day one instead — against the standard pre-launch-waitlist playbook.
- Views are a vanity trap: an 11M-view video produced almost nothing; a 1M-view video with the right two-word audience cue (LinkedIn vs Indeed) out-converted it.
- Don't be creative: 80% of organic should copy already-viral formats; experimentation is "irresponsible" at scale.
- Paid ads beat UGC for most conversion-driven subscription apps (better data, faster iteration) — said on a channel ecosystem that heavily romanticizes UGC.
- CRO/onboarding polish is commoditized ("we all ripped it"); creative quality is the real lever — he paid $100 through a broken 2010-era checkout.
- Anti-indie-hacker/anti-Levels: portfolio-of-small-bets culture kills big outcomes; scale the winner instead.

## Crave transfer

Category mismatch is the biggest filter: Massive is a broad, national, acute-pain utility (job loss) where one viral hook can find millions of in-market buyers; Crave is a single-city discovery product, so the "3–4M views/week" machinery and even the 11M-view case study describe a distribution physics Crave can't access at launch — but the underlying mechanism (the hook's first 3 seconds IS the audience filter; LinkedIn-vs-Indeed) transfers directly to Austin content ("stop picking restaurants off Google Maps reviews" vs a generic foodie hook). The waitlist finding (~0.5% conversion, first-party) is the strongest transferable datapoint and supports Crave's hard-paywall-from-day-one stance; likewise "charge immediately, even for a small version." His $1–2K influencer smoke test is cheap and well-suited to a one-city launch (Austin food creators have exactly the geo-targeted "proven distribution" he describes). Discount the Headway/Master School/Sensor Tower numbers heavily — secondhand, eyeballed, and survivor-selected — and note the venue's incentive gradient: a Superwall-sponsored show structurally over-weights hard-paywall subscription success stories. His "paid ads beat UGC for conversion data" advice conflicts with Crave's no-ad-budget constraint; the honest read is that he'd say organic-only is the slow, high-management path.
