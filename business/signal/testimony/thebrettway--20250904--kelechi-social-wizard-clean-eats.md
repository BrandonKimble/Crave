## Arc

Kelechi Onyeama, 22, moved from Nigeria to the US as a broke tourist, was kicked out by relatives (police called), and under a five-month roof deadline built Social Wizard, an AI "rizz"/texting-hints app that rode the GPT-4 Vision release window to ~$1M revenue, then built and sold Clean Eats (skin/weight food scanner) for low six figures. The interview is a growth-playbook walkthrough: micro-creator deals, onboarding psychology, ASO, and "inherently viral = show something never seen before."

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Launched Social Wizard at **$6.99/week** because that's what comparable apps charged ("$6.99 was what most apps were starting with"). (claimed)
- Later raised to **$9.99/week (~$10/wk)** on quality grounds; "that just shut up everything. LTV went up, more cash through the door." No churn numbers given. (claimed)
- Has monthly and yearly tiers but **pushes weekly** — "faster payback period." (claimed)
- Clean Eats charged **mostly yearly subscriptions**: more upfront cash, but "LTV is capped… most people end up canceling" before renewal; on Social Wizard's $10/wk, a 3-week stayer equals a Clean Eats yearly. (claimed)
- Host anecdote: a Whop co-founder rejected a subscription-pricing proposal because "we're only going to be a $150 billion company" — pricing decisions cap company ceiling. (anecdote)

### Trial & onboarding

- Onboarding opens with a **rigged test**: user must reply to a fake girl's message, an LLM scores it 1–10 but is internally capped at 5 — "everyone who takes that test is going to fail… max you can do is 50%" — then the app shows the reply it would have generated. "Once people see that, they're sold." (first-party description of own funnel)
- Then 3–4 goal/priming questions ("do you want to sound more flirty…"), then the paywall + trial ask. Deliberately avoided explicit sexual framing to stay Apple-approvable (app rated 17+; framed entirely as "social skills," the word "rizz" never appears in-app — also claims this widens the market). (claimed)
- "**Session one is everything**" — one shot to extract something (subscribe, start trial, leave a review) from a new user. (attributed to "one of my close friends")
- KPIs he tracked: **install → first hint time**, and **hints generated before first copy** (average user generates ~10 hints before copying one). Volume thesis: more generation = longer retention "even if the hints are ass." (first-party / claimed)

### UGC & creator deals

- Zero ad budget at start ("I was poor"): posted TikToks himself daily until one format spiked at **~17k views**; says even **3–5k views is signal** on TikTok worth dissecting and iterating. (first-party)
- Scaled via **micro-creators** (streamers pulling 2–3k views/video but with at least one spiked video = upside potential). First paid streamer clip: **$120**; typical streamer price in the niche **~$400/video**. That first collab video did **~2M views on Instagram, ~400k on TikTok**, and flipped revenue from **$0 (Jan–Feb) → ~$2,000 within one week → ~$25,000 over the next two weeks**. (claimed)
- **Micromanaged every creator video**: showed his own proof-of-concept clip, gave rigid guidelines ("it should be a system"), approved before posting; creators add tone/creativity only. (first-party)
- Negotiation playbook: always close on a phone call; open with status signaling (apps built, deals done, other creators — "sometimes I would even say I've worked with this creator. [expletive] lie"); frame first video as a **paid test** implying a long-term pipeline to bargain the price down. Claims ~90% of the time the first video pays for all future videos with that creator. (first-party, self-incriminating on honesty)
- Rule of thumb for spend: **$1,000 in should return ~$3,000 (3x)**. (claimed)
- Clean Eats creator rates much higher: average **$1,500–$2,000/video** (older, professional fitness creators, contracts, faster turnaround). Rizz-niche streamers were 16–17-year-olds, casual, flaky (3-day delays), but cheap — and had no other sponsor options ("you're either going to work with me or stake.com"), so **flat fee/retainer only, no CPM/affiliate**; fitness creators have many sponsor types so varied structures are negotiable. (claimed)
- Best Clean Eats deal: paid one creator **~$4.5–5k for 6 videos + stories** with a **2M-view minimum view clause (MVC)**; first video alone returned **~$6k**; total return **~$20k** from that creator. She missed the MVC (~"1 point something" million), so he extracted extra clips and stories twice before parting ways. (claimed)
- MVC mechanics he uses: tie the view clause to **one platform only** (whichever he expects to monetize best, e.g. Instagram — TikTok posts still required but don't count), **7-day view window per video**, sum across the deal's videos; shortfall = more content owed. (first-party)
- Creators optimize for the **total check, not clip count**: if they decline 4 clips/$5k and ask $7k, counter with $7k for 8 clips — "most times they will say yes" (attributes insight to "Zach too for account" — garbled, likely Zach of Cal AI). (claimed/secondhand)
- Host counter-anecdote: creators quoting $2k while pulling 2k views/video ("a dollar per view") — creator pricing is chaotic. (anecdote)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Marketing was **TikTok + Instagram Reels only**; no paid ads mentioned for either app. (first-party)
- Winning format = **pure product demo storytelling**: "damn, she's pretty, what do I say?" → screenshot → app visibly on screen → line → she replies → "boom." "Distribution is showing, not telling… just demo what it does." (first-party)
- "**Inherently viral means show me something I haven't seen before**" — cites Cal AI (photo→calories), Social Wizard (story screenshot→what to say), Umax (selfie→rating) as visuals never seen before. (claimed/opinion)
- The viral hook feature (story-screenshot opener) is NOT the retention feature (text-reply helper): "the story one is just how we get people into the app… the text one is the most retentive one." Funnel = novel visual hook in, habitual utility retains. (first-party)
- Referrals ≈ zero for this category: "no one actually refers their friends" even with giveaways and "crazy" offers — growth was 100% founder-pushed marketing. (first-party)
- Timing moat: built during GPT-4 Vision's staggered API release (juggled ~10 API keys against 10-req rate limits); at launch no competitor could do screenshot→reply. Attributes virality mostly to "timing and grit." (first-party)

### ASO & app store

- Renamed "Wizard"→"Social Wizard" because "wizard" surfaced games; now claims to rank #1 for both "wizard" and "social wizard." "The name of your app… in the early stage, it's probably like 30% of it." (claimed)
- Title format: **Name — dash — what it does** ("Social Wizard - up your game"; "Clean - food scanner"); subtitle equally weighted; shove target keywords into name, subtitle, description, even screenshots. Ratings also feed ranking. (claimed)
- **Health & fitness = most competitive App Store niche**: to rank for a keyword as generic as "food" you'd need "like 50k downloads a day," plus competitors run Apple Search ads against your keywords/brand. (claimed)
- NGL renaming anecdote (host): traction only after renaming to demographic-native slang. (secondhand)

### Launch & sequencing

- Revenue timeline: Jan–Feb 2024 = $0 → first viral creator video → $2k in a week → ~$25k in the following two weeks. (claimed)
- Clean Eats: launched ~Jan–Feb 2025, ~$60k revenue (~half profit) by mid-year, founder lost interest, tweeted he'd shut it down, tweet went viral → inbound buyers → **two-week buyer process, two-day close**, "very low six figure sale" (less than Social Wizard's earnings). Also rejected a Spain acqui-hire requiring relocation. (claimed)
- "Most apps are not sellable — like 90%"; sellability depends on evergreen niche (health/fitness). Selling was a "checking boxes play," mindset unlock more than money. (opinion/claimed)

### Retention & product

- Scale stats: ~1M screenshots uploaded, ~5M hints generated, ~50GB of images/text on Firebase. (claimed)
- Success proxy = return usage + hint-copy events (no explicit outcome feedback loop). (first-party)
- Product thesis: the app sells **brainstorming, not answers** — avg user generates ~10 hints before copying; the generation loop itself is the value. (first-party interpretation)
- Every winning consumer app solves one of three needs: **make money, find love, have fun** (health = all three). (opinion)
- One tool per problem wins in the US (vs. super-apps in China); rejects the "one AI interface replaces all apps" thesis; expects more apps/SKU-like niche variants, not fewer. (opinion)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Solo founder, no team mentioned for either app; ~10 OpenAI API keys at Social Wizard launch to dodge rate limits; data on Firebase. (first-party)
- Claims ~$70k spent on US immigration (tourist → student → O-1 → green-card transition in progress). (claimed)
- Prior failure: Caspid social app, built on a broken Dell laptop in Nigeria — ~2–3k users, 20k+ photos, dead in ~a week of US push. SAT 1260 (710 math / 550 English, per him). Mother gave 100,000 naira (~$100) for the flight. (first-party)

## Deal structures

- **Streamer (Social Wizard)**: first clip $120 flat; niche average ~$400/video flat. No CPM/affiliate — niche creators had no alternative sponsors, so flat-fee leverage was total.
- **Fitness creator (Clean Eats)**: ~$1,500–$2,000/video average. Marquee deal: ~$4.5–5k for 6 videos + stories, 2M-view MVC on one designated platform (7-day window per video, views summed), shortfall = additional clips/stories owed; actual: ~1.x M views, extra content extracted twice, ~$20k attributed return on ~$5k spend (first video alone ~$6k).
- Target economics: 3x return on creator spend; negotiate total check up while adding deliverables (creators anchor on the payout number, not clip count).

## Contrarian positions

- **Raise prices**: moving $6.99→$9.99/week improved LTV and cash with no reported downside — against the "cheaper converts better" reflex.
- **Push weekly over annual** for impulse/urgent-need apps (payback speed over LTV) — the opposite of standard "annual-first" paywall advice; yet used annual-first on Clean Eats, i.e., billing period should match use-case urgency, not a universal rule.
- **Referrals don't exist** in embarrassing-utility categories; don't build viral loops, buy/earn distribution instead.
- **Deliberately fail the user** in onboarding (score capped at 5/10) to manufacture the problem before the paywall.
- **3–5k views is real signal**, not failure — iterate from small spikes rather than waiting for a viral hit.
- **Most apps are unsellable (~90%)**; build for cash flow, not exit, unless in an evergreen niche.
- **Rejects the super-app/single-AI-interface future**; bets on ever-more single-purpose apps.
- Text-game nihilism: "when you meet a girl, she's already decided" — i.e., he doubts the core efficacy of his own product category; what he sells is confidence/brainstorming.

## Crave transfer

Category mismatch is the big caveat: both his apps are single-player, session-one-wow, weekly-billed impulse utilities with a near-zero-shame-referral dynamic; Crave is a repeat-use local-utility with data density as the moat, so his weekly-pricing and "extract in session one" maximalism don't map directly — though the underlying "session one must demonstrate the value proposition unmistakably" (his rigged-test = instant proof) is exactly the problem Crave's hard paywall onboarding must solve: show Austin-specific ranked-dish "proof of data" before the card ask. The most transferable, concrete material is the creator playbook: micro-creators with one spiked video at $120–400/clip (food/Austin creators will price nearer his fitness tier, $1.5–2k), rigid founder-written creative briefs demoing the product visually, single-platform MVCs with 7-day windows, 3x-return targeting, and negotiating clip count up while holding the check — all directly usable for Crave's no-ad-budget launch. His ASO advice (Name - what-it-does, subtitle keywords, niche competitiveness) transfers cheaply. Discount his revenue and deal-outcome numbers: all self-reported, he admits lying in creator negotiations, and the whole arc is survivorship (two dead apps reframed as prologue); also note his moat was a one-time model-release timing window Crave doesn't have — Crave's analog moat is city data density, which no creator video conveys unless the content shows a specific, surprising local ranking (his "show me something I haven't seen before" test applied to Austin food).
