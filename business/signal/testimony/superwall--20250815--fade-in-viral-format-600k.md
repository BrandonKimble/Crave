## Arc

Peter co-founded Stronger (bootstrapped, three engineers — him, best friend Jack, brother Joseph — from Northern Ireland, funded partly by government grants) roughly 2.5–3 years ago after pivoting from a hardware business. The app went viral organically on TikTok before it was even finished, and the team later discovered a repeatable 6-second "fade-in" Instagram format they estimate drove 150–300M views. The video exists to showcase that "viral on demand" playbook and, per the sponsorship, Superwall-driven paywall optimization.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Biggest monetization win was one macro change: moving from a freemium/premium model to a **hard paywall** — "overnight that was just our biggest boost, probably like 25% for us." (claimed)
- The hard paywall was discovered by accident: a toggle bug on New Year's Eve hard-paywalled everyone for "a couple of hours"; conversions spiked, complaints were minimal, so they made it permanent. (first-party anecdote)
- Current mechanic: free functionality still exists, but users must **start a free trial of Pro first**; if they cancel, the free tier unlocks after the trial expires. "If you can give people an excuse to not pay, they won't." (claimed)
- Paywall philosophy: macro tests (how often the paywall shows, how hard it is, which features it's attached to, video vs multi-stage paywall) over micro copy tests. Micro copy changes need "a couple of months" of volume to reach significance; a week-long test showing "2% difference" is "just standard deviations." (claimed/opinion)
- Category caps LTV: "your category defines what your macro limit is in terms of LTV per download" — a workout tracker's value prop competes with pen and paper; a workout planner competes with a personal trainer and can charge more. (opinion)
- Early monetization was "really bad" / LTV per user "super low"; AB testing (via Superwall, named) improved it to where they believe they're "probably one of the best at monetizing per user" among workout trackers. (claimed; sponsor-flavored)
- Hard paywall knowingly kills K-factor/sharing: "nobody sends a paid app to their friends" — and they don't even instrument K-factor. (claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Headline stats: 300M+ total views, 1M+ downloads, "multiple six-figure" business (host framing); Peter: ~1.2M users, ~$600k ARR, biggest market US but deepest penetration UK. (claimed)
- The "fade-in" format: ~6 seconds, fades in from black to a single image (the muscle-heatmap "strength score" screenshot). Estimated 150–200M views from it, "potentially even 300 million"; a "100 million view month" 2–3 months prior; run for 5–6 months and now decaying. Couple of videos hit 40M views; one shown had 143k likes. (claimed estimates)
- Average watch time on some fade-in videos was **over 2x video length** — average viewer watched twice. (claimed)
- The same near-identical video was replicated ~300 times (host framing) and still works. Conversion per view is "quite low," but the cost is trivial: ~a couple hours/week scheduling, plus internal tooling. (claimed)
- Horizontal scaling: a competitor ran the same format across ~10 accounts vs their 1–2 and "ratcheted up" downloads — creativity matters less than iteration volume. (secondhand observation)
- Spam-avoidance: micro-adjustments make each repost read as new to the algorithm — change a couple of hook words, light-mode background, legend, etc. (Figma/AI-assisted). (claimed)
- Sound: mostly one recognizable "brand sound" (a Future song, "let me know" — name uncertain from captions); trending sounds matter more on TikTok, "never found the sound to be make or break on Instagram." (opinion)
- Content taxonomy: educate or entertain. Their volume is mostly entertainment ("gym bro humor", skews Gen Z); education is more replicable/learnable for anyone ("literally just an AB test") and attracts the more profitable 25+ market. 30–40-year-olds are "where the money is." (opinion)
- TikTok vs Instagram strategies are "pretty much completely different": TikTok = 2–3 slide carousels/slideshows ("bread and butter", historically higher-converting than IG, weakening in the last year as ~20 workout apps copy); Instagram = the fade-in format. TikTok page has ~4.9M likes. (claimed)
- Format discovery method: mine other industries' working formats (e.g., a finance-tracker format), reverse-engineer WHY it engaged, reskin to your niche; the heatmap itself came from a viral Twitter-thread web app 6–7 years earlier that no one had done on mobile. Every piece of content is a funnel — hook/body/watch-time analytics identify the bottleneck; enough iterations and "you're guaranteed to win." (opinion/method)
- Founder-face content is an edge in the AI-slop era: vibe-coders won't show their face; authentic in-app screenshots (visible share/download buttons) outperform polished ads — "by looking less professional on TikTok, you actually perform much better." (opinion)

### Launch & sequencing

- First viral moment pre-launch: Jack posted a Canva-made video (volume-lifted-vs-objects timeline: Easter Island head, Stonehenge slab) from a work bathroom break; page gained **~15,000 followers in the first week** while the app wasn't yet downloadable; they finished/polished the app in ~1–2 weeks to catch the demand. (first-party anecdote)
- Follow-up engine: reply-stitch videos — commenters post their lifts ("I bench pressed 220 pounds for five reps"), Jack types it into the app on camera and shows the conversion ("that's 500 bowling balls") — an "infinite loop of content" (host's framing). Peter thinks it would work even better today. (claimed)
- Peaked at **#3 in Health & Fitness** on the App Store. (claimed)
- They had ~12 months with zero competition on the strength-score concept and regret not capitalizing harder. (claimed)

### Retention & product

- Product designed for screenshots: "those screenshots are what make us money" — heatmap colors, weight-lifted milestones (Empire State Building, Boeing 747 badges) all built to invoke curiosity in a TikTok slideshow. No designers on the team. (claimed)
- Fade-in format's weakness: viewers often don't realize it's from a workout tracker; app logo added to help; a more obviously-app screenshot would convert better at some virality cost. (opinion)
- Gamification dosage: rank/badge systems resonate with 15–23-year-olds but look "goofy" beyond that; keep it light because 30+ is the profitable market. Competitor that went full "League of Legends for the gym" won its niche via focus. (opinion/secondhand)
- Moat thesis for the AI era: features get vibe-coded in 1–2 weeks, so the durable moats are (1) network effects (social workout graph), (2) technical difficulty (their new isometric tile game — "if you try to get Claude to do it right now, it can't"), (3) invisible internal distribution tooling ("click one button... 20 social posts"). Copycats grow the pie and hand the category leader free App Store distribution (Cal AI vs "Cal's AI" clones with 100x review volume). (opinion)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- 3-person bootstrapped team, all engineers, no designers, no hired marketers; Peter + brother Joseph do almost all content; internal tooling for content production/scheduling. (claimed)
- Agencies: incentives never fully align; in-house wins because you skip "20% margin"; use agencies only to scale a format you already proved (pump one winner across "50 accounts") or to buy knowledge you can't get — never to find the winning format. (opinion)
- A few affiliates, "not that many anymore"; a more structured affiliate program planned as they go to two apps. (claimed)
- Government grant funding (Northern Ireland agencies) instead of VC. (claimed)

### Other

- Big players ("a few million a month" vs "50k a month") differentiate via brand + a small stable of high-quality ambassadors, each owning a hyper-specific niche (Runna, Represent, Ladder cited) — 3–4 ambassadors doing the heavy lifting beats spray-and-pray influencer spam. (secondhand/opinion)
- Two ways to build: visionary brand-first (Jobs) vs malleable product that ships its brand in collaboration with whoever it turns out to resonate with; they're doing the latter for app #2. (opinion)

## Deal structures

None discussed with numbers. Only directional: prefer 3–4 deeply-aligned ambassadors over one-off influencer deals; agencies take ~20% margin; affiliate program exists but terms not given.

## Contrarian positions

- Hard paywall over freemium for a _social_ app, knowingly sacrificing K-factor/viral sharing — and not even measuring K-factor.
- Repost the same video ~300 times with micro-adjustments; "spamming" a winning format beats novelty (they initially resisted this and say the restraint cost them).
- Entertainment content CAN convert (against the "viral entertainment doesn't convert" consensus) — though he concedes conversion-per-view is low and education is the better long-term lane.
- Micro paywall copy tests are mostly noise-reading; only macro changes matter at their volume.
- Copycats are net-positive for the category leader (free distribution + pie growth).
- Rough, obviously-in-app screenshots outperform polished creative.

## Crave transfer

The most transferable claim is the hard-paywall one — it's the single closest analogue to Crave's card-required gate, and Peter's "give people an excuse not to pay and they won't" plus the ~25% overnight boost is directional support, though note his "hard" paywall is softer than Crave's (free tier unlocks post-trial-cancel) and the number is a claimed anecdote from a sponsor-adjacent interview. The core growth engine transfers only partially: Stronger's virality rides on a screenshot-native in-app asset (the muscle heatmap) — the real lesson for Crave is not "post fade-ins" but "build one visually arresting, instantly-screenshotable artifact" (e.g., a city dish-ranking card or Crave Score map tile) and then horizontally scale one proven format across accounts with micro-variations. Heavy survivorship caveats: they were first-mover with ~12 months of zero competition in 2022-era organic TikTok, which Peter himself says is gone ("I don't know why anyone would go and make a workout tracker" now). Their category is global lifestyle with a universal audience; Crave is single-city discovery, so 100M-view spray formats are mostly wasted reach — but the founder-face reply-stitch loop ("tell me a dish, I show you its Crave Score on camera") is geography-targetable and matches Peter's own "human face is the edge" thesis. Ignore the K-factor indifference at face value for a discovery/social-utility app, but his reasoning (nobody shares a paid app) is a real risk Crave's hard paywall inherits.
