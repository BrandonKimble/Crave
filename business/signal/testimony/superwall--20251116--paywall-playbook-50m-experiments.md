## Arc

Superwall-podcast interview with a paywall-optimization consultant credited with 10,000+ paywall experiments and $50M+ in added client revenue across apps including Cal AI, Quitter, Claim, and Riz. His thesis: most founders chase acquisition while their paywall leaks; fixing placements and paywall mechanics can add ~$100k in months with zero new users. The episode is a walkthrough of his best-converting client paywalls and the reasoning behind each.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Benchmarks (claimed, from portfolio): trial start rate ≥15% is good, <15% needs work; trial-to-paid ≥30% is good, <30% needs work. (claimed)
- Non-trial (pay-now) apps, US-heavy traffic: install-to-paid ≥10% is "really good"; <4% is a problem; 4–10% is "not terrible but not great." (claimed)
- Auto-caption garble: "apps operating with like 81% trust star rate... 81% can easily become like a 15%" — almost certainly means an 8–11% trial start rate can be pushed to 15% in ~90% of cases. Flagged uncertain; the 15% target is consistent with his stated benchmark. (claimed, garbled numbers)
- Claim app: standard-looking 3-page multi-step trial paywall achieved 34% trial start rate (>2x his own benchmark); trial-to-paid was 50–55% ("Yeah, 50 55%" — could be "50, 55%"; treat as ~50-55%, uncertain). (claimed)
- Claim then REMOVED the free trial entirely and went paid-only because revenue was higher without it. Conditions he requires before removing a trial: (1) high install volume, (2) strong trial start rate, (3) strong trial-to-paid, (4) product solves a pressing, high-motivation problem. (claimed)
- Trial-mention density: mentioning "free trial" once or twice is not enough; Claim paywall mentions free ~5-7 times across 3 screens; label the annual product card "Try it free" (with "$59.99 per year" shown as billing detail), not "yearly plan." CTA = "Try for $0." (claimed practice)
- Hide trial LENGTH from the product card (it lives only in the 3-step timeline: "3-day free trial") — stating duration triggers "I have to remember to cancel in 3 days" anxiety. (claimed)
- Multi-step paywall vs single-screen: 20-40% conversion uplift, "many cases" 40%; average 20-40%. Caveat: only when executed right — copied multi-step paywalls often fail. (claimed)
- One paywall variant shows NO price at all on the product card — just "free" framing, price mentioned only in fine print. (claimed practice)
- Quitter (no trial): 3 content pages (visuals, podcasts/leaderboard/breathing exercises, real App Store reviews) with NO prices; prices only appear after tapping "Start my journey today" as a half-sheet covering half the screen — isolating the offer from distraction. He calls hiding prices until the CTA tap "the biggest thing that worked" for this paywall. (claimed)
- Quitter pricing mechanics: "60% off sale" + "limited spots remaining" (e.g. 9 spots) scarcity, borrowed from e-commerce; 60% computed against the monthly product; price anchoring with annual shown as $3.33/mo vs monthly $12.99/mo. (claimed practice)
- $3.33 pricing rationale: random-looking numbers feel "more authentic" than .99 pricing, which consumers have developed defenses against — though he separately guarantees $14.99 beats $15.00 in most tests. Host math: $3.33×12 = $39.96/yr, reads as "$3" but pads ~$4/yr vs $2.99 — buffer for paid acquisition. (claimed/speculation)
- Pricing methodology options: copy competitors; back into ROAS-positive price from UA data (payback within ~a month); cost-plus for AI-cost-heavy apps (e.g. $20/yr cost/user + ~20% perceived value → ~$40/yr); price-sensitivity-meter surveys. Early stage: don't over-engineer, pick something sane, price-test later. (claimed)
- Biggest founder mistake: copying a famous paywall one-to-one, then not iterating when it fails — "happens many times every week." Tactics don't transfer across categories (utility tactic in health/fitness fails). (claimed)
- Steal conversion tactics from e-commerce/dropshipping (Temu, Shein): spin wheels, scarcity, discount presentation. (claimed practice)

### Trial & onboarding

- "80% of the effort you put into building your app should go to onboarding and paywall" — host attributing to "a lot of big app founders." (secondhand)
- Trial toggle (Riz app): default ON = 7-day free trial $9.99/wk; toggle OFF ("skip free trial") = $6.99/wk, i.e. $3/wk discount for buying immediately. Rationale: capture buyers at different price points/intents; user feels in control. Host notes the math: at 30% trial-to-paid the two paths yield roughly equal revenue/user. (claimed)
- Cal AI uses the same toggle with NO discount (identical price with or without trial) — works because health/fitness users arrive with high purchase intent; discount-toggle fits lower-intent/younger audiences (Riz, under-18 with low purchasing power). Decision rule: test both. (claimed)
- ~10% of users choose to buy WITHOUT a trial when given the toggle. (claimed)
- Drawback of "Try for $0" framing: Apple's native sheet reveals the real subscription ("free for 3 days, then $29.99/yr") → maybe ~10% more transaction abandonment, but abandonment paywalls convert well so net positive IF trial-to-paid is strong (>30%); if weak, expect refunds / install-to-paid drop. (claimed)

### Retention & product (placements)

- Placement checklist he installs before ANY experimentation ("bare minimum foundation"): onboarding paywall; transaction abandonment; session-start paywall (every session start, at least once/day); paywall after every N user actions (e.g. every coin scan); trial-canceled offer; subscription-canceled offer; usage-limit reminder ("almost out of your 5 free scans"). Missing placements = money on the table. (claimed practice)
- Transaction abandonment rules: hit users with a discounted offer immediately after they cancel the native purchase sheet; ONLY discount the ANNUAL product — discounting weekly/monthly "doesn't work, we tested dozens of times." (claimed)
- Advanced abandonment tactic: trial toggle on the abandonment paywall — with-trial = 80% off, without-trial = 90% off (cheaper without trial) to harvest direct purchases (~10% of users take no-trial). Optionally add an undiscounted monthly/weekly below the discounted annual purely as an anchor. (claimed)
- Discount presentation: spin-the-wheel animation landing on the discount ("you won 80% off — claim it") beat showing the discount outright by a "double digit" percentage increase (exact number withheld). Earned-reward psychology from e-commerce popups. (claimed, number withheld)
- Case study (unnamed entertainment app): ~40-50k organic installs/mo, stable; April→September engagement took install-to-paid from 1.1% to ~4% with no new users, no paid acquisition — pure paywall work. (claimed, his own client)
- "$100k revenue increase in 3 months without new users" framed as a repeatable pattern from paywall experiments alone. (claimed)

## Contrarian positions

- Growth = fixing conversion leaks, not more users/ads/viral TikToks; the paywall is behind every viral TikTok.
- Remove a WORKING free trial (34% start, ~50%+ trial-to-paid) and go paid-only if volume + intent are high — most advice treats trials as sacred.
- Hide the trial duration and even the price from the product card; call the annual plan "free."
- Hide ALL prices until the user taps the CTA (Quitter).
- Random prices ($3.33) can beat charm pricing because .99 reads as manipulation — while simultaneously conceding $14.99 still beats $15.
- Never discount monthly/weekly on abandonment — annual only.
- Copy e-commerce (Temu/Shein) tactics into apps; but never copy another app's paywall verbatim.

## Crave transfer

Most transferable: the placement-foundation checklist is moot pre-launch for Crave's hard-wall model (nothing to see un-entitled), but transaction-abandonment and trial-toggle mechanics map directly onto Crave's card-required wall — the with-trial-annual vs cheaper-no-trial toggle (~10% direct buyers) and annual-only discounting are cheap, testable ideas, and the "mention free 5-7×, hide trial length, label annual 'try it free'" framing applies verbatim to Crave's $39.99/yr trial-annual SKU. His pay-now benchmarks (≥10% install-to-paid good, <4% bad, US traffic) are the single most useful calibration number here for Crave's monthly pay-now path — but they come from consumer apps with mass-market wow (calorie scanning, quitting porn), while Crave's value is local-data utility that may show slower on a first-session wall, so treat 4% as aspiration, not floor. Discount every claim heavily: this is a consultant on his tooling vendor's podcast showing only winners, with no verifiable dashboards, and the flagship 1.1%→4% case rode 40-50k organic installs/mo — a distribution position Crave will not have at launch, so absolute revenue extrapolations ("$100k in 3 months") do not transfer. The Claim precedent (drop the trial entirely when intent and volume are high) is the wrong lesson for a zero-brand-trust city launch; Crave's trial-annual path exists precisely because trust is unearned.
