## Arc

Podcast interview on the Superwall YouTube channel: Joseph (host, Consumer Club) interviews Jay, CEO of Bloom, a Gen Z learn-to-invest app claimed at millions in ARR and 1M+ active users. The video exists to showcase Bloom's organic TikTok engine (faceless/AI accounts + micro-creator retainer-plus-CPM program) and the organic→paid-ads recycling loop, while promoting Consumer Club and sponsor Superwall.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Creator sourcing philosophy: recruit small creators who already make "the right videos" (angle, lighting, narrative, trend selection), NOT big followings; have them create a NEW account from scratch, warm it up, then scale. (claimed; strategy attributed to "Lauren from Pebble" — name uncertain, auto-caption)
- Standard deal A: flat retainer $1,000/month + CPM bonuses triggered at 10K / 50K / 100K view thresholds; CPM bonus $2–$4 per 1,000 views. (first-party operational detail, claimed)
- Standard deal B: lower retainer $500 for 60 videos/month but a higher CPM. (claimed)
- Overall retainer range: $500–$1,000/month for 60 videos/month = 2 posts/day every day. (claimed)
- Payout cap: $1,000 max per video, reached around 1M+ views. (claimed)
- View-threshold logic by creator size: brand-new accounts start CPM eligibility at 10K views; creators with 5–10K followers start at 100K views. (claimed)
- Explicit rationale: below 100K views conversion is weak; the 10K–50K tier exists purely as creator-retention psychology ("like employee retention... bonuses"), not for downloads. Three-phase design: small retainer up front (pure-CPM caused creators to quit early), mid-tier incentives to 100K, real money above 100K ("$1,000 is pretty big money for college students"). (claimed, with reasoning)
- Creators run 1–2 accounts each, cross-posting the same content to A/B test which account the algorithm favors. Accounts look personal ("a person's face with 'learn to invest with XYZ'"), content is semi-branded. (claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- 2023: massively scaled TikTok paid ads; CAC deteriorated by mid-2023 and "really tailored down" by end of 2023 → pivoted to organic in 2024. (claimed)
- 2024 winning narrative: "how to get rich" — e.g. "what does the billionaire nanny do for their kids" format; ran viral for ~6 months. Reddit-carousel format also did well. (claimed)
- 2025 shift: relatability — "StudyTok" content targeting college students; worked for the past ~6 months. BUT: study-content views converted worse because Bloom is fintech, not a study app — views ≠ conversion when narrative mismatches product. (claimed)
- Production engine: internal team + an agency specializing in AI content generation (HeyGen — transcribed as "Hey Jen"/"hen", uncertain); 5–10 seeded accounts with existing following, ~2 posts/day per account; when something hits viral, propagate it to ~20 accounts. Scaled to 20 accounts "because we have HeyGen." (claimed)
- Conversion layer: NO direct CTA in organic ("if you say 'download Bloom today' they know it's an ad"); a dedicated comment team drops the app name in comments so discovery feels organic. (claimed)
- Trend cycles last 3–6 months before saturation. Current push: TikTok is promoting carousels as a main feature. Predicted next waves: Bible/prayer apps, therapy/AI-therapy, "low-key self-care" narratives; example of borrowing a movement's marketing angle without building for it (a quit-porn app marketed to Christians, "recently hit 100 or 200k a month" — host's secondhand claim, figure vague/uncertain, app name garbled in captions "Twitter"). Bloom plans to test a Bible angle (Proverbs 13:11 = invest wisely) with 1–2 accounts. (claimed/speculation)
- On AI saturation: content creation is commoditizing; "in the end, creativity wins"; framed as a current arbitrage window. (speculation/opinion)

### Paid ads

- Core loop: organic virality is spiky (viral for months, then decays); once a high-view organic video tails off, convert it into a paid ad targeted at the right age group/country to flatten the download valleys ("making the valley and peak a little flatter"). Testing happens free via organic; winners become ad creative, lowering paid budget. (claimed)
- Ad construction: first half = the original viral organic video; last ~15 seconds appended with app demo + CTA. CTA verbiage kept organic-flavored ("download now to flex on your friends," not "download now to be successful"); sometimes no modification at all. (claimed)

### Trial & onboarding

- Onboarding is revised roughly every quarter. Fintech conversion is inherently hard: KYC + personal info + real money → users are cautious vs. a homework-AI app with an obvious instant reason to sign up. (claimed)
- Onboarding sequence: (1) urgency/FOMO — invest $X at age 18 → $1M by 65, wait until 30 → only ~$340K; (2) feature explanation with animation/color changes to hold attention (memeified stocks: Netflix, Tesla); (3) trust — press logos (Forbes? — transcribed "Fox", uncertain — and Yahoo), SIPC backing, YC; (4) customer reviews; (5) paywall. CTA button copy being tested: "Continue" → "Let's go" for the college demographic. (claimed)
- Signup bait: "up to $75" free stock. Fine print: actual immediate grant is ~$2–$4 in fractional stock; the rest is earnable via in-game currency → loot boxes → fractional shares. Free stock (not cash) chosen deliberately to seed a long-term-growth mindset. Tested the headline number at $40 / $60 / $75 — impact "isn't as much"; currently running $75. Same subscription funnel unchanged for 2–3 quarters. (claimed)
- A/B result 1: splitting one dense info screen into ~5 tappable story-style screens lifted the download→add-personal-info step materially ("pretty big change") — counterintuitive vs. per-tap-dropoff intuition; users skim word-heavy screens and miss the point. Instagram-story muscle memory (tap right side to advance) carries them through. (claimed)
- A/B result 2: REMOVING the "Get started" button from those screens worked better than having it — with no escape hatch, users actually read. (claimed)
- Pricing A/B testing largely avoided: with a large existing user base, price experiments create payment discrepancies between cohorts. (claimed)

- Advice for a $10K starting budget, first 2–3 months: (1) AI video generation (Veo 2/Veo 3 — transcribed "V2/V3"; prompt via GPT → generate in Veo/"journey" [likely Midjourney, uncertain]) to scale one account to virality cheaply — Veo 3 released ~May 20, cheapest tier "I think ~$20/month" (explicitly unsure); (2) recruit 2–3 small creators without an agency via Sidesshift (transcribed "sides shift", uncertain — Gen Z UGC marketplace) and Handshake (college students), later scale to 5. Research stack: SpyTok (transcribed "spy talk", uncertain) viral-trend tracker for finding formats to copy. (claimed/advice)

Also mentions AI "satisfying videos" as a heard-of success (secondhand). (claimed)
- Consistency is the meta-advice: minimum 90 days to make any video go viral; Jay himself is currently in a ~60-day dry spell with no viral hits. (claimed/first-party candor)

### Retention & product

- The bait-and-educate funnel is defended: get-rich-quick hooks are "borderline" ethically but the in-app experience teaches compounding/long-term investing, and retention "actually really worked well" despite the aggressive ad angle. PMF framed as the overlooked half: "if the product doesn't stick with the users... marketing" doesn't save you. (claimed, no retention numbers given)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Internal content team + AI-content agency + comment team + creator-management agency; tools: HeyGen (AI avatar video), SpyTok (trend research), Sidesshift + Handshake (creator recruiting), Veo 2/3 + GPT prompting (generation), "viral TikTok trackers" from the host's toolset. (claimed)

## Deal structures

- $1,000/month flat retainer + $2–$4 CPM bonus unlocking at 10K / 50K / 100K views per video.
- Alternative: $500/month retainer for 60 videos/month + higher (unspecified) CPM.
- 60 videos/month (2/day) is the standard volume expectation; creators run 1–2 fresh accounts.
- Per-video payout cap $1,000 (~1M views).
- CPM eligibility floor scales with creator size: 10K views (brand-new accounts) vs 100K views (5–10K-follower creators).

## Contrarian positions

- Pay small/unknown creators monthly retainers for volume rather than paying established influencers for reach; make them build brand-new accounts from zero.
- More onboarding screens beat fewer: 1 screen → ~5 story-style screens lifted conversion; and removing the advance button beat having one.
- Deliberately DON'T price-test once you have scale (cohort payment discrepancies outweigh learning).
- Views below 100K are framed as near-worthless for conversion — mid-tier view bonuses are creator-psychology spend, not marketing spend.
- Ads should look like organic content, not follow classic direct-response frameworks; soft CTAs in comments beat in-video CTAs for organic.
- Marketing angle can borrow a cultural movement (Bible, therapy, self-care) without the product being for that audience at all.

## Crave transfer

The most transferable asset is the deal-structure blueprint: $500–$1,000/mo retainer + $2–$4 CPM with tiered view floors is a concrete, bootstrappable template for Austin food-content micro-creators, and the "recruit taste, not followings; fresh accounts; 2 posts/day; propagate winners across accounts" engine maps cleanly to faceless local-food content (ranked-list carousels of Austin dishes are a natural fit for the carousel wave he cites). The onboarding findings (story-style multi-screen with no skip button; urgency framing; trust stack before paywall) are directly relevant to Crave's hard paywall and are cheap to test. Discount heavily: Bloom's numbers are unverified founder claims on a sponsor's channel inside a mutual-promotion loop (Consumer Club, Superwall, referred agencies), and Bloom is a national-audience fintech with a free-stock bribe — Crave is single-city, so raw view volume mostly reaches people outside Austin, which breaks his own "views below 100K don't convert" economics even harder; geo-relevant reach, not virality, is Crave's constraint. His paid-ads recycling loop assumes an ad budget Crave doesn't have at launch, and the "get-rich" bait-then-educate funnel has no clean analog for a utility app — Crave's equivalent hook is specificity ("the single best dish in Austin per Reddit"), not aspiration.
