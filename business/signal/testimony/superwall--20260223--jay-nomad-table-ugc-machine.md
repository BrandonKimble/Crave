## Arc

Jay is a bootstrapped solo founder who quit big tech (~3 years there, quit ~September, ~1 year before this pod) to build Nomad Table, a social app for solo travelers to make activities and meet nearby travelers. He grew it to 1M+ downloads and ~$65k/mo almost entirely via organic TikTok/Instagram UGC: he personally learned to go viral first, then scaled the proven formats to a ~60-70 creator network he manages alone on performance (CPM) deals. The video exists as a Superwall-sponsored playbook episode: test formats yourself, use 3-second watch-through as the virality predictor, then scale creators.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Nomad Table monetizes via subscriptions: weekly, monthly, annual tiers exist (host lists them; Jay confirms "yeah, totally"). Host says "annual is like 69.99 a month or a year" — garbled; $69.99/yr is the plausible reading but UNCERTAIN. (claimed)
- Social app ⇒ ARPU "definitely going to be a lot lower than a classic hard paywall app" — known going in. (claimed)
- Refuses in-app ads despite believing they'd take him "easily over 100k a month" — protecting quality, "trying to build a generational kind of thing." (speculation, his own counterfactual)
- Host's observation: apps with this many downloads "tend to have a bit more" revenue; Jay agrees 1M downloads → $65k/mo is on the low side for the download count. (claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Scale: ~60 creators (later "60 to 70"), 44M views last month, 34M the month before ("roughly 30 to 40 million views a month"). Posting cadence: once/day per account, TikTok + Instagram. (claimed; view dashboards screen-shared with host)
- Traffic mix: "mostly UGC"; word of mouth now 10-15% of new users (his estimate from onboarding attribution); the rest essentially all UGC. Zero paid ads. (claimed)
- Sequencing: he posted solo for a long time, got "the first 50 to 100k downloads from my own content," proved formats go viral repeatedly, THEN hired creators (first hires ~April, ~7 months after quitting). "If I just hire them now, there's no point cuz they're not going to know what to do." (claimed)
- He still posts alongside creators and has been the top-views creator in his own network "for the past 2-3 months." Founder-in-the-trenches makes creators take feedback better. (claimed)
- He originates all ideas/formats; creators mostly stick to his formats with "some freedom" (freedom-videos usually underperform, occasionally hit; he accepts it for morale). All flopping/testing happens on his accounts; creators only remake proven winners. (claimed)
- THE metric: percent watching past 3 seconds (Instagram exposes it; unsure about TikTok). A good format hits 75-80% consistently; all his good formats are ≥70%. Average watch time on his videos: ~6-10 seconds. If 75-80% is hit consistently, "it's going to pretty much for sure go viral eventually — just a matter of time." (claimed, first-party analytics described)
- View floor for a good format: at least 4-5k views per video consistently on TikTok; "if you're getting sub-50 [views], it's probably cooked" — kill the format. (claimed)
- Virality hit rate: ~1 viral video/month per creator posting daily (his own accounts and most creators; some months 3-4). He once posted a month of videos all at 81% 3-sec watch-through and NOTHING went viral — virality itself is luck; the network distributes the luck ("with that many people it's pretty hard to not go viral once a week"). (claimed)
- Baseline math: 60-70 creators × ~5k views/day each is a large steady download stream even with zero virals. (claimed)
- Longevity: super-viral videos keep pulling views "for months," one for "literally a whole year" — evergreen marketing assets, like SEO articles. (claimed)
- Anti-pattern — the "view trap": clickbait hostel-zoom hooks got 300k views but only 174 likes / 8 comments ("horrible" engagement); algorithm rewards watch time past the hook, but undelivered clickbait doesn't convert. He posted that format "hundreds of times" before abandoning it (also unscalable — required him physically staying in hostels). (first-party, shown on screen)
- Hook craft: his first viral video (made pre-launch, app in pre-order) used a borrowed cross-niche hook — "I've been solo traveling for 2 years and now I find out about this" + facepalm — straight into a phone-on-table product demo. "Very versatile hook… probably works for any niche." (claimed)
- Specific details ("the most unsocial hostel in Thailand") make videos feel real/organic; could have gone further and named the hostel. (claimed)
- A good TEXT hook works "across the board" for a bunch of creators regardless of acting quality; given enough reps "pretty much everyone will get a big video with it." Reaction gimmicks (drinking a drink, tying a shoe, holding an object) — he tried them, stopped, "didn't see them making that big of an impact." (claimed)
- Creator-network example video: same format as his, 74,000 likes and 30,000 bookmarks; hook "imagine being scared of solo traveling when this literally exists." Had ~10 creators at that point (summer of prior year). (claimed, shown)
- One old account got shadow-banned / reach "killed" — he abandoned it and moved on. (claimed)
- Early content was heavily founder-led (face in everything, story/talking videos, public milestone sharing) → built an audience that "wanted me to win"; he credits early retention partly to that. Stopped for lack of time; says he should restart. (claimed)

### UGC & creator deals

- Comp model: "mostly all performance based on just views" (CPM), paid monthly. (claimed)
- Benchmark: $1-2 per 1,000 views is good/standard; involves negotiation. Highest competing offer he's seen in his DMs: $3 CPM (tempting enough he considered creating for them). Creators with multiple clients confirm many apps pay in his range. (claimed/secondhand for other apps)
- "At that CPM you're still way better than any paid ads by a long shot." (claimed)
- To get creators onto performance deals you must PROVE the app goes viral (show accounts/videos, ideally your own: "I've done this format eight times, it's gone viral a bunch"); a brand-new app with no viral proof must use retainers first. (claimed)
- No payout cap; his highest single-creator monthly payout ~$8,000 ≈ 8M views (possibly from ONE video). He suggests others might cap CPM payouts, but he doesn't — fairness/relationship. (claimed)
- Trailing views: he pays on views to old posts for ~3 months after posting; says most people don't. (claimed)
- Management: all in-house, solo — a giant WhatsApp group plus 1-on-1s; describes spending "most of my time" on creator feedback/relationships. "They are my distribution." (claimed)

### Paid ads

- Zero paid ads. Bearish on paid for social apps: low ARPU means paid can't pencil "even if you have a crazy good ad" with very low cost per download; acceptable only briefly to solve a cold-start, never a long-term strategy — "it's not going to make you money ever" for social. (claimed/speculation)
- Concedes paid is predictable (known CPM in → known volume out) while organic is uncertain; paid has public playbooks, UGC has "no rulebook… random podcasts, random tweets." High-ARPU categories (host cites betting apps) DO need ads to scale. (claimed)

### Launch & sequencing

- Built a waitlist pre-launch: ~12-15k signups; ~6k actually got the app on day one. In hindsight "not that many for the whole world," but the app works with as few as 2 people in a place; Barcelona had ~20 users day one, big cities 10+. (claimed)
- No location-based marketing — travelers move, so a city-by-city dating-app playbook is useless for him ("I could solve one location, but after a month it'll be gone"); otherwise markets like a single-player app, "whatever the algo takes it, that's where it goes." (claimed)
- Recommends Andrew Chen's "The Cold Start Problem" for social founders. (claimed)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Solo founder, no team, no VC, fully bootstrapped; 3 years of big-tech savings covered early unprofitable months ("some months in the red, nothing crazy"); profitable now. Big-tech boomerang policy (return within a year without re-interview) de-risked quitting. (claimed)
- Timeline: idea on a 3-week PTO solo trip last summer → quit ~1 week after returning (~September) → saw the Greg Eisenberg/Joseph Choi video ~November → first creator hires ~April. (claimed)

## Deal structures

- Creator CPM deals: $1-2 per 1,000 views, performance-only (no retainer once viral proof exists), paid monthly, views on old posts tracked and paid for ~3 months post-publish, NO payout cap. Highest observed month: ~$8,000 to one creator for ~8M views. Market ceiling seen: $3 CPM offers from other apps. Retainers recommended only for apps with no viral proof yet. Creators mostly required to reuse founder-proven formats.

## Contrarian positions

- Views are a trap: 300k views with 174 likes is failure; he abandoned his highest-view format. The predictive metric is 3-second watch-through (75-80%), not views or likes.
- TikTok is a science, not a lottery — a good text hook works across almost all creators — BUT which specific good video goes viral IS luck; the network exists to farm that luck at volume.
- Founder should be the best creator in his own network and keep posting; most founders outsource content entirely.
- Paid ads are structurally wrong for low-ARPU apps, full stop — against the "you need ads to scale" consensus (which he grants for high-ARPU niches).
- Pays trailing views for 3 months and refuses payout caps — deliberately more generous than the standard he says others follow.
- Deliberately leaves ~$35k+/mo on the table (no ads in-app) to protect product quality.

## Crave transfer

The mechanics transfer better than the economics: the test-formats-yourself-before-hiring-creators sequencing, the 3-second watch-through gate (75-80%), the kill-below-baseline discipline, and $1-2 CPM performance deals (only after Brandon has personal viral proof; retainers otherwise) are directly usable and cheap to attempt for Crave's no-ad-budget launch. But Nomad Table is a global-audience, low-ARPU social app whose algorithm can send viewers anywhere — Crave is Austin-only at launch, and Jay explicitly does NO location-based marketing because his users move; Crave's core problem is the inverse (geo-concentrated demand), so his distribution math (60 creators × global views) doesn't map — Crave needs Austin-local creators or geo-resonant hooks, a channel this transcript says nothing about. His anti-paid-ads stance rests on low ARPU; Crave's $7.99/mo hard paywall is the opposite economics, so that stance should not be imported. His waitlist→critical-mass launch is for network-effect apps; Crave's data-density value exists on day one without users, so the lesson reduces to "founder-led public building buys forgiveness," which does transfer. Standard survivorship caveats: one winner, self-reported numbers, on a sponsor's podcast.
