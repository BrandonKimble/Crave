## Arc

Podcast interview: host talks to Canyon, co-founder of Sideshift, a marketplace of 150k college students doing UGC work. Canyon lays out the "high-volume organic" playbook — hire cheap student creators to post daily TikToks from fresh accounts — with screen-shared case studies (Sideshift itself, solo-founder AI photo app Photogenic, app studio Vert Ventures). The video exists to promote Sideshift, Superwall (sponsor), and the host's Consumer Club + SpyTalk tool.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Sideshift itself grew from ~10,000 to 150,000+ active job seekers in 3–4 months using the high-volume organic playbook, on ~$10k total spend ("customer acquisition cost that's pennies"). (claimed, own numbers, screen-shared)
- Sideshift's last campaign: ~16M views from a ~300-post campaign (Canyon says "maybe closer to 20[M]" — number uncertain, dates on the dashboard admittedly off), roughly half of their total views. 5 creators, most posting 2x/day. (claimed, dashboard shown)
- It took 2–3 weeks of posting before finding a winning format; once found, all 5 creators pumped it out "every other day." Hit videos: one 2M-view, one 5M-view video, plus 100k-view videos stacking up. (claimed)
- One winning-format creator had under 2,000 followers (1,900) and started from zero — fresh accounts work; follower count doesn't matter, "algorithms reward quality content." (claimed)
- A slideshow post naming the app only on the last slide got 84,000 views; "few hundred thousand views… converts insanely well" for a subtle plug format. (claimed)
- Recommended pay: hybrid model — **$15–$20 per video plus a base retainer**, with "pretty generous performance bonuses" to align incentives (bonuses also incentivize post-publish comment engagement). Elsewhere ballparked as "$20 or so per video." (claimed, their standard recommendation)
- Photogenic case: solo founder, first-ever organic attempt, posted the job on Sideshift, got ~600 applicants (also said "few hundred"), hired ONE creator after vetting her personal TikTok for prior virality. Result: **46 posts → 35M total views** ("almost a million views per post"). (secondhand via Canyon)
- Photogenic saw ~10x growth in weekly downloads. (secondhand)
- Photogenic's App Store page conversion went from **5–10% (1-in-10 to 1-in-20) to over 50%** after organic — thesis: hearing about a product via organic content confers credibility that paid ads can't; viewers who close TikTok and search the App Store are pre-committed. (secondhand; host endorses the mechanism)
- The winning Photogenic content was AI failure cases ("third leg sticking out"), not polished best-output demos — the founder's ideas "performed terribly"; letting the creator run fully worked. (secondhand)
- Sideshift went in person to 25 college campuses to onboard its first 10–20k users. (claimed)
- College students are the arbitrage: hungry, cheap, no influencer-scale expectations; frame the gig as career-track (creator → strategist → growth role), "more than just a transaction." (claimed/opinion)
- Sideshift had to TURN OFF its funnels mid-May: ~50,000 new creators joined in 1–2 weeks, businesses were getting ~3,000 applicants per posting, product broke (one engineer at the time). (claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Playbook: fresh TikTok accounts, 3–5 day warm-up acting like a human in the target niche (engage naturally; anti-bot detection improves "every single month"); creator posts daily or 2x/day; expect weeks of 100–1,000-view videos before a breakout; when one video pops the whole account flywheel lifts.
- Patience framing: "sometimes it takes 300 posts before you find that winning format." Most people quit channels too early; UGC's advantage is cheap reps.
- Formats that work: macro-economy/job-market pain content (denied from jobs despite a master's degree), slideshows with the app named on the last slide, controversy or broad resonance; subtle product drops, not "screaming your product."
- Comment-section engineering: creator must engage comments aggressively; plug the product when people ask ("this is why I only use Sideshift") — that's where views convert to users.
- Creator brief (their template): what the company does in your own words + Loom walkthroughs; target audience/personas (niche focus — "targeted at anyone" content performs bland); links to prior viral videos as starting formats; competitor/adjacent-niche viral formats to transfer; a few hook ideas (optional); pay terms; rules (mention us, don't mention competitors); optionally ownership/usage rights via Google Drive upload. Keep it SHORT — a 50-page brief gets skipped entirely.
- Vet creators by their personal TikTok's prior virality and by responsiveness/strategic hunger in conversation; calibrate hands-on vs. hands-off per creator.
- Vert Ventures (Astra astrology + Haven Bible study, both claimed top-5 in category): finds winning formats in organic then "sparks them into paid" — organic as creative-testing lab for paid. Many creators across ages/genders/format styles simultaneously. (secondhand)

### Paid ads

- Organic-content credibility beats labeled ads on landing/App Store conversion (the 5–10% → 50%+ Photogenic claim). Host: ad-labeled traffic converts worse than search-initiated App Store visits.
- For fast validation via paid: hire creators week-to-week, produce 5–10 formats, run them as paid ads for quick convert/no-convert feedback.
- Guest predicts influencer and micro-influencer pricing converges downward toward UGC rates; big-brand influencer spend is brand awareness with "no strong ROI"; UGC's cheaper impressions are why startups and mid/late-stage growth companies are shifting.

### Launch & sequencing

- UGC as cheap market validation: hire a couple of creators, pump out 30–50 videos, watch whether downloads → retention → payment; ~2 weeks is a sufficient organic test window (shorter windows lack data; paid can test in a week). If two consecutive 2-week iterations show nothing, question the product thesis.
- App studios fundraise on this: test 10–20 products via UGC, double down on the 2–3 that stick; teams shipping a product every week and running a month of distribution on each.

### Retention & product

- Photogenic's user influx itself improved the product loop — more users → environment that "increased retention" and created super users. (secondhand, vague)
- Validation question hierarchy: downloads are cheap; the real signals are "will they stay… will any of them actually pay."

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Sideshift's entire acquisition spend across the board: ~$10k for the 10k→150k growth. (claimed)
- Per-video creator cost $15–20 + retainer + bonuses (see deals).
- SpyTalk (host's own product): keyword-searchable database of winning UGC formats per niche; born as a white-label research tool from a consultant who's done "millions of views" for clients. (host self-promo)

## Deal structures

- Standard Sideshift-recommended creator deal: **$15–$20 flat per video + base retainer + generous performance bonuses** (bonus terms unspecified). Hybrid model rationale: retainer attracts quality, bonuses align incentives incl. comment engagement.
- Ownership/usage rights: optional; some clients require uploads to Google Drive to secure usage rights.
- Testing cadence deals: week-to-week creator hires for paid-ad creative testing; ~2-week minimum engagement for organic tests.
- No rev-share, exclusivity, or per-view rate figures given.

## Contrarian positions

- Follower count is irrelevant; fresh zero-follower accounts can hit millions of views — against the influencer-marketing default.
- Organic UGC beats paid ads on ROI for consumer apps ("leaps and bounds more effective than even paid ads") — and influencer/micro-influencer marketing has "no strong ROI" beyond brand awareness.
- Showing your product FAILING (AI glitch content) can outperform polished demos.
- Don't give creators much direction: founder-supplied ideas "performed terribly"; creative freedom + a one-page brief beats a 50-page brief.
- Expect and accept 2–3+ weeks (up to 300 posts) of near-zero results — most advice treats a dead channel after 2 weeks as falsified; here it's the entry fee.

## Crave transfer

The channel-level claim — cheap college-student UGC on fresh TikTok accounts, $15–20/video + retainer, weeks of patience until a format hits — is the most directly transferable idea here for a zero-ad-budget Austin launch, and Austin's food-TikTok niche is dense with exactly the "adjacent viral formats" this brief methodology feeds on. But every scale number is suspect for Crave: the case studies are visual-wow (AI photos) or broad-pain (job market) categories with national audiences; Crave is a geo-fenced utility, so a 35M-view breakout is nearly impossible and mostly worthless (views outside Austin don't convert), meaning the "300 posts to find a format" math must be recomputed against a far smaller relevant audience. The 5–10%→50% App Store conversion claim is secondhand, unverified, and confounded (search-initiated traffic always converts better) — treat it as directional, not a number. Biggest incentive distortion: the guest sells the creator marketplace and the host sells the research tool and the sponsor sells paywalls; nobody in this video has ever mentioned a paying user or a dollar of revenue — downloads and views are the only currency, which is exactly the metric Crave's hard paywall makes least relevant.
