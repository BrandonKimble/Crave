## Arc

Marcus Burke is a German performance marketer with 14 years running Meta ads for apps — gaming (InnoGames, era of Clash of Clans/King/Supercell-scale spend) then subscription apps (Blinkist, StudySmarter, Tandem), now a freelance consultant for subscription apps. The host frames the thesis up front: subscription apps "need to run meta ads to grow at scale." The episode is Marcus's full system: research-driven creative (Reddit + Claude), app-vs-web ad format differences, AI generation stack, and his account-structure/placement-analysis framework. Headline claims: scaled an app to $2M/month and one ad format that crossed $1M in spend.

## Claims

### Paid ads

- Thesis (host): subscription apps need Meta ads to scale; not understanding app-specific creative formats "leaves six figures a year on the table." (speculation/positioning)
- Marcus scaled an app to $2M/month and his best single ad crossed ~$1M lifetime spend ("probably well above like a million dollars") across all its iterations. (claimed)
- The million-dollar sleep-app ad: 5-second skit hook ("I showed my husband the [Mogate — name uncertain] app and he passed out instead of picking up the kids") → glowing green orb + green noise → benefit/feature sentences every few seconds. ~100 versions of that ad; ~400 total iterations of the concept (later says "400 500 iterations"); variants for tinnitus-masking sub-audience. (claimed, first-party)
- App ads must PREPARE users that they'll land on the App Store or install rate suffers: say "app" in copy or show an app demo. D2C-ecom talking-head format is a poor fit for apps. (first-party practitioner claim)
- App ads are soft-CTA and emotional; the SALE happens in the app's long onboarding, not the ad. Web-funnel ads can be pure top-funnel content (Nibble geopolitics example — "could be an advertisement for anything") because the web funnel does the selling.
- Text-wall format: ~10-second, 3-clip video, on-screen text block drives conversion; language mined from Reddit so the audience "feels seen" ("rewire toxic attachments," "emotional regulation"). Wellness self-diagnosis buzzwords cycle: ADHD (past wave) → anxious attachment, overthinking (current).
- Targeting IS creative now: run broadly targeted adsets (US, 18+); specific audience-language in the ad lets the algorithm find the audience. Generic copy → broad low-converting delivery. Narrow manual targeting (e.g. 45-50 male NY) → very high CPM premium.
- Media/placement mechanics: statics deliver poorly in app-promotion campaign type but fine on web campaigns; scalable placements = FB/IG feed, reels, stories. Facebook feed = older users, IG reels = younger. Formula: message + media + content type × funnel = audience.
- Age economics: 18-24 can't afford ~$70/yr plans; trial-optimized campaigns buy cheap young trials that convert worst; 65+ cost per trial can be ~100% higher but converts much better ("inverse pattern"). Example account: 25-34 the most expensive cost-per-target bucket. Fix: (a) Meta value rules — tell Meta 45+ is 30-40% more valuable, bid up; (b) make creative that attracts older users. Trial event fires day 0 but trial→paid resolves ~day 7 — too late for the algorithm to learn from, which is why trial optimization misleads (predates ATT).
- Account structure: cluster creatives into adsets by media type (static vs video = "completely different things") × style (text walls / memes / UGC) × message (each pain-point audience its own adset); set budgets at ADSET level or Meta concentrates ~80-90% of spend on its single favorite. Memes bring lowest-quality traffic → require them at ~half the cost-per-target. Goal is scale across ALL audiences, not Meta's single winner.
- Placement-composition chart: pull ads that are below cost-per-target (e.g. $60 target; best ads $32-35) but spend 40-50% (one case 90%) on an outlier placement like FB feed into their own adset, broad targeting, to scale that placement/audience.
- Bootstrapped/low-budget start: throw everything into ONE adset, see what wins, iterate weekly; multiple adsets require 2-4× budget and only make sense once near break-even ("compared to when you're losing 30-40% on each"). VC-backed can split adsets early to buy learning speed. (first-party)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Organic-virality logic is the OPPOSITE of ad logic: organic must be broad and non-salesy to earn distribution; niche-targeted content won't get platform-pushed to millions. Selling in organic is subtle — app shown on screen without a CTA, people ask for the name in comments (which itself drives engagement).
- Visual research: train your IG/TikTok algorithm on the niche (~5 minutes of engagement → ~80% niche content in feed); look for recurring visual language ("every third piece of content looks like that"), not just viral outliers; comment sections are extra language data.

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Research pipeline: Reddit threads (anonymous → honest language, engagement = importance signal) + app reviews + competitor reviews → fed to Claude → per-sub-audience context files (e.g. 5 audiences) + a product context file (features, social proof) → prompt for e.g. 3 distinct text hooks, or feed a competitor text wall as a template. "The key is context" — default LLM copywriting is generic "marketing blah."
- AI generation: "99%" of client ad creative now AI-made via freelancers. Not yet cheaper than requesting content from ongoing creator relationships ("incredibly cheap") — the bet is controllable/testable variables and that in ~half a year it's cheap; dream = closed loop where tagged variables + ad-account performance make the system self-learning.
- Tool stack: Higgsfield (captioned "Hicksfield" — uncertain) as model aggregator (one subscription, all models); video models: Seedance 2 (captioned "Cance 2," ByteDance, good at authentic UGC style — likely TikTok-trained), Veo 3, Kling; image: GPT Image 2 (strong text consistency) vs Google Nano Banana (weaker on fine print). Workflow: perfect the scene as a cheap image first, then use it as the video model's reference frame — direct video prompting burns credits. Node-based workflow tools Weave (acquired by Figma — claimed) and Flora automate an established format (define prompt structure per scene, feed variables, "hit play" for iterations).
- User interviews: at a previous company every UA-team member did one user interview per week; much of their ad-copy understanding came from these. (first-party)

- Request AGE in onboarding so trial→paid can be broken down by age bucket and fed back into bidding decisions.

## Contrarian positions

- High-performing app ads look nothing like polished UGC/organic-style creative people assume converts — his $1M ad is a mundane skit + glowing orb, "barely shows the actual app."
- Don't hide that you're an app — deliberately break the native-content illusion so the App Store redirect doesn't kill install rate (opposite of the "make ads indistinguishable from content" school).
- "Creative is the new targeting" is too simplified — placement mix, media type, and message each independently steer audience; media buying structure still matters (vs the common "media buying is dead, only creative matters" take, which the host himself voices).
- Cheapest cost-per-trial is actively bad — deliberately bid UP on expensive older audiences and demand memes/cheap formats at half the target instead of letting Meta chase cheap trials.
- Don't chase week-to-week trends when small; multi-year identity waves (ADHD → anxious attachment/overthinking) are the durable layer.

## Crave transfer

The load-bearing frame — paid Meta ads as the necessary growth engine — conflicts directly with Crave's no-ad-budget launch and is delivered on a paywall-vendor's podcast by an ads consultant; treat the "you need ads" thesis as incentive-distorted, not evidence. What transfers regardless of channel: the Reddit→Claude language-mining pipeline is literally Crave's existing core competency (Reddit-mined dish testimony) repointed at marketing copy — mine Austin food subreddits for the vivid phrases people use about finding food, and write App Store/organic/creator copy in that language; "prepare people they're landing on the App Store" applies to any creator content; and the age-economics warning (young users don't pay ~$70/yr; Crave is $39.99/yr hard-paywall) argues for creative and channels that skew 25+ with income. His organic-content observation cuts against Crave, though: organic virality requires broad, non-salesy content, which is in tension with a single-city utility app — a niche-geo product gets little algorithmic push, so expect creator/UGC reach in Austin to behave more like his "niche adset" than like viral organic. His examples are emotional-identity wellness apps (self-diagnosis buzzwords); Crave is utility/discovery — the emotional text-wall playbook transfers weakly, but "write in the audience's own words about the pain" (endless where-to-eat scrolling, tourist-trap misses) transfers fully.
