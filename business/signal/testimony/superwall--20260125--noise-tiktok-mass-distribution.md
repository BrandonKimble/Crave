## Arc

Nick Weber co-founded Noise, a platform where ~50,000 people mass-post brand-supplied TikTok slideshows and UGC, paid per view. It began as an internal growth hack for his prior app (Playbite): hand 30 engaged users Canva slideshows to post; it worked so well they productized it. Founders on the platform claim 2.5B views in the past year; Noise grew itself using its own tool. The video exists because the Superwall podcast host had a prior guest (Kyle) rave about Noise, and it doubles as a pitch for the platform.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Noise creators are paid **per view (CPM)** — no retainers, no upfront fees. "We're not here to charge somebody $15, $20,000 ahead of time to go make content for you." (claimed)
- Brands set the CPM themselves, derived from their **target cost-per-install**: "what are you trying to target for a download? … that's usually what we set the CPMs at." (first-party as platform operator)
- Noise's own installs via its own platform average **~$0.30 per install**; one viral washing-dishes campaign hit **$0.08 installs for ~3 days** and pushed Noise to **#7 in its category**. (claimed, no attribution method shown)
- Contrast baseline he cites for the "normal way": pay a creator **$200–$500/month to post ~20 slideshows a month**, hit-or-miss. (secondhand/market norm claim, stated by host)
- Scale numbers: **50,000 people** posting; **~100,000 pieces of content per day** "at least"; **hundreds of thousands of posts** doing 500–2,000 views a pop; platform founders did **2.5B views in the last year** (they "weren't keeping track" and were surprised). (claimed)
- Creators don't invent formats: platform supplies exact assets, captions, descriptions, hashtags, auto-randomized so every post is unique. "Don't make your creators think about how to create this content." (first-party)
- Origin story: took **30 most-engaged users** of their prior app, paid them to post Canva slideshows; **35 people** posting Brawl Stars slideshows with an explicit "Playbite's the fastest way to get gems" CTA outperformed all expectations. (first-party)
- Some Noise users claim **$7,000 in a week** earned posting; but advertising that number reads as scam — **$20/day → "$500/month"** framing converts because it anchors against a car payment ("my car payment is like $498 a month"). (claimed)

### Content strategy (organic)

- **Two-pronged playbook run simultaneously**: (1) high-effort UGC hunting viral hits with a SOFT call-to-action; (2) thousands of cheap slideshows with a HARD CTA functioning "like a retargeting ad" for people who saw the viral video. Running both together "works great." (first-party)
- "Shots on goal" doctrine: ~100k posts/day guarantees something hits; failures return fast feedback via analytics. (first-party)
- Hook micro-variations swing outcomes 1,000 → 10M views. Concrete A/B: opening text **"don't get a second job" went viral; "everybody needs a side hustle" flopped** — same video otherwise. Psychology: don't tell people what to do. (first-party anecdote)
- **Eggs theory**: slideshows whose first image contained eggs consistently outperformed in healthy-eating content — instantly understood, healthy, cheap ("the last election was based on the prices of egg cost"). Hook image = the lever. Images were AI-generated, Pinterest-style aesthetic. (first-party pattern claim)
- Action-in-video mattered: **washing dishes** worked; vacuuming and pouring coffee with the same caption did not. Had "four, five, six" variants of the dishes video all over **1M views** each. (first-party)
- Viral format sourcing = **copying, not creativity**: find what already went viral, slot your app in. "Great artists steal." Hit **#5 once and #7 twice** in their category this way. (first-party)
- **Reddit as format mine**: popular subreddits ≈ proven content demand (e.g. r/RoastMe → "AI roasts you" TikTok format). Lingo Pingo example: creators handed a "crying at Google Translate pronunciation" format; woke up to **~20M views** next day; format "ripped all through October and November." (first-party/claimed)
- **Comment seeding at scale**: super-implicit videos that never mention the app (e.g. "mandatory Secret Santa, $60 requirement" fishing for comments) + plugged-in users seeding comments. Host's own seeded comment got **1,686 likes**, which Nick estimates drove "at least a thousand" downloads or branded searches. That campaign = the **#5-in-category** hit. (first-party, download estimate is speculation)
- Own dashboard shown: **54M views in last 30 days** across TikTok/Instagram/YouTube; slideshows alone only **2M** of that (they're the retargeting layer, not the viral layer); UGC hits at 3.8M, 5M, 2M, 2M, 1.6M views. One current video: **22M views in 4 days**. Playbook "how are we making money by Christmas": **9M views**. (first-party dashboard, on-screen)
- Kill criteria: a playbook with **446 posts in 1–2 weeks and ~200k total views** = didn't hit → pause it, slot in a new one. Watch window: **1–3 weeks** of tweaking before giving up on a format. (first-party)
- Craft notes: have the user paste caption text inside TikTok rather than baking it into the image (better authenticity signal to the algorithm — his theory); low-effort-looking content outperforms ("the ones that you spend the least amount of time on … always go viral"); AI (ChatGPT) used for caption variations but current AI "isn't quite good enough to feel that organic" — best results are human-curated; AI-prompted posts have still done 1–2M views. (first-party/speculation mix)
- Campaign structure deliberately mirrors Meta ads: campaigns → playbooks (≈ ad sets) → posts. (first-party)
- Duolingo referenced: an article said their numbers fell when they stopped unhinged trend content — repeated impressions in trending formats keep converting/reactivating even for a universally known brand. (secondhand)

### Paid ads

- Positioning claim: TikTok organic mass-posting is "the alternative to Meta ads" — Meta ads "don't work the way people think" and are "super expensive"; TikTok is "the best distribution system at cost." (claimed, vendor-interested)
- TikTok's interest-graph serving makes organic posts function like targeted ads: make your product relevant to existing content interest and it "converts like absolute gangbusters." (claimed)

### Launch & sequencing

- Aha moment: **July 2024**, first pseudo-promotional slideshow for their own app did **20,000 views in a week** → asked other app founders to try it → platform took off. Nick had never posted on TikTok before July 2024. (first-party)
- "Do things that don't scale, then scale them": Canva-slideshow phase → fully automated variation-serving in-app. (first-party)
- His personal experiment account: first 6 posts "ripped right out of the gate," **887K views on the first post**; one hook image did **>5M views** posted a few times. (claimed)

### Retention & product

- (For the Noise creator side) instant per-view payment feedback is the creator retention hook. (first-party, thin)

### Other — algorithm theories

- **Hot take: videos under 500 views still convert.** TikTok routes even tiny-reach posts to the right 500 people; 1,000 videos × 1,000 views = a million targeted views that produce downloads; has screenshots of "what app is that?" comments on 200-view videos; "plenty of math to prove that's true" (math not shown). (claimed)
- **Hot take: TikTok has a daily views "budget" per content space.** Adding 5,000 extra videos/day in a niche spreads the same total views thinner rather than creating new demand; flooding a trend redistributes rather than multiplies views — trend TAM is fixed, so 1,000×1k views ≈ 1×1M views in reach terms. (speculation, pattern-observed)
- **Hot take: anyone can learn to go viral** — it's science/repetition, not innate creativity; proven on himself, team, and creators. (claimed)

## Deal structures

- Noise: brands pay creators **per view (CPM)**; brand sets the CPM keyed to its target CPI; no retainers, no upfront production fees. Contrasted against agency norm of **$15k–$20k upfront** and creator-retainer norm of **$200–$500/month for ~20 slideshows/month**. Creator-side earnings anecdotes: **$20/night** ("adds up to an extra $500/month" — this is the ad copy, not verified payouts) up to a claimed **$7,000/week** for top users. No rev-share, exclusivity, or bonus terms discussed.

## Contrarian positions

- Virality is science, not art — creativity is nearly irrelevant; copying proven formats beats inventing.
- Mass low-effort slideshow flooding (100k posts/day) beats the standard "5–10 creators posting twice a day" playbook.
- Sub-500-view videos convert and are worth producing at scale — against the consensus that only viral hits matter.
- Organic TikTok mass-posting is a _replacement_ for Meta ads, not a complement.
- Showing income receipts is counterproductive — small believable numbers ($20/day) convert better than true large ones ($7k/week).
- Low production effort outperforms polish; AI-generated hook images are fine.

## Crave transfer

Category and incentive mismatches are big: Nick sells the platform he's describing, every cost number ($0.30/$0.08 installs) is vendor-self-reported with no attribution shown, and his showcase apps (money-making side-hustle app, AI roast app) have inherently viral "the app IS the content" hooks that a local food-discovery utility does not. The genuinely transferable ideas: (1) the two-pronged soft-viral + hard-CTA-slideshow structure, and food is arguably the single best slideshow vertical (eggs theory literally emerged from food/clean-eating content — Crave's dish-ranking data can be turned into endless "top 5 dishes in Austin" listicle slideshows); (2) format theft from proven Reddit/TikTok food content rather than invention; (3) fast kill criteria (1–3 weeks, pause and re-slot) and hook-text micro-iteration. The daily-views-budget and sub-500-view-conversion theories are unverified speculation — don't build a plan on them. Biggest structural caveat: Crave is single-city at launch, so national viral reach is mostly wasted impressions; a CPM-paid mass-posting network prices reach Crave can't monetize outside Austin, which undercuts the whole "shots on goal" economics unless geo-relevant formats ("Austin's best breakfast tacos") are the payload.
