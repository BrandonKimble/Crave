## Arc

Kieran joined StudyFetch on launch day (September 15, 2023) to make TikToks; his first video got ~3M views overnight and tens of thousands of sign-ups. The company bootstrapped on organic short-form for ~18 months, then raised an $11.5M Series A from Owl Ventures and the College Board (he mis-speaks "11.5 billion" once; corrected to $11.5M). The episode is a walkthrough of their creator-program system, centered on their signature move: paying large influencers five figures a month to run brand-new zero-follower StudyFetch accounts.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Headline stats: 7M+ users acquired over ~2.5 years; seven figures MRR, eight figures ARR ("at least a million in MRR and at least 10 million in annual revenue" per host's read); 2B+ cumulative views. (claimed)
- Core thesis: "UGC is becoming less and less effective because audiences are starting to understand that the videos... are straight-up ads." The hedge = influencers with real followings start brand-new zero-follower accounts branded with StudyFetch in the name; their existing audience migrates over and treats the content as authentic. (claimed/first-party strategy)
- Zero-follower accounts "are outperforming everything else" (host's framing in the intro). (claimed)
- Program mix: hundreds of active creators currently (has been "almost a thousand" at peak); mix of (1) regular UGC creators, (2) influencer-run separate accounts, (3) occasional sponsored posts on influencers' main accounts. (first-party claimed)
- Influencer separate-account deals: five figures/month retainers for multiple months, ~20-30 videos/month on the separate account. "Typically... 10 to 20 times as much as you pay a UGC creator." UGC creators run "a couple hundred bucks a month to a couple thousand." (claimed)
- Concrete example: Gemma — ~100k followers on her main account (later he says "close to 750,000" when the host asks how many followers "she" had — possibly a different influencer or a garbled number; UNCERTAIN which figure attaches to whom). Host's synthesis: target half a million to a million followers, expect ~$10,000/month for roughly a video a day — Kieran confirms "$10,000 a month as a whole, not for each and every video."
- Rates for the separate-account structure have come down since early days, when influencers quoted main-account rates because the concept was new. (claimed)
- Selection criteria: engagement over absolute reach. "Somebody that gets like 10,000 views a video but their comment section is filled" is a great sign; comments specifically (not just likes/bookmarks) because commenting = intentional viewing. Host's benchmark example they endorse: 160,000 views / 1,600 bookmarks / 33 comments = decent. (first-party heuristic)
- Sourcing archetypes they hunt: TikTok Shop creators (paid on affiliate, so they know how to sell), creators with prior brand partnerships, UGC creators with company experience. (first-party)
- Outbound > inbound for recruiting creators: DMs on TikTok/Instagram, email, even LinkedIn for high-priority targets; inbound applications ("Tally"-style listings) supplement. (first-party)
- Their public creator pitch: "Earn up to $1,200 per hour"; base rate $50–$2,500 per post (that's per-video base, not bonus-inclusive) with opportunity for more. (first-party, read off their own creator page)
- Bonus structure: $100 per 100,000 views on TikTok or Instagram, described as "pretty standard across... other creator programs"; individual deals vary (lower retainer / higher bonus, or vice versa). (first-party)
- Failure is routine: influencer accounts sometimes flop (shadow bans, wrong audience) — "it happens all the time... it can be a numbers game." (first-party admission)
- Creator website cited as "creators.sidehustle.com" — almost certainly a transcription error (likely creators.studyfetch.com). UNCERTAIN.

### Content strategy (organic)

- Origin: first-ever TikTok (cross-posted to Reels) got ~3M views overnight → tens of thousands of sign-ups in 24 hours. Attributed to feature novelty + timing + luck; maker had zero TikTok experience (photography/video background). (first-party anecdote)
- Biggest single video: lecture-hall format, 75M views, posted Nov/Dec 2023, "got us hundreds of thousands of sign-ups." Caption: "I thought finals were supposed to be hard." Wow factor = live lecture auto-note-taking streaming onto screen with hands visibly off the keyboard. Host notes Coconote had a similar lecture-hall video at 50–100M views. (claimed)
- Product-led virality doctrine: features are designed to be visually demoable BEFORE filming ("optimized for virality... they're very visual"). "When the product itself is the reason the video does well... it's going to convert very very well. But when... you loop in the product at the very last 5 seconds, it's not going to convert well." (first-party)
- CTA doctrine: last 4–5 seconds show the bare URL (studyfetch.com) on screen — hard CTA doesn't kill engagement when the product is the video's subject. But it's per-creator: lifestyle influencers (e.g., Helene, Harvard student) weave the product in 2–3 times through a day-in-the-life with no hard CTA, because their audience is already curious what they use. (first-party)
- Don't override a creator's native style; match format to creator (comedy for Sebastian, lifestyle for Helene, pure product-usage videos for others). (first-party)
- Problem/solution format "worked very well a couple years ago" but is harder now due to saturation and copycats. (claimed)
- Positioning shift over time: from "virality at all costs" to targeting the receptive niche (med students, type-A learners) who don't mind that the video is an ad. (first-party)
- Sell outcomes, not features: creators are instructed to sell "you will get better grades." (first-party)
- Internal tooling: "internal viral dashboards" that ping the team on trending videos across a group of niches; formats are pulled cross-niche. (claimed)
- Post-Series A shift: higher-production brand content (cafe pop-up activations, "emotional connection"), inspired by big companies that do quality over volume. (first-party)

### Platform mechanics (TikTok vs Reels)

- TikTok audience is "way higher intent" than Instagram Reels; Reels virality can convert "close to none" because Reels pushes to global/low-converting geos. US converts highest of any country. (claimed)
- Target demographic: >80% US viewership per video; a US-based creator posting on TikTok generally lands a US audience; overseas creators on Reels risk audience misalignment — have non-US creators favor TikTok. (first-party heuristic)
- Sourcing filter: if a creator does well on Reels but poorly on TikTok, they generally don't reach out — TikTok performance is the bar. (first-party)

## Deal structures

- Influencer zero-follower account: five-figure monthly retainer, multi-month commitment, ~20–30 videos/month on the new account; benchmark ~$10k/month total for ~daily posting from a 500k–1M-follower influencer. Retainer/bonus split negotiable (lower retainer + high bonus for confident creators).
- Standard UGC creators: a couple hundred to a couple thousand dollars per month; influencer separate-account deals run 10–20x UGC rates.
- Public program terms: $50–$2,500 base per post + $100 per 100k views bonus (TikTok or Instagram); "up to $1,200/hour" marketing pitch; $2,500/video base has been paid even to non-influencers if talent warrants.

## Contrarian positions

- UGC is decaying as a channel — audiences now read it as ads; the fix is fewer, more authentic, brand-labeled accounts, not more cheap volume (directly opposite the "scale UGC by hiring more cheap creators" consensus).
- Pay MORE per creator, not less: 10–20x UGC rates for influencer-run accounts; pay premium base rates ($2,500/video) for talent even without a following.
- Outbound recruiting of creators beats inbound applications (most programs run inbound).
- Views are explicitly no longer the goal ("if we're going for views, which we aren't anymore"); comments > views/likes/bookmarks as the selection metric.
- Hard on-screen URL CTA in the final seconds doesn't hurt engagement — when the product is the star.
- Don't hire a "TikTok native"; hire a marketing generalist — short form is just another channel.
- Virality is a product decision made pre-filming (design visually demoable features), not an editing/hook trick.

## Crave transfer

The transferable pieces are the deal-structure benchmarks ($50–$2,500/post base, $100 per 100k views, influencer separate accounts at 10–20x UGC rates — likely above a bootstrapped budget except with micro/local creators), the engagement-over-reach selection heuristic (comment density), and the doctrine that the product itself must be the visually demoable star (Crave's map-of-ranked-dishes is a plausible on-screen wow).
