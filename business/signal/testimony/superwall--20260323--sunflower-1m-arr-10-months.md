## Arc

Kobe Conrad, ex-addict (7 years sober) and former growth lead at Rupa (name garbled in captions: "Rupa/Ruper/Rupee" — likely Rupa Health), built Sunflower, an AI addiction-recovery app ("AI sponsor" named Sam + sobriety timer + community). Claims $0 → $1M annualized run rate in ~10 months and 10,000 paying subscribers. The episode exists to showcase two things aligned with the host's interests: an "hire the #1 influencer as an employee" UGC playbook and a paywall/trial-reminder experiment.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Started as a fully free product (Duolingo-inspired: "they spent like six or seven years before they started charging"); reversed because investors wanted revenue AND AI token costs were "scaling hundreds of thousands of dollars." (claimed)
- Paywall evolution ladder, each step claimed to add revenue: monthly-only → add annual → make annual the DEFAULT selected option ("a lot more annual revenue") → pay-upfront with a toggle-off trial ("made more money"). Core principle: "put the thing you want them to do as the default option" — ideally an annual subscription paid immediately, "no trial, no nothing." (claimed, no figures per step)
- Offer variety matters: weekly + monthly + annual so users self-select; framed as optimization that "doesn't hurt anyone." (claimed)
- Users are highly price sensitive (addiction demographic, often low income); they made "a lot of gains by actually LOWERING our price," not raising it. No numbers given. (claimed)
- Host states Sunflower's prices as $9/month or $35/year; guest neither confirms nor corrects. (secondhand/uncertain)
- Host mentions "no commitment, cancel anytime" copy increases conversions per "paywall experts" (secondhand); the green "offer applied" checkmark on Sunflower's paywall was NOT tested — "just part of the ship." (first-party admission)
- Design-level dopamine framing: cute bee/sunflower characters on the paywall to reduce paywall anxiety; "all that stuff helps when you're asking people to pay." (claimed, untested)

### Trial & onboarding

- "Trial anxiety" is a bigger factor than founders think. Fix = a priming screen before the paywall: "We want you to try Sunflower for free. We'll remind you before your trial ends" → then paywall.
- Numbers claimed for the trial-reminder experiment — NOTE the transcript contains TWO different revenue figures: "+56% revenue bump" said first, then "+48% increase in our revenue" when showing the data slide (48% is likely the on-slide number; flag as inconsistent). Also: +70% increase in annual revenue, ~46% increase in trial conversions, ~30% increase in trial starts, and a shift in plan mix toward annual. Still A/B testing versions of the priming screen. (claimed-first-party, slide shown on stream)

### UGC & creator deals

- Core playbook: don't buy posts — hire the #1 influencer in your niche as an actual employee (salary + equity + mission). Sunflower hired Eric "Mr. Impulsive" (name uncertain: captions give both "Mr. Impulsive" and "Mr. Impulse"), claimed #1 sobriety influencer, as "VP of Sobriety" — BEFORE the app had any revenue or even a monetization model.
- Eric's claimed scale: 50–100M views/month solo; ~3.5M followers; biggest platforms Facebook and TikTok (not Instagram); drives 50–100k link clicks/month to the app; links the app in everything he posts.
- Kobe claims $10–20M spent on influencer marketing over the last 10 years across his career. (claimed, unverifiable)
- "Never be the smallest sponsor — be someone's BIGGEST sponsor": if a main sponsor represents 50–75% of an influencer's revenue, you get defensibility, better treatment, better iteration. Same logic at conferences (at Rupa: ~$500k/yr on conferences; ask "what's your biggest package?" — "$100k gold" — then "what will you give me for $200,000?").
- Employee model > per-post: "purchasing in wholesale," defensible (Eric gets hit up by competitor apps constantly), influencer learns the product/roadmap, iteration compounds ("the way we drive link clicks today is way different than a year ago"), and mission feedback (Eric sits in their Slack seeing "this app saved my life" messages).
- Explicitly rejects the 100-account TikTok-farm / faceless-UGC model: "arbitrage," platform risk, won't scale past ~$1M — to reach $100M you need 10–30M owned followers, working paid ads, community, or retail partnerships ("four to five ways" total).
- Bootstrapped alternative he endorses: partner 50/50 with a creator — position the CREATOR as the hero ("wouldn't it be great if someone made an app for your audience? I'll be your developer"), rather than offering equity for promo. Admits "I don't think we could have built what we've built without the venture capital."
- Host claims Sunflower achieves ~$0.10 CPM ("10 cent CPM, which is kind of insane"); guest doesn't confirm the number directly, pivots to the be-the-biggest-sponsor principle. (secondhand/uncertain)
- Facebook is underrated: "this current generation is completely sleeping on Facebook" — huge reach on FB/IG Reels right now.

### Content strategy (organic)

- All marketing is brand marketing ("spaced repetition"); never do PURE brand marketing — every dollar should be either directly profitable or building an owned audience. Instead of a $5–10M Super Bowl ad, "go build yourself 30–40 million followers."
- Strength of impressions > count of impressions: what the viewer feels/context when they see you matters ("headliner effect" — biggest booth / #1 influencer posting you makes a no-name app look like a category leader).
- Long-term ambition: build owned media — grow Eric from 3.5M to 30M followers, build a "Sunflower social," use audience for legislative leverage. (speculation/vision)

### Launch & sequencing

- V1 was deliberately tiny/ugly: a timer, Fiverr sunflower graphics, a CRUD journal — built himself via a bootcamp as a learn-to-code project (~2020–21, pre-AI). Advice: "build the smallest thing you freaking can... you're in the self-improvement game, not the app-building game."
- He cloned an existing "quit something" app category — "I wasn't the first one to do it."
- Fundraising arc: couldn't get funded for 3–4 years; led growth at Rupa ($5M → $75M in 3.5 years; "scaled user acquisition by 4,000%"; nine-figure exit ~3 months after he left); put in $100k of his own money; raised initial ~$350k from people he'd made rich; total raised ~$6.5M (YC — works with Garry [Tan] as GP; a16z speedrun; Flybridge led seed; angels incl. CEOs of "Reven Capital" [uncertain], Beehiiv, SaaStr).
- First deck (22,000 MAU, 762 reviews at the time) pitched his past track record and FAILED to raise; the successful post-YC deck pitched the FUTURE (massive market, PMF proof, revenue-going-up chart — he admits one raise chart "filled in the month" they hadn't hit yet — LOIs for pilots). Lesson: "you can't raise off a playbook that came out of 2025... raise off where the future is going." Revenue up, not usage up.

### Retention & product

- The most important feature is NOT the AI — it's the sobriety TIMER: "the core job to be done that 1.2 billion humans have." Simple thing done in the most motivating way possible.
- The AI companion ALONE fails: "if you only do the AI companion, the whole thing is horrible. The engagement is horrible, the retention is horrible, no one is going to pay." Value = bundle (timer + tools + courses + community + content + agent).
- AI sponsor "Sam" evolution: ChatGPT wrapper → prompt optimization → agent (payday follow-ups, responds in the in-app social feed, cross-app context, memory system, biometric lock + data erase for users with abusive partners, VP of Therapy + clinical escalation for scary content, HIPAA compliant).
- Claimed usage scale: 4M+ messages handled in the last year ("more than a human provider could handle in a lifetime"); 109 years' worth of sobriety tracked per day; hundreds of thousands of "super users"; anecdotes of users talked out of suicide/relapse (Patty: 60-yr-old, 30-yr alcoholic, now 1 year sober). App localized in Spanish, Japanese, Portuguese, Korean. (all claimed)
- Moat framing: build where the model companies CAN'T — OpenAI told them (via YC) they avoid behavioral health due to (1) legal liability, (2) no outcome data to optimize on. Sunflower owns relapse/outcome data; "any space where the model company doesn't have the data + the agent isn't the whole solution" is the best place to build.
- 90% of people struggling with addiction never access any care — their users mostly live in that 90%. (claimed stat, uncited)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Five "truly S-tier" engineers; Kobe self-describes as non-technical; vibe-coding internal tooling replaced some work; AI token costs pre-paywall were running "hundreds of thousands of dollars."

### Other

- Hot take: subscription apps are going away; software becomes as cheap to produce as content, so the app becomes a lead magnet for the REAL monetization deeper in the funnel — services (they're launching a teletherapy clinic; "the bulk of revenue in this market is therapy, billions of dollars"), physical goods (milestone pins — "I bet that would do more revenue than our subscription revenue"; cites AA-coin-style token sellers doing $1M+/yr), and advertising/attention.
- Host context/plugs: Consumer Club members median $1M ARR; Superwall's free tool trained on 422 profitable paywall experiments (paywallexperiments.com); Superwall 100k+/mo founder dinners; Spytalk ad.

## Deal structures

- Eric ("Mr. Impulsive"): hired as a full employee — "VP of Sobriety" — with salary and equity (amounts undisclosed), started pre-revenue; links the app in all content; embedded in company Slack. Claimed to be worth 50–100k link clicks/month.
- Bootstrapper template (endorsed, not his own deal): 50/50 partnership where the developer builds an app FOR a creator's existing audience; creator handles all promotion.
- Conference template (from Rupa): ask for the biggest package then offer 2x ($200k vs the $100k top tier) in exchange for custom perks (stage speakers, email list access, private dinners, logo on lanyards).
- No per-post rates, CPM deals, or rev-share percentages disclosed.

## Contrarian positions

- Hire influencers as employees (salary + equity) instead of paying per post; one giant creator beats 30 tracked micro-influencers.
- Never run pure brand marketing; "I'm not sure brand marketers should exist" — yet all marketing IS brand marketing.
- Faceless UGC account farms are fragile arbitrage, not a $100M path.
- Default to annual, paid upfront, no trial as the ideal ask (trial exists as a toggle concession).
- Lowering price increased revenue (against raise-prices-until-it-hurts orthodoxy).
- Subscriptions are dying; apps become lead magnets for services/physical goods.
- Facebook is the most underrated organic channel right now.
- VC money was necessary — openly disputes the bootstrap-is-enough position for this playbook.

## Crave transfer

The paywall-mechanics ladder is the most transferable material: annual-as-default-selected, plan-variety self-selection, and especially the trial-reminder priming screen (+46% trial conversion, +48% revenue claimed) maps directly onto Crave's annual-with-trial option — worth testing a "we'll remind you before your trial ends" screen ahead of the hard paywall. His "pay upfront, no trial, annual default" ideal is literally Crave's current monthly pay-now design, which is mild validation, though his users' price sensitivity finding (lowering price won) comes from an addiction demographic and shouldn't be assumed for Austin foodies. The hire-the-#1-influencer playbook explicitly depends on ~$6.5M of VC ("I don't think we could have built this without venture capital") and does NOT transfer to bootstrapped Crave — but his stated bootstrap alternative (50/50 creator partnership where the creator is the hero) and be-someone's-BIGGEST-sponsor principle scale down: one Austin food creator treated as a genuine partner beats ten per-post micro deals. Discount the visionary material (subscriptions dying, apps as lead magnets, legislative leverage): it's a VC-narrative from a single survivor on a podcast whose host sells paywall tooling, and Sunflower's category (emotional AI companion, global TAM) is structurally unlike a local repeat-use utility.
