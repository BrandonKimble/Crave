## Arc

Daniel co-founded Sway AI (summer 2024 launch, idea from Dec 2023), a dating-profile-analyzer app that hit a claimed $2M ARR in 6 months, then joined Cal AI as Head of Product, which was just acquired by MyFitnessPal. The video is the Superwall podcast walking through his pre-launch Instagram/TikTok playbook, Sway's onboarding/paywall structure, and Cal AI's influencer-driven retention/growth model.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Sway defaulted to the ANNUAL plan because apps defaulting to annual "had a pretty good LTV" — annual collects revenue up front to fuel growth; they tried a weekly plan but "that's like a lot slower growth." (claimed)
- Sway monetized heavily on purpose (high prices) because private feedback is high-willingness-to-pay; explicitly the OPPOSITE of Cal AI, whose prices are "intentionally a lot lower" than everyone else in the market to fuel growth. (claimed)
- First paywall version: after onboarding, "see your action plan now" → tap continue → carousel of 8 slides listing everything included, to convey subscription value. (first-party description)
- Consumable credit upsell (added post-launch after seeing users repeat the flow more than expected) contributes "over 10%, I think like 11%" of revenue. (claimed)
- Biggest upsell = AI-optimized photo packs: $14.99 for a one-pack, $49.99 for the 10-photo pack; delivered by email after the user uploads face photos. (claimed)
- Whale thesis: consumables have no spend cap; top 1% of spenders make up the majority of revenue at Tinder/Hinge/Bumble and in gaming (co-founder learned this building his own game; host anecdote: ~$200 spent on Clash of Clans as a teen, others spend tens of thousands). (claimed/secondhand)
- Title/intro claims a "paywall structure that doubled his revenue per user overnight" — the specific doubling is never substantiated in the conversation itself. (host framing, unverified)

### Trial & onboarding

- Onboarding's job pre-paywall = raise motivation and purchase intention; structured like a sales funnel: clarify why the user is there → explain/label the problem → sell the dream result (incl. "what other members are experiencing") → personalize via questions → show dream result again ON the paywall. (first-party)
- Explainer videos BEFORE questions matter: initially they went straight to questions and got emails asking "what does this app even do? What am I even subscribing for?" — context first, then questions make sense. He says Cal AI ("Calion" in captions, garbled) goes straight to questions and "misses the whole point." (first-party)
- Question design: simplicity over ordering — multiple choice so answers take one tap; onboarding must not slow people down before the paywall. (first-party)
- Aha-moment placement: they A/B'd upload-photos-before-paywall vs after. Before-paywall risks users leaving the app (to grab screenshots) and never returning; "not asking people to do too much... performed a lot better" with paywall first, aha after. BUT he concedes that if you CAN show the aha before the paywall cheaply, "it would have been better." (first-party, tested)
- Cal AI: 3-day trial; Daniel's north-star when joining was retention through the trial window — ship enough value in 3 days to stay subscribed. Levers: fewer bugs, redesign of core logging screens (food database, camera, food detail page), groups/community feed (Strava-inspired network effect, clone-proofing), streaks (Duolingo-inspired daily return), leaderboards within groups, friend groups then public goal-based groups (lose weight / bulk / recipes). (first-party)

### UGC & creator deals

- Pre-launch: co-founder started an Instagram account ("how I cook on Hinge" prompt-advice videos); some went viral within a couple weeks; added a link-in-bio to the app PRE-ORDER before any code existed. (first-party)
- Willingness-to-pay proof: asked followers to DM for a manual profile review at ~$15; strangers paid before the app existed. CTA lived in the caption, not the video. (first-party — the "thousands of paying customers pre-launch" is the host's intro framing; Daniel's own account is more modest)
- TikTok slideshow system: designs in Figma + Canva, text lifted from another page's most-viral posts (account name he recalls as "men's improvement" — uncertain), Excel sheet auto-populated slides in seconds → a month of content batched in 1-2 hours. Videos got 10-50K views each, one at ~600K. (first-party)
- Warm-up sequencing: first weeks = unbranded aesthetic carousels with no/soft CTA to let the account catch the algorithm; only after guaranteed traction did they add full branding (orange theme) and per-slide CTAs. Fully-branded from day one "wouldn't have gone as viral." (first-party)
- Christmas Day post: pinned TikTok hit a couple million views (he says ~4M in context), drove $5-6K revenue in ONE day — their biggest day at that point; heavily branded. (claimed)
- Influencer ads: worked with several, best was "Defunct Sibling" (name uncertain — auto-caption); he made dedicated ad creatives that ran ON HIS OWN PAGE to his own audience (familiarity effect). Repurposing his hard-sell organic videos as paid ads worked "a lot better" than the organics themselves. (first-party)
- Creator-fit law: dating creators are BAD partners — the app cannibalizes their own coaching/course business; fitness creators are GOOD for Cal AI because calorie tracking doesn't conflict with selling fitness coaching. Beauty niche is bad: creators get fat brand deals from makeup companies (big margins), so they're expensive. Pick niches with MANY influencers who are CHEAP. (first-party insight)
- Inverted model he was exploring pre-Cal AI: build MANY vibe-coded apps fast, give the biggest YouTubers/Instagrammers EQUITY so they promote "their own" app; access via a talent agency that also takes an equity cut; alternative = spray-and-pray outreach. (speculation/exploration, never executed at scale)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Organic UGC ceiling: Sway plateaued around $30-40K/month on organic (slides + influencer + own posting) before paid ads unlocked the run to $2M ARR (~4-5x). (claimed)
- Self-posted UGC is hard to scale: you're limited by your own output/accounts and you saturate your own format; influencers stack (guaranteed views, built-in audiences) — "hoping and praying" vs consistency. (first-party opinion)
- Influencers as idea-validation: one variable (do people download/pay) vs UGC's two (can YOU go viral AND will people pay); faster test. But not all ideas work with influencers — Sway didn't grow via influencers-using-the-app because showing yourself using a dating-feedback app doesn't make sense; Cal AI requires it. (first-party)
- App-idea framework from Cal AI: (1) simple/legible in a marketing video — every Cal AI influencer video shows the same 3 screens (camera/scan, loading, food detail) and the app must be self-explanatory from those; (2) novel mechanic (photo→calories was unique at scale); (3) big existing market (calorie tracking / MyFitnessPal-sized) for durable growth. Established market = demand is proven; apply the novel twist there rather than a blank-slate idea ("solving too many variables"). (first-party framework)
- Content-niche-first ideation: find a big viral content niche (motivation, horoscopes) and plug an app into it — the unnamed therapy/mood app (~$800K/mo iOS + $200K/mo Android per Sensor Tower, heavy Facebook ads) and Astra (soulmate-picture framing, recycled idea reframed as new) are cited as niche-riding, not idea-driven, successes. (secondhand/speculation)

### Paid ads

- Paid ads were the inflection at BOTH Sway (30-40K/mo organic → $2M ARR) and Cal AI ("big inflection point... when we started really ramping up on paid ads"). "If you can get paid ads to work, it's the best option, but it's also kind of hard." (claimed)
- Controversial ad creatives that generate comment-section arguments performed well. (first-party)
- Scaling bottleneck = creative volume; relying on one influencer for creative starved variety. Build a creative-scaling pipeline before you scale spend. (first-party)

### Retention & product

- Sway LTV "very high as opposed to any other app I've seen." (claimed, no number)
- Product-led growth at Cal AI: progress-photo uploads shared to Instagram stories, meal/calorie screenshots shared organically, milestone badges (e.g., full-year logging streak) designed to be story-shareable. (first-party)
- Share features inside Sway underperformed expectations — consistent with the core private-feedback hypothesis (people don't want public dating feedback; that's WHY they pay). (first-party)
- Speed doctrine: "human motivation only lasts about 2 weeks" per feature; Cal AI co-founders move very fast; Robinhood Crypto went idea→launch in ~1 month. Rally the whole company behind one big project. (first-party/claimed)
- Branding as growth: a product that LOOKS unlike everything else in the category stands out (Robinhood Crypto looked like a video game vs Coinbase's banking look); but for influencer-driven apps, clarity beats edginess — the 3 screens must be instantly legible. (first-party)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Sway = 3 co-founders: Charles (iOS eng, built the app), Luis (marketing/ops), Daniel (design + product). (first-party)
- Cal AI: bootstrapped, started by 17-year-olds ~2 years before acquisition; "couple million a month" when Daniel joined; sale to MyFitnessPal closed ~December (announced ~a week before recording); teams and apps staying separate. (claimed)
- Snap product design team ~10-15 people despite thousands of employees; Meta = data/AB-driven, Snap = intuition/psychology-driven. (first-party color)

## Deal structures

- Manual pre-launch service: ~$15 per DM'd profile review (payment before product existed).
- Photo packs: $14.99 (single) / $49.99 (10-pack) consumables; credits upsell = ~11% of revenue.
- Influencer-equity model (explored, not executed): big creators get equity in the app they promote; the introducing talent agency also takes an equity cut. No flat-fee, per-view, or rev-share numbers disclosed anywhere; the Defunct Sibling arrangement's terms were not stated. "None discussed" beyond this.

## Contrarian positions

- Sell the manual service BEFORE building the app; distribution before code; paying DMs as the validation gate (vs. the standard build-then-launch).
- Paywall BEFORE the aha moment — tested and kept, against the show-value-first orthodoxy (with the caveat that a cheap pre-paywall aha would be better if achievable).
- Deliberately HIGH prices (vs. Cal AI's deliberately low prices) — pricing posture should follow the privacy/value psychology of the niche, not a universal rule.
- Start social accounts UNBRANDED and CTA-free to warm the algorithm; branding comes after traction (against always-plug-your-app advice).
- Steal proven text: he openly repurposed another account's most-viral copy rather than originating content.
- UGC/organic is a ~$30-40K/mo ceiling, not a scaling strategy; it's the on-ramp to paid.
- "AI wrappers" dismissal is wrong — the biggest current acquisitions are wrappers (Cal AI/MyFitnessPal).

## Crave transfer

Category mismatch is the big caveat: both Sway and Cal AI are single-user AI-wow utilities with global TAM and influencer-legible demos; Crave is a local-density repeat-use discovery product launching in one city, so the paid-ads inflection story and the "3 legible screens for influencer videos" bar transfer only partially (though map+ranked-dish-list IS arguably video-legible: "the app that ranks every dish in Austin"). What transfers cleanly: annual-default pricing (Crave already does this), paywall-before-aha being viable when the onboarding sells the dream result well (supports Crave's hard paywall — but note Sway's aha needs user-supplied photos while Crave could show real Austin data pre-paywall cheaply, which Daniel himself says is BETTER when possible), the creator-fit law (Austin food creators don't sell competing products and are cheap/plentiful — a genuinely good fit by his own criteria), and pre-launch manual validation ("DM me and pay $15 for a where-to-eat-in-Austin itinerary" is a direct analog). Discount the $2M number itself: claimed, survivor-told, on a paywall-vendor's channel with three tool plugs — the tactics are more credible than the totals.
