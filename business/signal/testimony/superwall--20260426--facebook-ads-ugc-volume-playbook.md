## Arc

Alex built Boost App Social, an iOS Instagram-growth/hashtag subscription app (pivoted from a 2018–19 web hashtag tool with a vector-database backend), scaled it to a claimed $100k MRR almost entirely on Meta ads fueled by a mass-cold-email UGC creator pipeline, and sold it for seven figures in January (2026) to a secretive PE-style buyer from the gaming-app world. The video is a paid-acquisition postmortem: the thesis is that Meta ads are a volume game (1 winning ad in 85) and the durable asset was the ad infrastructure — warm account, Meta rep, 300 perpetual-rights creatives — which transferred in the sale.

## Claims

### Paid ads

- Total Meta spend "probably 1.5 to 1.8 million dollars" over ~2.5 years; "more than 2 million, probably" over the full app lifecycle before sale. (claimed)
- "Something like 800k was wasted" — on bad creatives, banned accounts, and non-performing ads. (claimed)
- Hit rate: "one video out of 85 would get all the ad spend and work" — intro frames it as 84 of 85 ads barely working. Elsewhere he says ~97% of creatives don't work "either because Meta didn't love them or because just audience wouldn't convert." (claimed; the 1/85 ≈ 98.8% and 97% figures are loosely consistent, both approximations)
- One duplicated ad at 11:30 p.m. wasted $8k "in a blip." (claimed, first-party anecdote)
- Spend ramp: $100/day → $500/day → $1,000/day → $10k/day on Meta. (claimed)
- Unit economics framing: "You spend 50, you make 100 and you're profitable in the middle." (claimed, illustrative)
- Banned "six, eight times" including permanent bans; always recovered accounts; each recovery a month-long(ish) fight through offshore support, appeals, "legal calls" (intro's phrasing). Biggest challenge in scaling was bans, not creative volume. (claimed)
- Got a dedicated Meta rep only after large spend; early-stage support is useless ("sometimes in third world countries... without people who can really push the buttons"). (claimed)
- A creative can be "technically excellent" and still bomb; needs to be "both a Meta match and an audience match." Meta is predictive about creatives (colors etc.) and pushes spend on ones it believes in. Sometimes spent ~$20k on a great creative that never worked. (claimed/speculation about Meta internals)
- Three distilled creative rules: (1) always caption — "80% of the users... use Instagram or TikTok... without the sound" (claimed stat, uncertain provenance); (2) green screen: person talking with app demo behind them — "a natural perfect match" for app ads; (3) "go crazy with hooks" — first 5 seconds most important; hook = copy angle AND image/video angle.
- Hook specificity beats generality: example hook "How to avoid getting stuck on 1,000 views" — targets a specific frustration; an ad reaching 1M precisely-frustrated people beats a generic 50M-reach concept. (claimed)
- Show the app in-hand: separate camera filming a hand holding the phone, sometimes combined with green screen in the same ad; mix-and-match footage across creator submissions. (claimed)
- Remix ratio: don't over-spin; ~5 creator videos → ~15 edited variants ("feed the algo better"), not 2,000 micro-variants. Post-edit creatives to be "crispier"/"Mr. Beastie style" with quicker transitions. (claimed)
- AI-hooks today (speculation, he sold before doing this): use top LLMs + "Valar 3" [uncertain transcription — almost certainly Google Veo 3] to script/generate emotionally extreme ("weird, scary") openers humans couldn't film; edit in CapCut; mine the Meta ad library for inspiration.
- Platform risk was a sale motivator: fully ads-dependent, no organic; "maybe the CPMs are going to double... you're not profitable anymore." Selling = "taking chips off the table." (claimed)

### UGC & creator deals

- Outreach engine: 1,000 Gmail accounts on a shared inbox + internal script, ~10 (elsewhere "10 to 30") emails per account per day; self-described "a little bit black hatty"; built in-house, not for sale. (claimed)
- Reply rate "I don't know, 10, 20%" from Instagram creators (emails scraped from bios). (claimed, vague)
- Funnel: cold email → VA email exchange + pre-recorded video from Alex → move to a Telegram group (chat, file transfer, contract signing, MP4 delivery) → contract → creative delivery.
- $100 sent up front once a creative looked promising, explicitly to prove it wasn't a scam and get them to install Telegram ("they feel that they can make another 1000 bucks" — implies ~$1k-scale total payouts, not stated precisely). "One or two ran with the money" — accepted as cost of doing business. (claimed)
- ALL contracts were perpetual, buyout-style rights — no annual renewals ("they might ask you for 100k" if an ad blows up); contracts also granted freedom to edit/mix-and-match. ~200–300 creatives ("something like 300," self-flagged as uncertain) with signed contracts transferred in the acquisition.
- Creator direction: an A4 one-pager with the app, working hooks/triggers for the customer type; freedom within that; reject only obviously low-effort hooks; occasionally "a diamond that... pays back hundreds of thousands of dollars in profits." (claimed)
- Host's gloss: today you can replace this infra with purchasable creator lists + tools like Instantly/Smartlead [transcript says "smartly" — uncertain]; the unique part was the volume mindset.

### Launch & sequencing

- Origin: 2018–19 web hashtag tool (vector-DB related-hashtag suggester) with no PMF → saw Hashtag Expert getting traction → pivoted the same tech into an iOS subscription app because the mobile context (research hashtags on the phone, paste into Instagram) removed the clunky web→mobile UX. (claimed)
- They were already in "the Instagram services business," so they knew the target market intuitively — product/channel match: advertising an Instagram-growth tool ON Instagram to people who want growth. (claimed)
- Growth to 100k MRR = added features beyond hashtags (fonts, video/photo editing) to raise willingness to pay from ~$40/yr toward ~$100/yr, plus heavy Meta spend, Mixpanel, A/B testing, studying successful apps' onboardings/paywalls. (claimed)

### Pricing & paywall

- Boost App Social pricing evolution: "40 bucks a year, maybe 100 bucks a year" as value features were added. (claimed, vague)
- Business-app pricing thesis: business/prosumer apps can charge $200–300/yr (weekly/monthly slicing optional) because buyers see ROI ("they spend 200-300 bucks a year and they make back 2000") — "it smooths out the conversion." (claimed/opinion)
- Business-app multiples: buyers paid better because "our numbers were pretty good as far as conversions, as far as profitability" vs e.g. a fitness app. (claimed)
- Host ad, not guest: Superwall's free tool at paywallexperiments.com trained on 422 profitable paywall experiments; Consumer Club members/interviewees "build apps at a median of 1 million ARR"; Superwall dinners require $100k/month revenue. (host promotional claims)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Team: Alex + business partner at top of creative strategy; a head of VAs + VA team handling email negotiation and Telegram; internal email tooling; broker (Charlie from UpFlip) and a dual-qualified European/American lawyer for the sale. (claimed)
- Exit mechanics: negotiation 4–5 months; ~100-page contract; escrow in Singapore; buyer was a low-online-presence PE-style group from gaming apps with "hundreds of millions to deploy"; diligence comfort came via a reference call with another founder who'd sold to them; ~1.5–3 months of Alex's time was pure legal work. Sale included the warm Facebook account, the Meta rep relationship, 300 perpetual-rights creatives, dashboards — "the infrastructure was the actual business." (claimed)

### Retention & product

- Consumer apps' advantage is organic/virality loops (share-with-friends, freebies); his app had essentially none and lived or died by paid. (opinion)
- Anti-cloning stance: don't copycat existing apps ("very sad... probably going to put you in trouble... not going to work very well"); new niches = blue ocean = "crazy hooks, crazy ads" work.
- Adam Little anecdote (secondhand): built ~100 apps; 2–3 doing "really good money," a couple more profitable; moat is app age + reviews for ASO — newcomers won't outrank by "placing some couple ASO keywords."
- Current venture Screens Design: library of 2,200+ full app walkthroughs (onboarding/paywall videos), growing purely organically; launching "Screens Design Create" canvas (concept → Figma export / AI-coder feed) built on abstracted principles from top apps. Demo example shown: a "daily devotional app for women" at $15k revenue from 7k installs (revenue ≈ 2× installs cited approvingly), scored 9/10 on low-friction design, 1/10 on social proof density. (claimed, product demo)
- Non-compete: NDA/contract bars them from the Instagram-growth space for ~1 year but allows code reuse and other business apps. (claimed)

## Deal structures

- Creator UGC deals: $100 up-front good-faith payment once a creative looked promising; implied total payout on the order of ~$1,000 per creator ("they can make another 1000 bucks" — imprecise); PERPETUAL usage rights, no renewal terms, edit/remix rights included; negotiated by VAs over Telegram; ~300 such contracts existed and transferred in the exit. Explicitly refused time-limited (1-year) licenses to avoid renegotiation leverage if an ad scaled.
- Acquisition: seven figures (exact price undisclosed), brokered by Charlie of UpFlip, ~100-page contract, Singapore escrow, 4–5 month negotiation.

## Contrarian positions

- The biggest obstacle to scaled Meta spend is account bans, not creative production or CPA math — and bans (even "permanent" ones) are recoverable if you refuse to accept them, 6–8 times over.
- Wasted spend is reframed as the product: the $800k was "tuition" — the deliberately-run losers are how you find the 1-in-85 winner. Most people quit "before the data gets interesting."
- Buy perpetual rights to UGC outright rather than the industry-standard limited usage windows.
- Don't maximize creative permutations (against the "spin every asset 100 ways" school): ~3× remix per creator batch, curated by human judgment.
- Creative quality is only half the outcome; Meta's model "liking" a creative is an independent, unfixable factor — a technically excellent ad can bomb for no diagnosable reason.
- "Consumer apps" is the wrong frame; think "subscription apps" and graduate to businessy/prosumer niches with ROI-justified $200–300/yr pricing instead of another to-do/journaling app.
- Anti-cloning, pro-blue-ocean — implicitly against the prevalent "copy a proven app + outspend on ads" meta.

## Crave transfer

Almost none of the paid-ads core transfers to Crave at launch: this is a $1.5–1.8M-spend, $10k/day playbook for an app whose product (Instagram growth) was natively matched to the ad platform's own audience — Crave has no ad budget and a local-utility product with no such channel-product identity. What does transfer: the creator-outreach mechanics at small scale (specific hook doc, $100 up-front trust payment, Telegram for negotiation/file exchange, and especially perpetual+edit rights in every creator contract so assets compound and could transfer in any future sale); the creative grammar (captions-always, green-screen person + app demo, hyper-specific pain hooks — "best tacos in Austin according to 40,000 Redditors" is exactly his specificity principle); and the 1-in-85 expectation applied to organic content — treat UGC/TikTok volume as a portfolio where ~97% of posts flopping is the plan, not failure. His $200–300/yr "real-ROI business app" pricing logic does NOT justify Crave's $7.99/mo — Crave is consumer discretionary, closer to the fitness-app category he says commands worse conversion/multiples; that's a caution flag on the hard paywall, not support for it. Weight everything as one survivor's rounded recollections on a podcast whose host sells paywall tooling and whose guest is promoting his next product.
