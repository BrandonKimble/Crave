## Arc

Blake Anderson, 23, non-technical, built RizzGPT (AI dating-reply generator) mostly with ChatGPT-written SwiftUI a month after graduating, hit ~$80k MRR in week one off two $50 TikTok promos, then repeated the playbook with UMAX (GPT-Vision face-rating/looksmaxxing app, influencer equity partner) and Cal AI (calorie tracking, run by two 17-year-olds he staked). Claims ~$5M career revenue in the past year and projects $10M+ over the next 12 months, with profits funneled into "Apex," a free-apps + content-house self-improvement brand. The video exists as a founder-hype interview; every number is his own claim.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- RizzGPT: originally free with limited credits, then $7/week; later moved to $7/week flat with no free usage. (claimed)
- He asserts the highest-LTV configuration they found across all his apps was "charging like an inordinate amount for subscriptions" and making cancellation hard — practices he says he declined on ethical grounds. (claimed, first-party observation of own tests)
- Frames whole consumer-app space as "purely a game of maximization of LTV," a dynamic he's now reacting against with free apps under Apex. (opinion)

### Trial & onboarding

- Credits-then-paywall was RizzGPT's initial mechanic; no trial-length, conversion-rate, or funnel numbers given for any app. (claimed, sparse)
- UMAX product philosophy: "give people what they're looking for as quickly as I can" — ratings + recommendations immediately; simplicity over depth. (claimed)

### UGC & creator deals

- RizzGPT launch: paid $50 each for two promos to "rizz content" creators (screenshot-slideshow meme accounts) who inserted the app mid-slideshow as their native content. The two videos did "a few million views each," generated almost 200,000 downloads in under a week, and "shot us to $80,000 in monthly revenue off the jump" from $100 total spend. (claimed; the single most-cited number set in the video)
- Creator selection: views over followers. The promo accounts had ~50k–100k followers max but millions of views/video; he explicitly calls follower-based pricing by big brands "very clearly not the correct strategy." (claimed/opinion)
- Why $50 worked: new niche with no other monetizer bidding — "who else is going to do a promo in this space?" Cheap promos exist where a viral content trend has no obvious advertiser yet. (claimed)
- No affiliate/rev-share deals — "attribution is very difficult with mobile applications"; flat fees only. (claimed)
- Outreach volume: "hundreds of people a day"; for must-have creators he joined their Discord, spammed hourly, messaged the creator's mom and girlfriend to get contact (specific case: a creator named Malik — name uncertain, auto-caption). (claimed)
- Outreach copy: direct two-liners win — "love your content, looking to pay you X dollars for a promo"; value-upfront messages (redesigned logo, concrete suggestion) beat calendar-link pitches. (opinion)
- UMAX: escalated from paid promos to an equity partner — brought on looksmaxxing influencer Sam Zia (~200k TikTok followers at the time but 1M+ views/video; niche "big three" named as K. Shami [uncertain spelling], Dylan Leam [uncertain], Sam Zia) plus his older brother as co-founders. Rationale: tight-knit niche with a small set of taste-leading creators → partner with one. (claimed)
- Counter-case: Cal AI got NO influencer partner because calorie tracking is a huge market with no single community leader — "no influencer will be able to provide their keep" if you're going for market dominance. Partnering is a function of niche structure and time horizon. (opinion/framework)
- Influencer marketing overall: "the hardest to crack" of the three channels he sees working (paid platform ads, influencer, in-house UGC); most people take the first yes at 5k followers and lose money; audience density/loyalty is the hidden variable; he's "highly skeptical" of anyone selling a course on it. (opinion)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Trend-spotting method: scroll TikTok/Reddit/Instagram for what's "popping," verify with Google Trends (looksmaxxing rising steadily Nov–Dec 2023), then marry the trend to a just-released AI capability (GPT API → RizzGPT; GPT-Vision → UMAX). "Market X new technology, timing was perfect." (claimed)
- Product ideas come from observed user behavior in the wild: guys already screenshot dating convos into group chats → productize that exact loop. (claimed)
- Apex plan: content house between LA and San Diego, founders as creators (FaZe Clan / Barstool analogy), free apps as the top of funnel; monetization deferred. (pre-launch, speculation)

### Paid ads

- Mentioned only in passing: the Cal AI studio "runs all the paid ads" and does in-house content generation alongside influencer work; no spend, CPI, or ROAS numbers anywhere in the transcript. (claimed, thin)

### ASO & app store

- Getting on the App Store: "really wasn't that bad" — LLC + developer account; co-founder handled it for RizzGPT, he did it himself for UMAX. No ASO tactics, keyword, or ranking discussion. (claimed)
- The bigger competitor ("the Rizz app") had done <200,000 lifetime downloads when RizzGPT did that in its first week; competitor later forced a rename via threatened lawsuit (RizzGPT → Plug AI). (secondhand/claimed)

### Launch & sequencing

- RizzGPT: ~1 month to build (95% ChatGPT-written SwiftUI, no backend, no auth, app displayed as "RZGPT-production" on the home screen, API key exposed client-side); a couple hundred downloads in the first weeks pre-promo; the two $50 promos were the entire launch. (claimed)
- Revenue arc: $80k MRR "off the jump" → scaled to a bit over $200k MRR over the next few months → steady ~10 months → ~$2.5M total revenue at recording. (claimed)
- UMAX: ~1 month build (ChatGPT ~90% of code, SwiftUI + AWS); $100k month 1, $200k month 2, then went viral → 500k+/month; "a little over $3M" cumulative (he first misspoke $5M, corrected), projecting >$5M in 2024 for UMAX alone. (claimed)
- Cal AI: he supplied idea/designs/playbook/capital, two 17-year-olds (Zach — who previously sold a multi-million-user website for ~$100k — and Henry) built and run it on an even split; >$300k revenue in the trailing month, ~$3M ARR claimed with the caveat "not all of that is recurring." (claimed)
- Career totals: ~$5M revenue past year; "at least $10M" next 12 months; says he could do "$20M minimum" next year if all-in on consumer apps. (claimed/speculation)
- Team pattern: split roles (engineering / design-product-appstore / marketing); ">2x outcome when you have two highly skilled people"; consumer apps = rapid iteration on everything, unlike ecom's sample lead-times. (opinion)
- Cost datapoint: pre-GPT-Vision, an equivalent face-analysis model would have required a custom ML vision model at "$50 to $100,000" with no quality guarantee vs. "less than a cent an image" via API. (claimed)

### Retention & product

- Explicitly concedes UMAX has weak continuity: "There's not too much" reason to keep using it — updated recommendations barely change; progress pictures/score tracking are the retention feature; he chose not to invest in prolonged value because he lacked passion, calling UMAX "opportunistic," a "launchpad business." (claimed, candid)
- UMAX scoring mechanics: LLM classification (20+ binary/categorical traits: acne, discoloration, grooming, jawline contour, eye bags) then deterministic point deduction from a baseline — because GPT is bad at direct quantification ("everyone gets a 70"). Recommendations skew toward malleable traits (skincare, hair, jawline, weight) plus an aspirational "potential score." (first-party technical detail)
- His product thesis: dead-simple, single clear problem, distributed better than incumbents — mocks a water-reminder app as the convoluted-value counterexample. Competition without distribution "is as if they don't exist." (opinion)
- Ceilings: plateau despite doing the right things = local ceiling; breaking through requires product change + passion he didn't have. (opinion)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Built RizzGPT non-technically: asked ChatGPT "how do I build a mobile app," chose SwiftUI, Figma designs with co-founder, ChatGPT as tutor/debugger; Heroku backend initially (regrets, would pick AWS). (first-party)
- Left RizzGPT amicably, kept equity, still paid from it; was broke during the Apple payout-cycle lag (couch-surfing November, brother lending grocery money). (claimed)
- Studio model at Cal AI: engineers who want startup experience lead one app each; shared designers, social media marketers, paid-ads infrastructure. (claimed)
- Money allocation: ~2/5 Apex, ~2/5 parents (father has SPMS), ~1/5 himself. Apex to be a B-corp [he says "BC corp"], funded with "at least a couple million" of his own money, no near-term raise. (claimed)

### Other

- Ethics positioning: declined max-LTV dark patterns, declined targeting the female-beauty niche with UMAX despite believing it would have been lucrative; "insecurities drive change" defense. (claimed)
- Confidence heuristic: projects where his confidence grows as he thinks/builds succeed; where confidence decays, they fail. (anecdote)
- Anti-market-research stance: no focus groups, no five-month beta; "I build the app and I put it on the market and I hope that it works." Caveat he adds: fine for consumer utility, NOT for consumer social where first impressions and churn are fatal. (opinion)

## Deal structures

- RizzGPT launch promos: $50 flat per video × 2 creators (~50k–100k followers, millions of views/video), app inserted into their native slideshow content; total spend ~$100 → claimed ~200k downloads/wk and $80k MRR. No affiliate or rev-share ever — attribution too messy on mobile; flat fees only.
- UMAX: equity partnership with influencer Sam Zia (co-founder-level; exact % not stated) + older brother.
- Cal AI: even split ("we'll go even split") between Blake and the two teenage operators; Blake contributes idea, designs, playbook, capital, mentorship; they build and run it.

## Contrarian positions

- Pay creators on views/engagement, never followers — calls the industry's follower-based pricing plainly wrong.
- Competition doesn't matter if it isn't distributing: "if your competition is not known, it's as if they do not exist" — he says "a lot of people actually disagree" with this.
- Skip market research and betas for utility apps; shoot from the hip, launch, iterate.
- Flat-fee promos over affiliate deals for mobile (against the affiliate-everything consensus) because attribution is broken.
- 95% ChatGPT-written code shipped to $200k+ MRR — against "you can't build a real app with ChatGPT."
- Influencer equity partnership is niche-dependent, not a universal playbook — only where a tight community has a few taste leaders; explicitly refused it for the mass-market calorie app.
- Anyone selling an influencer-marketing course is suspect; the channel is all nuance and iteration.

## Crave transfer

Category mismatch is the dominant caveat: all three apps are single-session AI-wow utilities riding a fresh model release plus a viral content trend, monetized at $7/week against impulse demand — the opposite of Crave's local-density, repeat-use utility with a hard $7.99/mo paywall, and Blake himself admits UMAX had near-zero retention, which he could ignore and Crave cannot. What transfers: (1) the creator-selection math — pay small niche accounts flat fees priced on views not followers, and hunt niches where a content format (Austin food TikTok/Reels is exactly one) has high views but no incumbent advertiser bidding, so promos are cheap; (2) native-format insertion — the promo worked because the app appeared inside the content people already watch ("this is where I get my rizz from"), which maps cleanly to Austin food creators crediting Crave as where they found the dish; (3) flat-fee-over-affiliate given mobile attribution reality; (4) his niche-structure test for equity partners — Austin food is closer to his "tight-knit, few taste leaders" case than to calorie tracking, so a local-creator equity or deep-partnership play is at least coherent by his own framework. Discount all revenue figures heavily: self-reported, round, on a hype podcast, from a survivor whose two $50 promos are a lottery ticket presented as a playbook — the replicable part is the targeting logic and outreach volume (hundreds/day, direct two-line offers), not the $100-to-$80k-MRR outcome.
