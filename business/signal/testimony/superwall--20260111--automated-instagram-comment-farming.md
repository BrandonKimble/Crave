## Arc

Nick and Ivan are a two-person technical team (8 years together: Minecraft server → private blockchain dev → an AI flight-search consumer app) who, needing distribution for their own app, built TikTok posting automation, got ~100M+ views, then pivoted the automation itself into the business: an army of automated physical phones posting Instagram/TikTok comments at industrial scale for app clients. The video exists because the host covers "edge of distribution" tactics and the guests are launching a self-serve platform version of their agency.

## Claims

### UGC & creator deals

- Core model: mass-scale Instagram comments on trending/viral videos in a niche, posted from branded accounts that pose as the app's official sub-pages, "interns," employees, "friends of founders," or founders themselves. Link-in-bio or @-mention routes to the app store / brand page. (claimed, first-party to their own operation)
- Flagship campaign (USA-based healthcare app — fitness/nutrition/sleep): ~300,000 Instagram comments → ~7,000 clicks. Guest hedged the comment count: "I believe around 300,000 maybe." Campaign ~half complete at recording; they PROJECT ~4x return for the client (projection, not realized). (claimed)
- Profile-funnel example: 5,690 profile activity ≈ 4,900 profile visits, ~700–750 link-in-bio clicks ≈ 15% profile→link CTR. Range across campaigns: 3% (bad) up to a best of ~50–60% "when the content was engaging and the profile was set up in the right way." 15% called a good benchmark. (claimed, single-screenshot evidence)
- A single comment with ~800 likes can drive "thousands of profile visits" — but explicitly "not trackable to be clear." (speculation/claimed)
- Power-law: only "the smallest percentage" of comments get exposure; like UGC video, ~10 of 100 assets generate almost all results. (claimed)
- Comment styles that work: (1) answer a real question in the comments, soft CTA at end; (2) ask a question / name-drop the app; (3) pure story with no CTA (curiosity → profile visit); (4) "check out the clip on our page" (video-not-app ask — lower friction than "install another app"). Warm-up comments carry no CTA at all — same logic as cold-email inbox warm-up.
- The counterintuitive winner: transparent founder/co-founder framing ("the health app co-founded by my friends and me") outperformed covert approaches — "being upfront… does the opposite often. So, people like it." (claimed)
- Comment quality rules: must be context-dependent to the video and existing comment thread, never copy-paste promotion; timing matters — be among the first commenters on a video with viral potential; skip old videos and junk-filled comment sections; only reply to people who actively engage (questions/opinions), never "thanks for the video" comments.
- Iteration is constant: one angle "worked for two weeks and then it just stopped working without any changes on our side" — Instagram began detecting it; fixed by changing the angle/prompts. Early campaign phase produced nothing until they found the co-founder/friend angles.

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Infrastructure: custom Android build that loads/unloads the full device ROM so each account effectively gets its own unique phone; custom human-like input simulation to evade bot detection. Two-person team, no VA army. (first-party description of own tech)
- Current fleet: 60 physical phones. Per-device throughput: Instagram 12–24 accounts/day/phone, ~100–200 accounts/day/phone on TikTok; up to 60 comments/day/account. Normal users get ~10 accounts per device on TikTok before algorithmic trust degrades.
- Scaling math (projection): 1,000 devices ≈ 60,000 active accounts × 3 videos + 45 comments/day ≈ ~1.5M videos and 21M comments/month — "like replying to every single person on Mr. Beast reels on Instagram within like a month." 1,000 is framed as "the first step," then "thousands." (speculation/projection)
- AI is used for: filtering the For-You feed (prompt-based video analysis to pick engagement targets), generating comment variants from angle prompts, and account warm-up decisions.
- Platform roadmap: MVP "within a week"; fleet dashboard, plain-English automation building, community templates, per-agent branding control (bios, names, stories, highlights). No pricing discussed.

## Contrarian positions

- Comments, not content, as the primary organic channel — treat Instagram comments as "UGC in text form" and B2C growth as cold-email-style outbound (warm-up, inbox/account spreading, volume + small hit-rate).
- Transparency beats stealth: openly saying "this is my app / my company" in promo comments outperforms covert seeding.
- Mass-automated engagement helps platforms rather than fights them (fills content voids, improves targeting) — directly disputed by the host in-episode.
- No-CTA story comments can outperform CTAs by pushing curiosity-driven profile visits.
- "Watch the clip on our page" converts better than "install the app" as the in-comment ask.

## Crave transfer

The funnel arithmetic is the sobering part: ~300K comments → 7K clicks (≈2.3% comment→click, before install, before a hard $7.99 paywall) is only viable with an automated device farm — a solo founder cannot manually replicate this, and buying it means ToS-violating gray-hat spend with platform-ban and brand risk for a local-trust product. What DOES transfer cheaply: the transparent-founder voice ("I built this app, here's the Austin answer") in manual, genuinely context-relevant comments on Austin food content (r/Austin, Austin FoodTok/IG), where a niche+local match should beat their generic health-app rates; the "answer the actual question, soft mention at the end" comment structure; the profile-as-landing-page discipline (bio, pinned content) since 15% profile→link is the realistic mid-case, not 50%; and only engaging high-intent commenters. Discount all numbers heavily: self-reported, mid-campaign, projected 4x ROI from vendors pitching their own platform, with the key comment-count "maybe" hedged by the speaker himself. Category mismatch is mild (their client was lifestyle/health, comparable consideration level to food discovery), but the scale mechanism — not the copywriting insight — is the part that doesn't transfer.
