## Arc

Roy Lee, 21, got rescinded from Harvard, built Interview Coder (a tool to cheat on technical interviews) at Columbia, filmed himself using it to get an Amazon offer, got suspended when Amazon complained, live-tweeted the scandal into virality, and raised $5M off it. He relaunched the tech as Cluely ("cheat on everything" — an invisible AI assistant that sees your screen and hears your audio), claiming $500k MRR / ~$6M ARR within two months of an April 20, 2025 launch. The interview is a tactical walkthrough of his "mindshare over views" marketing doctrine plus his in-house UGC machine; the host uses it as a wrapper to sell his own mass-UGC program.

## Claims

### Content strategy (organic)

- Core thesis: "distribution-first company" — "if you can get a billion views a month on anything... people will buy," regardless of product. (claimed/speculation)
- Short-form supply/demand gap: per-person content consumption has "100xed" but creator supply hasn't; creators are massively underpaid; a 150–200k-follower TikToker whose videos get ~5M views each still can't make a living beyond creator fund. (claimed)
- Mindshare ≠ views: Subway Surfers-type clips get (his guess) "a hundred billion views a month" but nobody talks about them; one controversial launch video seen once ≈ the mental effect of seeing "a Stripe billboard 100 times." Optimize for talked-aboutness, not impressions. (speculation, load-bearing)
- Cluely launch video: ~12M views, maybe ~100k people telling friends; the demo showed glasses hardware that "does not exist" — deliberately depicted the end-state, not the product. (claimed)
- "50 interns" stunt: deliberately ambiguous post (real plan = a few manager hires + ~1,000-scale creator recruiting); generated ~5M views; "resulted in not much money flowing to Cluely" but massive talk. (first-party admission)
- Split channels by purpose: X/Twitter = pure mindshare/controversy (explicitly "not intended to be highly converting"); Instagram = the converting UGC funnel ("the performance engine") where you show the product and use cases. (claimed)
- Instagram UGC results: 20M+ views in the prior ~2.5 weeks; expecting first 50M-view month; over 1 million installs from short form alone in ~2 months. (claimed)
- Platform deltas: X rewards controversy and hasn't caught up to IG humor; IG tolerates low-quality/repetitive content (why clipping works — "you have probably seen the same clip 20–30 times"); TikTok and YouTube both UNCRACKED by Cluely ("we haven't tried YouTube"); IG allows educational step-1-to-5 content, TikTok attention "is done." (claimed)
- Launch cadence: startups launch quarterly/yearly; "you can get away with doing a launch once every day." Bought a ~$100,000 camera to build an in-house film studio; plan = a cinematic launch video for every feature, every ~2–3 weeks. (first-party)
- Virality formula (copied explicitly from friend.com / Avi Schiffmann, who spent "$4 million" [number uncertain — garbled audio] on his launch video): cinematic opening + controversial story that conveys the product; cinematic WITHOUT controversy flops (YC startup launch videos cited as the failure mode). (claimed/secondhand)
- The 50% rule: a tweet/launch should be extreme enough that ~50% of all viewers would have a strongly negative reaction; "if the number is not 50%, you're probably not being ballsy enough." (speculation/doctrine)
- Personal volume: ~3,000 tweets since Feb 1 (incl. replies), 1–2/day minimum to "keep my algo pull hot"; pays a LinkedIn ghostwriter; runs the Cluely corporate X account himself as "an extension of my personal account" — corporate voice must be the founder's real voice, never "corporate vanilla." (first-party)
- "Cluely universe" plan: Duolingo-style recurring cast/skits (reality-TV-style) using the influencer marketing team as characters; the viral "dancer/lap dance" post was unplanned BTS from one such shoot — front page of tech Twitter for 1–2 days. (first-party)
- Interview Coder arc numbers: first demo 500k LinkedIn impressions; Amazon-offer video ~20k views; total 200–300M views across socials after the Feb 1 paid launch. (claimed)
- AI-generated content: tested extensively, "just not there" — IG heavily rewards content that FEELS real (feels-real skit ≈ 10M views vs 1M if it doesn't); human-in-the-loop until models close the "final 10%." (first-party)

### UGC & creator deals

- ~50–60 in-house/contracted short-form creators posting daily; brief is literally "make a post that mentions Cluely in some way." (first-party)
- Pay structure: $25–$40 flat per video posted regardless of views, PLUS view bonuses — $500 at 500k views, $1,000 at 1M views, tiers beyond rarely reached. Described as "pretty close to market rate... a little more," same as clipping deals. Creators are contractors, not employees. (first-party, concrete)
- Recruiting: everyone on the team scrolls (mandatory scrolling hours); when someone spots a hot creator they drop them in a group chat and DM/poach. Targets include tiny accounts (3k–4k followers) who "have the right eye"; key filter = has already posted ≥3 videos (past the embarrassment barrier). (first-party)
- Marketing team = "my timeline in person": all hired off his For You page; average age 21.5; CMO Daniel Mintz has only 50k IG followers but total algo pull in the motivated-Asian-college-kid demo. (first-party)
- Ops: managed via Instagram group DMs, outgrowing into Discord. (first-party)
- Host's ad-read numbers (selling his own program): first 60 days of mass UGC = 10,914 videos posted, 72M views, 500k+ signups; "only pay after the views are generated"; cites Cal AI to $30M ARR (Zach Yadagari) and Quittr to $500k/mo (Alex Slater) as the same method. Host's own channel: started short form in January, peak 22M views in a week. (claimed, heavy incentive distortion)

### Launch & sequencing

- Cluely launched April 20, 2025; $500k MRR in under 2 months; "about to do $6M ARR." (claimed)
- Raised $5M seed off the expulsion virality; round closed in <24 hours; "15-minute calls that translate into millions"; pitch changed 3 times in 18 hours; regrets not raising bigger. Fundraising advice: traction first — "if your s— is working, the money will come." (first-party)
- YC-afterparty recruiting stunt: rented camera + party bus for a hype trailer; invite-only on purpose ("events need to be exclusive... if it's open invite nobody's going to talk about it"); ~1,000 cumulative DM requests; targeting 50/50 gender ratio; goal = engineer recruiting funnel via being the memorable party. (first-party)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- 3 full-time engineers, going to 4–5 within a week; believes ~6–7 engineers "will take us to the billion dollar valuation"; headcount under 50 "for eternity"; ~13–14 people in the office total. Built Interview Coder "almost 99% with AI." (first-party)
- Strategic bet: research labs won't compete at the application layer (race to AGI; OpenAI's "100x ARR" Windsurf/Codeium acquisition cited as buy-not-build evidence); design the product so each model generation makes it "four times as good"; whoever holds mindshare when the Jarvis-capable model API lands wins distribution. (speculation)

### Retention & product

- Essentially nothing on retention, churn, conversion rates, or pricing — Roy explicitly says "we can do better on the conversion but we're not really going for conversion right now. We're going for mindshare." No paywall/trial mechanics discussed for Cluely at all. (notable absence)

## Deal structures

- Cluely UGC creators: contractors, $25–$40 flat per posted video (paid regardless of performance) + performance bonuses of $500 at 500k views and $1,000 at 1M views, with higher tiers rarely hit. No exclusivity or rev-share mentioned. Host's competing model: pay only after views are generated (his tool's pitch).
- Roy's stated hiring alternative: full-time retainers to mid-tier creators (the "whatever retainer fee it takes to get the 150k-follower college kid" line) — ~60 creators effectively in-house.

## Contrarian positions

- Mindshare over conversion: deliberately runs the loudest channel (X) with zero conversion intent; argues talked-aboutness beats view count and beats performance marketing for compounding brand. Directly disputes Zach Yadagari/Blake Anderson's "product must be visually demoable" doctrine — "you don't need your product to be viral, you need your product to be in a viral situation."
- Reputation is "the last thing to worry about" pre-escape-velocity; companies "never fail because the founder is too controversial" (Jake Paul analogy). Anti-professionalism as strategy.
- Launch daily, not quarterly; corporate accounts should sound like the founder, never neutral.
- Raise the maximum possible at the highest valuation when you don't need it (explicitly anti-bootstrap for trillion-dollar ambitions, though he says he respects bootstrappers).
- Manufactured controversy as a repeatable system (the 50%-negative-reaction rule), not an accident.
- Creator > engineer as the $10k five-year bet; distribution now matters more than product because AI democratized shipping.

## Crave transfer

The transferable core is the UGC price sheet ($25–40/video flat + $500/$1k view bonuses, tiny 3k–4k-follower creators with "the eye," recruit via DMs off your own feed) and the channel-split insight (one loud awareness channel, one product-demo conversion channel) — both are cheap enough for a bootstrapped solo founder to test in Austin. Almost everything else is category- and structure-mismatched: Cluely is a VC-funded, globally-marketable, single-download AI-wow desktop product run by a founder whose scandal WAS the product story; Crave is a local-density utility with a hard $7.99 paywall where a national viral moment mostly buys non-Austin installs that can't convert. The "mindshare over conversion" doctrine is explicitly subsidized by $5M+ of venture money and zero disclosed unit economics — Roy admits conversion is weak and doesn't care; Brandon has to care. Discount every revenue/install figure (self-reported, no retention or churn data, and the "cheat on everything" category likely has brutal churn), and discount the host's 10,914-videos/72M-views/500k-signups numbers hardest — they appear inside an ad read for the tool he sells. The genuinely portable contrarian idea is that a controversial, talkable framing of the LAUNCH (not the app) can substitute for ad budget — e.g. an Austin-scoped provocative claim ("the objectively best taco in Austin, computed") aimed at local argument, where 50%-disagreement mechanics work in Crave's favor because food rankings are inherently fight-bait.
