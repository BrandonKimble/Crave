## Arc

Casper is a finance/side-hustle creator (~9M followers) who co-founded Payout, a class-action-settlement app, 50/50 with developer Connor after a competitor offered him a lowball $500 sponsorship — he took the validated idea and built his own. The app did $217K in 5 months and he claims ~$100K/mo. The episode is the creator's-side view of creator–developer equity partnerships: how to find pre-validated app ideas on creator profiles, the pitch that gets a top creator to say yes, and the two organic video formats that drive nearly all Payout's revenue.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Payout is subscription; the value framing is "spend X, get back X-plus": "you spend $40 in a year, you're probably going to make back… at least 200 in class actions." He calls this ROI-positive framing "such an easy sell" and the biggest formula for successful apps. (first-party framing, speculative payback number)
- Download → paid conversion: "from download to paid, I think is 6.7% or around 7%." (first-party, hedged)
- Coupon.ai has no in-app purchases; monetizes via affiliate links at "2 to 10% off each purchase" — requires "crazy volume." (secondhand/observed)

### UGC & creator deals

- His trigger event: a Payout competitor offered him ~$500 for a sponsored video; he called that low for his following and declined — then built the competitor. (first-party)
- The pitch he says is near-impossible to refuse: "we built this app for you based on this ad/brand deal that went well for you… I want to give you 50%. All you have to do is make these videos over and over again" — optionally sweetened with "Try one video. I'll give you 100% of the revenue." (first-party opinion)
- Detecting genuinely-converting vs boosted sponsored videos on a creator's profile: like-to-view ratio. "2 million views and 2,000 likes = boosted; 50,000 likes and 2 million views = probably organic." Creators must tag paid posts (#partner) — scan descriptions to separate paid from organic. (first-party heuristic)
- Multiple videos for one brand ≠ proof of conversion — brands sometimes buy 5-video packages up front to hedge. (first-party)
- Creator market is a "gold rush": influencer friends report ~5 emails/day from devs pitching app partnerships; you must outcompete that inbox. (first-party/secondhand)
- UGC rates: bigger creators "don't usually go under $1,000 to $1,500 per video," including only a few months of usage; "maybe 20–30% of ads will be profitable"; top creators normally charge "$6,000 plus per video." Premium UGC creators may take a % of ad spend or time-limit usage rights. (first-party as a seller of these deals)
- Friend-network rates: his creator friends post for free or ~70% off; even so, he lost money on a $1,500 discounted ad — creator marketing stays iterative and risky. (first-party)
- Big sponsors have budgets "like 100k to spend on paid ads this week or this month or 250k this quarter"; winning creatives get boosted to 35–40M views and "they definitely made back 10 times over easily." (speculation about sponsors' economics)
- Equity split evolution: first app 50/50 with the developer; by his third app he gave the dev/product side only 15%, because he proved he can scale "zero to like 100k" mostly on his own distribution, and dev work could alternatively be bought for $10–15K. (first-party)
- Who deserves equity: creators who understand the algorithm (first hit within ~3–4 videos, "$1,000+ in sales") OR creators with fiercely loyal audiences (e.g. Alex Sedlak) who can sell anything to their base — he'd actually prefer the loyal-audience type if he were the developer. (opinion, self-contradicting mid-answer)
- Coupon.ai signed him to a year-long exclusive partnership (paid monthly) — he reads that as venture-backed market-domination attrition strategy. (first-party deal, speculation on rationale)

### Content strategy (organic)

- Only TWO formats generate "significant money" for Payout: (1) "Watch me make up to $900 in just 30 seconds" step-by-step how-to; (2) skit → how-to hybrid ("Hey Temu, is it true I can make $1,000 from your lawsuit?"). Format 1 was run until it "dried up," then format 2 was invented. (first-party)
- The Payout remake of his best Gelato sponsored format (same hook, same structure, made ~a week later) got ~500K views, ~20,000 comments [uncertain — later restated as 24,000 likes ≈ ~1M views], and "probably close to $5,000 or more in subscription revenue" [transcript garbled: "close to dollars" in one spot, "5,000" in the replay]. (first-party, hedged)
- Temu skit video: "close to $10,000 in revenue… if not more with the subscriptions recurring." (first-party, hedged)
- Replication mechanics: keep idea + structure identical, swap the number ($900→$800), swap backgrounds, tweak duration (30s→45s) to dodge ad fatigue — "it just keeps crushing over and over." (first-party)
- ManyChat comment automation: "comment claim and I'll send you the app" → ~13,000 commenters → ~50% click-through on the DM'd link (~6,500 clicks). DM notification "sticks there forever" until clicked. (first-party)
- Never say the app's name out loud: "I always say go to this app" — naming it "immediately triggers… ad ad ad, scroll." Pinned soft-sell comment on YouTube Shorts/TikTok instead of in-video CTA ("the one I use in the video is called Payout"), because CTAs reduce retention; cut videos shorter for TikTok/YT Shorts. (first-party)
- Sell the bag, not the news: videos about the lawsuit itself got views but no sign-ups; videos about how YOU get paid convert — "~90% of people" only care about getting paid. (first-party)
- Sponsored-video benchmarks: 100K views = okay; target 200–500K per sponsored video; his recent all-platform average ≈ 1.4M views/video; ~70% of views are non-followers (For You page reach). (first-party)
- Short-form longevity: one video hit 17M views — 2.9M in week 1, 7.5M in month 1 (less than half of lifetime); 500+ days later still gets 30–50 views/hour, ≥10K views/day at plateaus. If he stopped posting, back-catalog would still drive "a few hundred downloads every single day." Revenue chart: each viral spike raises the baseline floor. (first-party dashboards shown)
- Niche demand read: on his channels, save-money videos are lotto shots (100K or 10M views); side-hustle videos reliably ≥300K — consistent demand is why Payout fit. (first-party)
- Coupon.ai told him one creator video (creator >1M followers) generated 300,000+ downloads; his own coupon video hit ~12M views (but promoted the website, not the app). (secondhand + first-party)
- He estimates the creator-equity app play is only "~25% through its lifespan." (speculation)

### Paid ads

- Connor repurposes Casper's organic winners as Meta ads (file or boosting code); one video ran profitably "for quite some time. Not a lot of time, few weeks." Equity creator = unlimited free ad creatives vs. paying per-creative. (first-party, vague)

### ASO & app store

- Coordinated push: 5 creators posting in one day → $9,700 revenue that day and App Store finance-chart placement (#70 or #80 in Finance [uncertain — earlier says "number 20"]). Charting drives organic App Store browse/search traffic + "aura." Takeaway: batch all creator posts on one day. (first-party)
- YouTube Shorts has no ManyChat, so the CTA is "search it on the App Store" — organic search is the conversion path there. (first-party)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- 50/50 creator–developer split; each owns their lane (product vs distribution). Creator's time is worth more creating than negotiating (pays agencies 20%). Both co-founders need business sense; business sense, not skill, should dictate equity — skills are commoditizing via AI/vibe coding. (first-party opinion)
- Ideal partnership target is a $100K/mo niche app, not a winner-take-all market — "you don't need 12 million views every video to make a lot of money with apps." (opinion)

## Deal structures

- Payout: 50/50 equity, creator does all distribution, developer does all product; no cash changed hands mentioned.
- His third app: developer/product side gets 15% equity (alternative: pay a dev $10–15K flat).
- Recommended cold pitch: 50% equity + "try one video, keep 100% of that video's revenue."
- UGC market rates: $1,000–1,500/video floor for bigger creators (few months' usage), $6,000+/video for top creators; premium creators take % of ad spend or cap usage months; agencies take 20%.
- Coupon.ai: year-long paid exclusivity, monthly payments (amounts undisclosed).
- Friend network: free or ~70%-discounted videos.
- Declined inbound: $500/video from a Payout competitor (the idea seed).

## Contrarian positions

- Giving creators 50% equity is framed as the smart move, against the common fear that a creator's audience "dries up" — he argues For You-page distribution means audience never depletes if formats keep iterating.
- Never name your app in the video — inverts standard sponsored-content branding advice; soft-sell pinned comments beat in-video CTAs because CTAs kill retention.
- Short-form content has long-tail longevity (500+ days of daily views) — against the "short-form dies in 48 hours" consensus.
- Views ≠ conversions: topical/news framings got more views and fewer sign-ups than "here's how you get paid" framings.
- Multiple brand-deal repeats on a profile aren't proof the deal converted (package buying).
- By app #3 he flips the standard equity logic: distribution keeps 85%, development gets 15%.

## Crave transfer

The transferable core is tactical, not structural: like-to-view ratio for vetting creators, batching creator posts on one day to chart in a category (Food & Drink is far less contested than Finance), soft-sell "this app" phrasing, ManyChat comment-to-DM funnels, and sell-the-outcome-not-the-data scripting ("here's the best taco in Austin" not "we mined Reddit"). His ~6.7% download-to-paid is NOT a benchmark for Crave — Payout's pitch is literally "pay $40, receive $200+ in cash," the easiest ROI sell that exists, and he says so; a $7.99/mo hard-paywall discovery app has no equivalent cash-back framing. His formats are also nationwide-audience formats; Crave's Austin-only utility means a 17M-view video wastes >99% of reach, so the creator-equity play would need Austin-local food creators with far smaller but geo-dense audiences — his $1,000–1,500 floor and 20–30% ad-profitability rate probably transfer better than his view counts. Heavy incentive discount: this is one surviving 50/50 partnership narrated by its beneficiary on a sponsor-driven podcast, and every revenue figure is self-reported and hedged.
