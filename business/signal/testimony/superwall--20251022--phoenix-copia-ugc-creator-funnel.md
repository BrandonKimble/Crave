## Arc

Phoenix built Copia, an AI manifesting/affirmations app riding the viral ChatGPT "day in your dream life" prompt trend (~Oct 2024), reaching a claimed 25K MRR and 100K downloads primarily through organic TikTok/Instagram UGC with his creator partner Chloe, then scaling to ~40 paid UGC creators. The interview (Superwall-sponsored) walks through his full funnel: viral hook-and-demo formats, onboarding conversion fixes, paywall philosophy, creator sourcing, and a Coldplay/astronomer-CEO newsjacking stunt.

## Claims

### Pricing & paywall

- Hot take: "If you do a hard paywall, it's honestly because your app is [garbled — presumably 'bad/shit']"; genuinely useful apps (Strava cited) use free trials, let users get entrenched, then convert on an emotional trigger. (claimed/opinion)
- Copia's paywall-to-trial conversion was 15–20%, which he calls "a decent standard." (claimed, first-party)
- Concedes hard paywall + a good product with investment/variable reward can work (Cal AI example), but says trigger-action-variable-reward-only apps with no investment "never get the model that made [garbled — likely 'Duolingo'] flow into a billion dollar company." Cal AI framed as "the easiest path to 100K" — trigger/action/variable reward, no investment. (opinion/secondhand)
- Host's framing: master top-of-funnel + good onboarding + paywall (A/B tested via Superwall) → "you can probably make like $100,000 a month." (speculation, sponsor-adjacent)

### Trial & onboarding

- Initial funnel: install-to-paywall view rate ~50–55% (half of users dropped during the four onboarding screens); paywall-to-trial 15–20%. (claimed, first-party)
- Original onboarding: login screen → name → type your goals → "day in your dream life" output. Users dropped at the free-text goals step — not enough trust built to share dreams/goals despite UGC-derived intent. (first-party)
- Fix: expanded onboarding with social proof, an onboarding video, and "a couple questions along the way" → install-to-paywall rose from 50–60% to 85–90%; "a couple questions... doubled our conversion." (claimed, first-party)
- Onboarding design rules: social proof phrasing like "proven methods, meaningful results" (modest, not outrageous claims); progressive tap-through questions with buttons, never a paragraph of typing; Duolingo-style minimal screens; "don't put more than like five words on each screen" — TikTok-caption pacing. (claimed/opinion)

### UGC & creator deals

- Target creators in the 50K–250K TikTok-follower range, NOT mega influencers: they aren't getting brand deals, often still work part/full-time jobs, so it's a mutually beneficial cheap partnership; 500K–1M+ creators have a brand to protect and won't touch an unproven product in its first 2–3 months. (claimed, first-party practice)
- Target CPM when paying creators: "aiming for like a $1 CPM. You can up that as you go along." (claimed; no per-creator flat fees disclosed)
- Scaled to 40 creators simultaneously. Sourced via Sideshift (UGC marketplace — name possibly garbled by auto-caption) which yielded "10–20 people that were actually really good"; also mentions agencies and college ambassadors. (claimed, first-party)
- With non-professional UGC creators/ambassadors, be strict with guidelines (they don't know angles/hooks); same core format for all 40 (slideshow, hook-and-demo, same product demo), A/B testing only the first 10 seconds/hooks. (first-party)
- Audience discovered via metrics: mostly girls 18–25. (claimed)
- Claimed "10% view to download rate" strategy (host's framing in intro; the body's closest datapoint: 13M-view slideshow → 10–15K downloads, which is ~0.1% — the 10% figure is likely the host's hype or refers to something not elaborated; flag as uncertain/unsupported in-transcript).

### Content strategy (organic)

- Core mechanism: "hook and demo" — show a magic moment, seed curiosity in comments, don't reveal too much sauce so it doesn't feel like an ad. E-commerce/Gymshark playbook (free product to creators → authentic-feeling endorsement) applied to apps. (claimed)
- Origin: ChatGPT "day in your dream life" prompt trend (~last October); 10–20M-view videos of girls crying at the output; turned the prompt into the app's magic moment. Raw prompt content alone didn't convert to installs ("people don't read a lot and people already use ChatGPT") — had to be funneled through the in-app experience. (first-party)
- Breakthrough: Chloe's Instagram video hit ~10M views. Format: "If you're seeing this before [date], repeat these affirmations for the next 7 days from Copia and watch your dream life unfold" — hard deadline hook + promised transformation + phone-screen demo overlay of the app. Evergreen/repeatable by swapping the date. (first-party)
- A 6.6M-view video that omitted the hook-and-demo still drove downloads (name-dropped "affirmations from Copia" → comment curiosity → bio link), but Phoenix estimates 20–30% fewer downloads than it could have gotten. (claimed)
- "Crash out" content: invented a big red "crash out now" button (breathing exercise); one two-slide TikTok slideshow got 12–13M views → 10–15K downloads; estimates a 15M-view proper hook-and-demo version would have gotten 30–40K downloads. (claimed; view count stated as both 12M and 13M in transcript)
- Newsjacking stunt: created a fake "astronomer CEO's daughter" account during the Coldplay kiss-cam scandal — ~200K followers and ~50M views in about a week; a follow-up video with Copia product placement got ~5M views but "only probably drove a couple thousand downloads" — low conversion without a clear demo. (first-party)
- Format inspiration: Sarah Pearl, a manifesting-niche creator — ~1M Instagram + 2.5M TikTok followers built over ~2.5 years of repeatable affirmation content; sold ~100,000 copies of her book; she in turn copied a self-help book that got ~1M purchases on TikTok Shop. Framework: find course/book sellers monetizing a niche (aim at 500K–1M-follower creators to study what they sell via link-in-bio), convert their one-time info product into a recurring-subscription app. (secondhand)
- Study hyper-competitive marketing spaces (ecom/affiliate, "DC Midas" tweets — name uncertain) for tactics; app founders from Silicon Valley/coding backgrounds under-import ecom marketing alpha. Meet your market where they are (e.g., Facebook-style creative for 45+). (opinion)

### Paid ads

- "You have to run ads eventually. It's really hard not to." Everyone bidding the same CPM with the same format skyrockets costs; edge = innovating new channels/audiences/demos (Duolingo's per-country, per-activity accounts cited). But dominate ONE funnel/one niche first; target 2–3x ROAS before expanding horizontally. (opinion/claimed)

### ASO & app store

- Naming matters: "You can get 100 million views but if your name is something super popular you're not going to be found in the app store. If your name is like 'AI journal,' you're going to be screwed." Pick a brandable, culturally relevant name. (opinion)

### Retention & product

- Hooked-model framing throughout: external trigger → simple action → variable reward → investment. Investment is the missing piece in most AI apps (Cal AI: "slot machine" calories, "no investment there... no actual product there") — fine for ROAS-driven top-of-funnel shoveling, never a billion-dollar company. (opinion)
- Design backwards from bottom of funnel: define the day-30/day-7/day-1 experience and work back. (opinion)
- Next-idea framework: "recently ubiquitous, newly possible" — ubiquitous personal data + newly possible LLM insights; wants to build "the Strava for stress and mental health": passive daily actions (heart rate, walks) require no user effort, LLM feeds back insights. Core critique of self-help: it demands user effort; passive data collection sidesteps that. (opinion/first-party direction)
- Virality is now "reverse engineered" — build a viral feature (K-factor action: scanning, swiping, shareable output) rather than waiting for natural virality. (opinion)

### Team, tools & cost structure

- Two-person core: Phoenix + Chloe (creator/co-founder). Tools: Sideshift (UGC marketplace), agencies, college ambassadors, Superwall (paywall A/B, sponsor plug), Figma for onboarding iteration. (first-party)

### Other

## Deal structures

- Creator pay benchmark: ~$1 CPM target when starting with 50K–250K-follower creators, raised over time. No flat fees, rev-shares, bonuses, or exclusivity terms disclosed. Gymshark free-product-for-content model cited as the philosophical template.

## Contrarian positions

- Anti-hard-paywall: hard paywalls signal a weak product; genuinely useful apps should trial-first and convert after entrenchment (directly against the pay-now hard-paywall meta popular in the AI-app scene — and stated on a Superwall-sponsored show).
- Anti-mega-influencer: deliberately avoid 500K+ creators early; 50K–250K is the value pocket.
- Cal AI-style trigger/reward apps are dead ends without investment — against the "easiest path to 100K" consensus he himself names.
- App founders should mine ecom/affiliate marketing tactics, not Silicon Valley playbooks.
- Virality must be manufactured via engineered viral features; natural virality is mostly gone.

## Crave transfer

The onboarding math is the most transferable, concrete signal here: install-to-paywall view rate is a measurable leak (his 50%→85-90% jump from social proof + tap-through questions + short screens), and Crave's hard-paywall-at-onboarding-end makes that rate existentially important — audit it from day one. His anti-hard-paywall stance deserves skepticism as a universal rule (it's a lifestyle/emotional app with cheap dopamine, and he's a single survivor), but it does flag a real risk for Crave: a hard paywall works best when the wow is demonstrable pre-payment, and Crave's repeat-use utility value is harder to prove in one onboarding than a "day in your dream life" emotional hit — Crave's paywall preview needs to show the actual Austin data density (real ranked dishes) before the card ask. The 50K–250K-creator / ~$1-CPM sourcing playbook transfers well to Austin food creators (local micro-creators are even cheaper and more geo-relevant than his national lifestyle pool), but his view-to-download numbers (13M views → 10–15K downloads, ~0.1%) are the honest benchmark — discount the "10%" intro claim entirely. His virality mechanics (deadline hooks, hook-and-demo, comment-bait) assume emotionally charged, broadly relatable content; a city-scoped utility app should expect strictly worse ceilings but better intent per view.
